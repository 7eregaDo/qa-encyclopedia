## The Intent
The interviewer is testing resource allocation thinking — a core lead responsibility. Quality engineering capacity is finite. When compliance deadlines, reliability incidents, and new product launches all compete for the same team capacity, a lead needs a defensible, transparent model for how to distribute effort. They want to see structured decision-making, not intuition.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** As Director of QE at a regulated fintech, my team faced three simultaneous demands: a new open banking API needed testing for a product launch in six weeks; a PCI-DSS audit required evidence of security testing across all payment APIs within eight weeks; and three existing high-traffic APIs had increasing error rates that needed stability investment. The team had capacity for approximately two of the three at the level each deserved.

**Task:** Make an explicit, communicated resource allocation decision and manage stakeholder expectations around the trade-offs.

**Action:** I used a portfolio model with three buckets, each with a defined capacity allocation based on the current business context. I brought the CTO, CPO, and CISO into a 45-minute prioritisation session with the following framing: "We have three demands and capacity for approximately two. Here is the impact of under-investing in each." Innovation (new API launch): under-investment risk was a delayed product launch and reputational damage with early adopters. Stability (existing API error rates): under-investment risk was continued customer impact and potential SLA breach. Compliance (PCI-DSS audit): under-investment risk was audit failure, regulatory fine, and a mandatory remediation programme. The group agreed that compliance was non-negotiable given regulatory consequences, and that stability had a direct customer impact that outweighed the launch timeline. We agreed to delay the open banking API launch by three weeks and negotiate reduced scope for the launch feature set, reducing the QE effort required. I then allocated: 50% of team capacity to PCI-DSS compliance testing, 30% to stability investment on the three degraded APIs, and 20% to the reduced-scope open banking launch. I documented this allocation as a formal decision record with the attendees' names and sent it to all stakeholders so the trade-offs were explicit and owned collectively.

**Result:** The PCI-DSS audit passed with no findings. The three degraded APIs had error rates reduced within the stability sprint. The open banking API launched three weeks late with reduced scope, which the product team managed with design partners. No stakeholder was surprised by the outcome because the trade-offs had been communicated before, not after, the decisions were made.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Works on assigned tasks; not involved in capacity allocation |
| **Mid** | Flags competing priorities to their manager; provides effort estimates to support planning decisions |
| **Senior** | Maintains a prioritised backlog of quality work; makes trade-off recommendations within their area to engineering management |
| **Lead** | Designs the capacity allocation model, facilitates cross-functional prioritisation sessions with CTO/CPO/CISO, documents trade-offs as formal decisions, and manages stakeholder expectations proactively |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Portfolio Model** | A resource allocation framework that divides engineering capacity into defined buckets — such as innovation, stability, and compliance — with proportional investment based on current priorities |
| **PCI-DSS** | The Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard, a regulatory framework requiring specific security controls and testing evidence for systems that handle payment card data |
| **Compliance Testing** | Quality assurance activities performed to generate evidence that a system meets regulatory or contractual obligations, distinct from functional or performance testing |
| **Stability Investment** | Engineering capacity allocated to improving the reliability and performance of existing production systems, as opposed to building new features |
| **Trade-Off Documentation** | A formal record of a resource allocation decision including the options considered, the criteria used, and the expected consequences of the choice, owned by named stakeholders |
| **Prioritisation Session** | A structured meeting with key stakeholders — typically CTO, CPO, and QE lead — to make explicit, collectively-owned decisions about where to allocate finite quality engineering capacity |
| **Reduced Scope Launch** | A product launch strategy that ships a subset of the planned features to meet a deadline with available quality capacity, with remaining features delivered in subsequent releases |
| **Regulatory Obligation** | A quality or security requirement imposed by law or regulation, which is non-negotiable in prioritisation and carries consequences (fines, audit failure) if not met |
