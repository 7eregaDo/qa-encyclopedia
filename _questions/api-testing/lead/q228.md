## The Intent
The interviewer is probing whether you can think about API quality at a business strategy level, not just an engineering level. When APIs are external products rather than internal plumbing, the stakes for quality mistakes change entirely — breaking changes, poor error messages, and undocumented behaviour have direct commercial consequences. They want a lead who has navigated this shift.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** I was Head of QE at a company transitioning from a B2C product to an API-first platform business. The engineering team was experienced at building internal APIs but had never shipped a public API as a commercial product. The first external API was due to launch to 50 design partners in three months. Quality failures in this launch would directly affect the company's ability to close its Series B.

**Task:** Reframe the QE approach for an externally-facing API product, addressing the quality dimensions unique to a commercial API and ensuring the launch gave design partners an excellent experience.

**Action:** I identified four quality dimensions that didn't exist for internal APIs. Developer experience as a quality criterion: I introduced a DX review process — before any endpoint shipped, a QE who had not seen the API before attempted to implement a simple integration using only the public documentation. Any friction, ambiguity, or inconsistency they encountered was treated as a quality defect. This was the most impactful change — it caught documentation gaps, confusing error messages, and non-intuitive parameter names before external developers saw them. Backwards compatibility as a zero-tolerance standard: I worked with engineering leadership to establish that any breaking change to the public API required a deprecation period of at minimum 90 days with direct notification to affected partners. I built automated breaking change detection into the CI pipeline. SLA-driven quality gates: the commercial SLA committed to 99.9% uptime and p95 latency under 300ms. I made these testable — every release required a performance test demonstrating SLA compliance before shipping. Partner-driven contract testing: I onboarded each of the 50 design partners to provide consumer-driven contract tests, giving the engineering team real consumer expectations rather than assumed ones. This also gave partners confidence that their integration would not break silently.

**Result:** The design partner launch had zero breaking changes in the first six months. Three of the 50 design partners cited the API's consistency and documentation quality as a reason for their positive evaluation. The DX review process caught 19 quality issues before external release. The company closed its Series B, and the API quality programme was cited in the investor due diligence as evidence of engineering maturity.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Tests API functionality against acceptance criteria; does not differentiate internal from external quality standards |
| **Mid** | Understands the heightened importance of backwards compatibility and documentation for external APIs; applies stricter standards |
| **Senior** | Designs quality strategy for external-facing APIs; implements DX testing, breaking change detection, and SLA-driven performance gates |
| **Lead** | Frames API quality as a business product dimension; owns DX as a quality criterion; establishes backwards compatibility as a zero-tolerance policy; builds partner contract testing; presents API quality as evidence of business readiness in investment contexts |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **API-First Business Model** | A business strategy in which APIs are the primary product or distribution channel, consumed directly by external developers and partners as a commercial offering |
| **Developer Experience (DX)** | The quality of the experience an external developer has when discovering, learning, and integrating a public API, treated as a first-class product quality dimension |
| **DX Review** | A quality process in which a tester with no prior knowledge of the API attempts an integration using only public documentation, surfacing friction and ambiguity as defects |
| **Commercial SLA** | A contractual commitment to API consumers about availability, latency, and support response, with financial or contractual consequences for breach |
| **Breaking Change Detection** | Automated CI tooling that compares the current API spec against the published version and fails the build if incompatible changes are introduced |
| **Design Partner** | An early external customer who provides feedback on a product in exchange for preferential access or pricing, used to validate product-market fit before general availability |
| **Zero-Tolerance Policy** | A governance stance that treats a specific class of quality failure — such as an unannounced breaking change — as always requiring escalation and remediation, regardless of perceived impact |
| **Investor Due Diligence** | The process by which investors assess the technical and operational quality of a company before committing capital, in which engineering quality maturity is a relevant signal |
