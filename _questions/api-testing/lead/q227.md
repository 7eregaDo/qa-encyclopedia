## The Intent
The interviewer is testing your leadership and people development capability — a non-negotiable lead competency. Technical excellence without people excellence doesn't scale. They want to see how you hire, how you grow people, how you handle performance challenges, and how you build a team identity that attracts and retains talent.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** I took over a six-person API testing team that had a mixed reputation in the engineering organisation. Two engineers were highly skilled and well-regarded; three were competent but disengaged; one was underperforming. The team was seen as a testing function rather than an engineering team, and attrition was high — two people had left in the previous six months. I had approval to hire two additional engineers.

**Task:** Build the team into a high-performing function that was respected as an engineering peer, reduced attrition, and had the technical capability to lead the org-wide API quality strategy I was designing.

**Action:** I worked across four dimensions. People assessment: in my first 30 days I had one-on-one conversations with each team member focused on understanding their career goals, frustrations, and technical interests — not just their current work. I identified that the disengaged engineers were bored with repetitive manual test execution and had no growth path. The underperformer had unclear expectations and no feedback history. Restructuring for leverage: I redesigned the team's remit from "test API features" to "own API quality infrastructure and standards." Each engineer owned a domain — contract testing platform, security testing, performance testing, developer tooling — that gave them genuine scope and visibility. The underperformer was given clear, written performance expectations and weekly coaching. Within two quarters, their performance improved to acceptable; within four, they became a reliable contributor. Hiring: I hired two engineers specifically for their security testing background and Python engineering depth — skills the team lacked. I involved the team in the interview process, which increased buy-in and signalled that the team's voice mattered. Culture: I introduced a weekly team "API quality demo" — 20 minutes where one engineer showed something they'd built or learned. This created a learning culture and gave engineers visibility across the wider engineering org, which several cited in performance reviews as a motivating factor.

**Result:** Attrition stopped — no further departures in the 18 months following the restructure. The team's engineering survey scores improved from the 38th to the 71st percentile company-wide. Three team members were promoted within two years. The team became the most requested collaboration partner for new API projects.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Focuses on individual technical growth; may not be aware of team health as a concern |
| **Mid** | Mentors junior colleagues; contributes to onboarding; raises team health concerns to their manager |
| **Senior** | Mentors multiple engineers; gives structured feedback; may lead hiring panels; contributes to team strategy |
| **Lead** | Owns team hiring, career development, performance management, culture, and identity; restructures team remit for leverage; manages attrition proactively; builds the team's reputation as an engineering peer |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Team Remit** | The defined scope of responsibility and ownership of an engineering team, which shapes how the team is perceived, what work it attracts, and how its members develop |
| **Domain Ownership** | The assignment of a specific technical area — such as contract testing or security tooling — to an individual engineer, giving them clear scope, accountability, and growth opportunity |
| **Performance Expectation** | A written, specific description of the behaviours and outcomes required of an engineer at their level, used as the basis for feedback and performance management |
| **Engineering Survey** | A periodic internal survey measuring engineering satisfaction, team health, and culture, used to track the impact of leadership interventions |
| **Attrition** | The rate at which team members leave an organisation or team voluntarily, often indicating unmet career needs, poor culture, or lack of growth opportunity |
| **Hiring for Leverage** | A hiring strategy that targets skills or specialisations the team lacks and that create disproportionate impact on the team's capability and output |
| **Team Identity** | The shared sense of purpose, values, and reputation that distinguishes a team within an engineering organisation and influences both retention and external collaboration |
| **API Quality Demo** | A regular team ritual where engineers showcase their work or learnings, building a knowledge-sharing culture and creating internal visibility for quality engineering work |
