## The Intent
The interviewer wants to see whether you can create durable quality culture rather than a quality function that atrophies the moment you leave. At lead level, your most important output is the engineering culture you shape — the practices, habits, and values that persist independently of any individual. They want to know how you've done this specifically for API quality.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** At a platform company, API quality was treated as the QE team's responsibility. Engineers wrote the code and handed it to QE to test. When API quality issues occurred, the reaction was "QE should have caught that." The QE team was small, overloaded, and had no leverage to change a cultural norm that had existed for years.

**Task:** Shift the cultural norm so that engineers felt genuine ownership of their API's quality, without creating resentment or additional burden.

**Action:** I worked across four levers over 12 months. Make quality visible to the people who create it: I introduced per-squad API quality metrics — error rate, contract coverage, security scan findings — into the squad's own sprint dashboard. Engineers saw the quality of their API alongside their velocity metrics. Quality was no longer something that happened downstream of them. Remove friction: I invested heavily in making quality easy. The API testing framework was a three-line addition to any project. Contract test templates meant adding a contract test to a new endpoint took 20 minutes. Automated security scanning ran without configuration. When quality requires significant effort, it gets skipped; when it's trivial, it becomes a habit. Reframe quality as engineering pride: I worked with engineering managers to introduce "API quality" as a discussion topic in performance conversations — not a box to tick, but a genuine area of engineering craft. I ran internal talks where engineers presented APIs they were proud of — well-designed, well-tested, well-documented — creating positive role models. Shared accountability for incidents: when an API-related incident occurred, the post-incident review included the engineering squad, not just QE. The question shifted from "why didn't QE catch this?" to "what does this tell us about how we build and test APIs?" This was uncomfortable initially but changed the conversation over time.

**Result:** Within 18 months, engineers were raising API quality concerns in design reviews unprompted. Contract test coverage became a peer expectation in code review — PRs without contract tests received review comments from other engineers, not just QEs. The engineering survey showed a 31-point improvement in engineers' sense of ownership over quality. Three engineers from product squads applied to join the QE platform team, citing the quality culture as a reason.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Follows quality practices set by others; may not feel responsible for API quality beyond their assigned tasks |
| **Mid** | Takes personal ownership of their API's quality; raises quality concerns proactively in design reviews |
| **Senior** | Models quality ownership visibly; influences squad culture through code review standards and design review participation |
| **Lead** | Designs the systemic levers — visibility, tooling friction reduction, performance narrative, and shared incident accountability — that shift quality ownership from QE to every engineer, and measures cultural change through surveys and unsolicited engineer behaviour |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Quality Ownership** | The cultural norm in which engineers who build APIs feel personally responsible for their quality, not dependent on QE to assure it |
| **Squad Dashboard** | A squad-level view of both delivery metrics (velocity, throughput) and quality metrics (error rate, contract coverage, scan findings) that makes quality visible to the people creating it |
| **Friction Reduction** | The deliberate removal of obstacles to quality practice — complex tooling, long setup times, unclear processes — so that doing the right thing is the path of least resistance |
| **Engineering Pride** | The sense of craftsmanship and satisfaction an engineer derives from building something well — including its testability, documentation, and reliability — used as a cultural lever for quality ownership |
| **Post-Incident Review** | A blameless retrospective conducted after a production incident that examines systemic causes and involves the squad who built the system, not just operations or QE |
| **Peer Expectation** | A quality standard enforced through social norms in code review and design discussions, rather than through a centralised gatekeeping function |
| **Performance Narrative** | The framing of API quality as an aspect of engineering craft discussed in performance conversations, making it a career-relevant concern rather than an afterthought |
| **Unsolicited Engineer Behaviour** | Spontaneous quality actions taken by engineers without being asked — raising concerns, adding tests, flagging design issues — used as evidence that quality ownership has become genuinely cultural |
