## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing whether you can move beyond simple input-output test design into multi-dimensional business logic. Mid-level testers are expected to handle complexity without either oversimplifying (testing only obvious cases) or over-engineering (testing every possible combination). They want to see a structured, efficient approach with clear rationale.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** I was assigned to test a loan eligibility feature with the following interdependent rules: applicants must be aged 21–65, have a minimum credit score of 600, have been employed for at least 12 months, and not have any active County Court Judgements (CCJs). Additionally, applicants aged 21–25 required a guarantor regardless of other factors, and applicants with a credit score of 600–649 required proof of income even if all other criteria were met.

**Task:** I needed to design a test suite that covered the full rule set efficiently — including the interaction between conditions — without writing hundreds of individual test cases.

**Action:** I worked through three stages. First, **rule decomposition**: I listed every individual condition and its valid/invalid states — age (below range, 21–25, 26–65, above range), credit score (below 600, 600–649, 650+), employment duration (under 12 months, 12 months+), CCJ status (active, none). This gave me the building blocks. Second, **decision table construction**: I built a decision table mapping combinations of conditions to expected outcomes. This surfaced the interdependencies clearly — the guarantor requirement for 21–25 age range applied regardless of credit score or employment, and the proof of income requirement for 600–649 applied regardless of age. The table forced me to think about every combination explicitly and revealed two rule interactions the requirements document had not made clear: what happens when an applicant is aged 21–25 AND has a credit score of 600–649? Do both the guarantor and proof of income requirements apply simultaneously? I flagged this as an ambiguity to the product owner before writing test cases. Third, **pairwise reduction**: after clarifying the ambiguities, I used pairwise testing to reduce the full combination set from 48 theoretical cases to 16 targeted test cases that covered every two-way interaction at least once.

**Result:** The 16 test cases uncovered three defects: the guarantor requirement was not being triggered for 21-year-olds with high credit scores (the age condition was being overridden by credit score logic), proof of income was not being requested for the 600–649 band when the applicant was over 50, and the system was approving applications with active CCJs when employment duration exceeded 24 months. All three were logic defects in the rule implementation that only appeared at specific condition intersections — invisible to happy-path testing.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Decompose a simple rule set into equivalence partitions and test each condition independently. |
| **Mid** | Build a decision table for interdependent rules, identify condition interactions and ambiguities, and apply pairwise testing to reduce combinatorial explosion. |
| **Senior** | Design a test strategy for a complex rules engine at scale — using model-based testing, automated rule verification, and a traceability matrix linking each rule to its test cases. |

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Decision Table Testing** | A technique mapping combinations of input conditions to expected outputs — exposes interdependencies and missing rule coverage. |
| **Rule Decomposition** | Breaking a complex rule set into individual conditions and their possible states before designing tests. |
| **Pairwise Testing** | A combinatorial technique ensuring every two-way combination of conditions is tested at least once — reduces test count without sacrificing coverage. |
| **Combinatorial Explosion** | The exponential growth in test cases when multiple conditions each have multiple states — the problem pairwise testing solves. |
| **Condition Interaction** | A scenario where the outcome depends on the combination of two or more conditions — not visible when conditions are tested independently. |
| **Rule Ambiguity** | An undefined or contradictory specification about how two or more rules interact — must be resolved before test cases are written. |
