## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing operational maturity. Writing test cases is a skill; managing a test suite at scale is a discipline. Mid-level testers are expected to own the health of their test suite — not just add cases indefinitely. They want to see that you have a framework for maintenance and can articulate the warning signs of a suite in decline.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** I inherited a manual test suite in TestRail with 650 test cases accumulated over three years by multiple team members. There was no ownership model, no review history, and no retirement criteria. When I ran a sample of 50 cases, 14 had incorrect expected results, 6 referenced features that no longer existed, and 3 had preconditions that were impossible to set up in the current test environment.

**Task:** I needed to assess the suite's health, establish a maintenance model, and restore confidence in its results — without halting the testing work the team relied on it for.

**Action:** I introduced a four-part maintenance model. First, **initial audit**: over two weeks I worked through the full suite systematically — tagging each case as Current (accurate and executable), Needs Update (structure valid but content outdated), or Retire (feature deprecated or duplicate). 71% were Current, 21% needed updates, and 8% were retired immediately. This produced a clean baseline. Second, **ownership assignment**: I aligned test cases to product feature areas and assigned a named QA engineer as owner for each area. Owners were responsible for reviewing their cases when a related story was completed — not on a fixed schedule, but event-driven. Third, **update-on-change policy**: any story that modified an existing feature required the responsible QA engineer to review and update affected test cases before the story was marked done. I added this to the definition of done. Fourth, **quarterly health check**: at the end of each quarter I ran a 30-minute sweep looking at cases that had not been executed in over 90 days — these were candidates for retirement or automation. I tracked two suite health metrics in the team dashboard: percentage of cases executed in the last 90 days, and percentage flagged as needing update.

**Result:** Within two quarters the suite had stabilised at 580 active cases — 70 had been retired and 40 had been updated. The "needs update" flag dropped from 21% to 4%. More importantly, the team's confidence in the suite recovered — failing tests were investigated rather than ignored because the false-failure rate had dropped significantly. The maintenance model added an estimated 20 minutes per story to QA effort — a small overhead for a significant increase in suite trustworthiness.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Describe keeping your own test cases up to date when requirements change. |
| **Mid** | Design a suite maintenance model for a large inherited suite — audit, ownership, event-driven updates, retirement criteria, and health metrics. |
| **Senior** | Build a test suite governance framework across multiple teams, integrate suite health metrics into the engineering quality dashboard, and define a strategy for gradually automating the high-value manual cases. |

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Test Suite Governance** | The policies, ownership model, and review cadence that keep a test suite accurate and trustworthy over time. |
| **Test Case Retirement** | The deliberate removal of test cases that are obsolete, duplicated, or no longer provide value. |
| **Event-Driven Maintenance** | Updating test cases in response to a specific change (a story completion) rather than on a fixed schedule. |
| **Suite Health Metrics** | Indicators of test suite quality — e.g., percentage of cases executed recently, percentage flagged as outdated. |
| **Definition of Done** | A checklist of quality criteria — extended here to include test case review and update when related features change. |
| **False Failure Rate** | The percentage of test failures caused by outdated or incorrect test cases rather than real defects — erodes team trust. |
