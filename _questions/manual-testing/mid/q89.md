category: manual-testing

theory: "Flaky or inconsistent results in manual testing — where a test passes on one execution and fails on another with no apparent change — are a signal that there is an underlying instability in the system, the test data, the test environment, or the test case itself. They must be investigated and resolved rather than dismissed as 'one-off glitches,' because unresolved flakiness erodes team trust in test results and can mask real defects."
---

## The Intent

The interviewer is testing your analytical rigour and professional discipline. Many testers mark inconsistent results as "intermittent" and move on — which means real defects get buried in noise. Mid-level testers are expected to investigate the source of inconsistency systematically and either confirm a real defect, improve the test case, or fix the environment condition causing the instability.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** During a regression cycle, I had a test case for the cart total recalculation that was failing roughly one in three executions. The failure showed the cart total momentarily displaying an incorrect value before correcting itself. When I re-ran the test immediately after a failure, it often passed.

**Task:** I needed to determine whether this was a real intermittent defect, a test execution timing issue, an environment problem, or a test case design flaw — and resolve it either way.

**Action:** I followed a four-step investigation process. First, **increase the sample**: I ran the test case 10 times in sequence, logging exactly what happened each time — pass or fail, what was displayed at the moment of failure, and the time taken between actions. The failure occurred on 4 of 10 runs, and I noticed it correlated with faster execution — when I moved quickly between steps, failure was more likely. Second, **isolate variables**: I tested on a different browser (same result), a different test account (same result), and at a different time of day (same result). This ruled out browser-specific, data-specific, and load-specific causes. Third, **form a hypothesis**: the pattern of failing on fast execution and passing on slow execution strongly suggested a race condition — the cart total was being recalculated asynchronously, and the UI was displaying the old value briefly if the next action was triggered before the recalculation completed. Fourth, **confirm and report**: I added a deliberate 2-second wait between the triggering action and the assertion step in my test case — the failures disappeared completely. I reported the defect as a confirmed race condition with evidence: screen recording of the failure, the execution timing correlation, and a recommendation that the UI should not display the cart total until the recalculation was confirmed complete.

**Result:** The developer confirmed the race condition. A loading state was added to prevent the stale value from displaying during recalculation. The test case was also updated to include the timing note so future testers understood the context. The fix eliminated the inconsistency entirely. What could have been dismissed as a flaky test was actually a real, reproducible race condition defect.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Describe re-running a test multiple times and reporting an intermittent result with reproducibility information. |
| **Mid** | Investigate an inconsistent result systematically — increase sample, isolate variables, form and test a hypothesis — and resolve it as either a real defect or a test/environment issue. |
| **Senior** | Build a flakiness investigation protocol for the team, define policies for how long intermittent defects can remain open before escalation, and identify systemic sources of test instability at the infrastructure level. |

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Flaky Test** | A test that produces inconsistent results without a code change — passes sometimes, fails other times. |
| **Race Condition** | A defect where the outcome depends on the relative timing of two or more operations — a common cause of intermittent failures. |
| **Variable Isolation** | Systematically changing one factor at a time to identify which variable is causing inconsistency. |
| **Sample Size** | Running a test multiple times to observe the failure pattern — essential for characterising intermittent issues. |
| **Timing Correlation** | A pattern where failures correlate with execution speed or timing — a diagnostic signal for race conditions or async behaviour. |
| **Reproducibility Rate** | The percentage of executions on which a defect occurs — essential information for an intermittent defect report. |
