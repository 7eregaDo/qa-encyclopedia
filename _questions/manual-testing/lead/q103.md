## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing executive communication and the ability to advocate for a discipline under existential pressure. This is a question about influence, evidence, and framing. Lead-level QA professionals must be able to enter a leadership conversation where the prevailing view is against them, and shift that view through data and logic — not defensiveness or appeals to convention.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** At an engineering leadership planning session, the VP of Engineering proposed eliminating the manual testing budget in the next financial year and redirecting the investment into expanding the automation team. The argument was compelling on the surface: "We have 88% automated coverage. Manual testing is redundant." Two of the four engineering directors nodded in agreement.

**Task:** I had 10 minutes to respond — not with a prepared presentation, but in the room, live. I needed to shift the conversation from "manual vs. automation" to "what does each do and what happens when we remove one?"

**Action:** I made three moves. First, **challenge the premise with data**: I asked the VP to look at the previous quarter's production incident data with me. Of the 14 production defects that had reached users, 11 had been in areas with full automated coverage. The 88% coverage figure was real — but it was measuring execution, not effectiveness. Automation was catching the defects it had been programmed to catch; it was not catching the novel, judgment-dependent, or experiential defects that manual testing found. I had this data because I maintained a defect attribution tracker as a standing practice. Second, **make the structural argument concretely**: I asked the VP to name one automated test that could evaluate whether the new onboarding flow felt confusing to a first-time user, or whether the error message on the payment page was clear enough to prevent support calls. There was no answer — because no such test existed or could exist. These were judgment calls that required human perception. Third, **reframe the proposal**: I agreed that the current manual testing resource allocation was not optimal — some of it was genuinely redundant now that automation had matured. I proposed a restructure rather than an elimination: reduce manual regression effort (already partially redundant), invest in exploratory testing capability and usability testing, and redirect three manual testing engineers toward quality intelligence work that would feed directly into product decisions. This was not a defence of the status quo — it was a forward-looking proposal that accepted automation's gains while defining a distinct value for the human layer.

**Result:** The elimination proposal was withdrawn. A restructure proposal was approved instead. The quality intelligence role became a new function within the QA team. Six months later, insights from the quality intelligence work directly influenced two product roadmap decisions — providing the business-level evidence that the VP had originally doubted manual testing could generate.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Explain what manual testing does that automation cannot with two or three concrete examples. |
| **Mid** | Use defect attribution data to demonstrate the value of manual testing relative to automated coverage. |
| **Senior** | Build a data-backed case for manual testing investment, framed in terms of risk and business outcomes rather than testing philosophy. |
| **Lead** | Make a live, evidence-based case to a hostile leadership audience — challenging a flawed premise with data, making the structural argument concretely, and proposing a forward-looking alternative that builds on automation's gains rather than defending the status quo. |

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Defect Attribution Tracking** | Classifying each production defect by where it was found and what type of testing should have caught it — the primary evidence base for manual testing's value. |
| **Structural Argument** | The case that automation cannot replace manual testing for certain activities due to fundamental differences in what each can assess — not a preference argument but a capability argument. |
| **Coverage vs. Effectiveness** | The distinction between how many tests are running (coverage) and whether they are finding the right defects (effectiveness). |
| **Quality Intelligence** | A forward-looking role for manual testers — using deep product knowledge and testing insight to inform product and engineering strategy. |
| **Reframing** | Shifting a conversation from its current terms (replacement vs. retention) to more productive terms (optimisation and evolution) — a key leadership communication technique. |
| **Live Advocacy** | Making an evidence-based case for a position in real time, without prepared materials — requires mastery of the data and the argument. |
