## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing domain breadth and the ability to operate in high-stakes, high-accountability environments. Lead QA professionals in or adjacent to regulated industries must understand that quality in these contexts is both an engineering and a legal obligation — and that the consequences of inadequate test evidence extend beyond user experience to regulatory sanction and legal liability.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** I was appointed Head of Quality for a medical device software company whose products were subject to FDA 21 CFR Part 11 and IEC 62304 regulations. Every software release required a formal validation package — including a documented test plan, executed test protocols with tester signatures, and a summary report confirming that validation criteria had been met. The company had historically managed this through manual processes in Word documents, which were error-prone, audit-risky, and extremely time-consuming.

**Task:** I needed to design a testing strategy that met all regulatory documentation requirements, was efficient enough not to cripple delivery velocity, and produced audit-ready evidence as a natural byproduct of the testing process rather than a separate documentation exercise.

**Action:** I structured the strategy around four regulatory compliance pillars integrated with quality effectiveness. First, **traceability by design**: every test case was linked to a specific requirement in the requirements specification, and every requirement was linked to its risk assessment in the risk management file (per ISO 14971). This traceability matrix was not a retrospective exercise — it was built as test cases were written. No test case was created without a requirement link; no requirement existed without a corresponding test case. The matrix was the primary audit document for demonstrating that all requirements had been tested. Second, **electronic test execution records**: I migrated test execution from Word documents to a validated test management system (Jama Software, which itself held FDA 21 CFR Part 11 compliance). Each test execution was recorded electronically with tester identity, timestamp, pass/fail result, and any deviation notes. The system generated audit trails automatically. Third, **formal review and approval workflows**: the test plan, test protocols, and summary report all required electronic sign-off from the QA lead, the software development lead, and the regulatory affairs manager — recorded in the system with date and identity. No validation package could be submitted without all three approvals. Fourth, **deviation and non-conformance management**: any test case that failed, was executed with a deviation from protocol, or was executed outside its specified conditions was formally documented as a non-conformance, investigated, and resolved before the summary report could be approved. This ensured the validation package accurately reflected what had happened during testing — not a sanitised version of it.

**Result:** The first FDA audit after the new system was implemented lasted one day rather than the previous three — the auditor noted that the traceability matrix and electronic records made evidence retrieval immediate rather than requiring manual searching through document archives. Two findings from the previous audit (inadequate audit trail and incomplete traceability) were closed with zero repeat findings. Delivery velocity improved by 30% because the documentation was generated during testing rather than after it — eliminating the two-week post-testing documentation sprint that had previously preceded every submission.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **FDA 21 CFR Part 11** | US FDA regulation governing electronic records and signatures in regulated industries — requires audit trails and access controls. |
| **IEC 62304** | International standard for medical device software lifecycle processes — defines requirements for software development and testing documentation. |
| **Validation Package** | The complete set of testing documentation required for a regulated release — test plan, executed protocols, summary report, and traceability matrix. |
| **Traceability Matrix** | A document mapping every requirement to its test case and every test case to its requirement — the primary audit evidence for requirements coverage. |
| **Non-Conformance** | A formal record of a test deviation, failure, or procedural departure — must be investigated and resolved before a validation package is approved. |
| **Audit Trail** | An electronically maintained record of who did what, when, and what the result was — required by most regulated industry frameworks. |
