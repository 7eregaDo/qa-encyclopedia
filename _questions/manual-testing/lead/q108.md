category: manual-testing

theory: "Enforcing standards across teams with different maturity levels requires a differentiated approach — the same standard applied uniformly to a high-maturity and a low-maturity team will either be too demanding for the latter or insufficiently challenging for the former. Effective standard enforcement combines a non-negotiable minimum baseline applicable to all teams, tiered expectations above the baseline calibrated to team maturity, and a support model that helps lower-maturity teams meet the baseline rather than simply measuring their failure to do so."
---

## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing the ability to manage organisational complexity and resist the temptation to apply one-size-fits-all solutions. Lead-level QA professionals overseeing multiple teams must be able to hold a standard while recognising that the path to that standard differs by team — and that enforcement without support creates resentment rather than improvement.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** I was Head of Quality across six product squads at dramatically different maturity levels. Two squads had mature, well-maintained test suites with experienced QA engineers. Two were mid-maturity — functional processes but inconsistent quality. Two were genuinely low-maturity — recent QA hires, ad-hoc processes, and significant quality gaps. Applying the same standards to all six immediately would have set the two lowest-maturity squads up to fail, while applying the lowest common denominator would have dragged down the high-maturity squads.

**Task:** I needed to define standards that were universal in their intent but differentiated in their application, with a support model that moved all teams toward the same destination at different rates.

**Action:** I built the framework across three tiers. First, **the non-negotiable baseline** — four standards applicable to every squad immediately, regardless of maturity: every defect report must include steps to reproduce, expected result, and actual result; every story must have at least one test case before it is marked done; every sprint must include at least one exploratory session with a documented debrief; and no release may proceed without a documented smoke test result. These were the minimum floor. I was explicit: these were not aspirational, they were required, and I would measure compliance. Second, **maturity-tiered expectations above the baseline**: high-maturity squads were expected to maintain the full quality standards — complete test suites, risk-based regression, accessibility coverage, and peer review of test cases. Mid-maturity squads had a six-month roadmap to reach the full standard, with quarterly milestones. Low-maturity squads had a 12-month roadmap starting from the baseline, with my direct support and fortnightly coaching sessions. Third, **support before enforcement**: for the two lowest-maturity squads, I spent the first two months working alongside their QA engineers — not inspecting, but building. I helped them write their first structured test cases, ran their first exploratory sessions with them, and wrote the first compliant defect reports together. Enforcement of the baseline started in month three — after support had been provided.

**Result:** All six squads met the baseline standards within four months. The two low-maturity squads reached mid-maturity standard within 12 months — six months ahead of the roadmap — because the early investment in direct support had accelerated their development significantly. The differentiated approach meant no squad felt their maturity level was being used against them — they all had a clear path and visible support. The high-maturity squads maintained their standards and, seeing the investment being made elsewhere, became advocates for the programme rather than resentful of the attention given to lower-maturity peers.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Follow team standards and raise questions when the standard is unclear. |
| **Mid** | Onboard a new team member to existing standards and provide feedback when standards are not being met. |
| **Senior** | Define quality standards for a team, measure compliance, and provide improvement support when gaps are identified. |
| **Lead** | Design a tiered standards framework with a universal baseline, maturity-calibrated expectations, and a support-first enforcement model — applied consistently across teams at different levels without creating unfairness or resentment. |

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Non-Negotiable Baseline** | The minimum quality standards applicable to every team regardless of maturity level — the floor below which no team operates. |
| **Maturity-Tiered Expectations** | Standards calibrated to team capability level — the same destination, different timelines and intermediate milestones. |
| **Support-First Enforcement** | Providing direct assistance to help teams meet standards before beginning formal compliance measurement — prevents failure without opportunity. |
| **Compliance Measurement** | Tracking adherence to defined standards using observable, objective indicators — not subjective impression. |
| **Maturity Roadmap** | A defined, time-bounded plan for a team to progress from their current quality level to the target standard. |
| **Universal Intent** | The principle that standards apply to all teams in purpose, even when the timeline or implementation differs by maturity. |
