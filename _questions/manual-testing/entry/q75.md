## The Intent

The interviewer wants to know whether you treat exploratory testing as a structured professional skill or as unguided clicking. They also want to see that you understand when it is the right tool — and that you can produce useful, documentable output from an exploratory session, not just vague impressions.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** After completing scripted regression testing on a new account management feature, I had 90 minutes remaining before the end of the testing window. All scripted test cases had passed.

**Task:** Rather than signing off early, I used the remaining time for a structured exploratory session to investigate the feature from angles that scripted tests don't cover — unusual user behaviour, edge cases, and cross-feature interactions.

**Action:** I started by writing a brief charter: "Explore the account management feature as a user who makes unexpected choices — switching between tabs mid-edit, using browser back/forward navigation, making simultaneous edits in multiple browser tabs, and entering unusual but technically valid inputs." I time-boxed the session to 60 minutes and kept a running note in a simple text document as I tested — logging observations, questions, and anything that felt unexpected, even if I couldn't immediately confirm it was a defect. I used the remaining 30 minutes to investigate two observations that had seemed odd and confirm whether they were defects. I focused my exploration on areas that felt most "brittle" — places where state was being managed across steps or where user actions could interact unexpectedly.

**Result:** I found two defects that scripted testing had missed: saving account changes in one browser tab silently overwrote changes made in a second tab with no warning, and navigating away mid-edit and then pressing the browser back button restored the unsaved form state but showed it as "saved." Both were genuine usability defects that users would encounter in realistic scenarios. My session notes provided enough context for both defects to be reported clearly and reproduced by the developer without a follow-up conversation.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Exploratory Testing** | Simultaneous test design and execution guided by tester learning, curiosity, and domain knowledge. |
| **Charter** | A brief mission statement for an exploratory session defining the target area and focus. |
| **Session-Based Testing** | Exploratory testing structured into time-boxed sessions with defined charters and debrief notes. |
| **Session Notes** | A running record of observations, questions, and findings during an exploratory session. |
| **Debrief** | A post-session review summarising what was tested, what was found, and what requires further investigation. |
| **Heuristic** | A mental shortcut or rule of thumb that guides exploratory investigation — e.g., test what changed, test the edges, test with multiple users simultaneously. |
