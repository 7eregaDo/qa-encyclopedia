## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing strategic thinking about regression at scale. Full-suite regression becomes increasingly impractical as a product grows — senior testers are expected to design intelligent, adaptive approaches rather than simply running everything on a fixed schedule. They want to see a framework that uses real data to make prioritisation decisions, and a maintenance model that keeps the strategy current.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** I was leading QA for a product that had grown to 320 manual test cases in the regression suite over two years. Running the full suite took 14 engineer-hours per cycle. With fortnightly releases, this was consuming an unsustainable portion of team capacity — and because the team was running everything, nothing was being reviewed or retired.

**Task:** I needed to redesign the regression strategy so it was risk-driven, adaptive to product change, and sustainable within the team's actual capacity.

**Action:** I built the strategy around four inputs. First, **change impact analysis**: before each release, I reviewed the list of stories and bug fixes included in the build. For each change, I identified which areas of the product were directly affected and which adjacent areas could plausibly be impacted by side effects. This gave me a change-driven test selection — the minimum set of cases that must be executed based on what changed. Second, **defect history weighting**: I analysed 18 months of defect data and identified which product areas had the highest historical defect rate. These areas received elevated regression priority regardless of whether they had changed in the current release — because history showed they were inherently prone to regression. Third, **business criticality**: I worked with the product owner to define the three highest-value user journeys in the product — the ones whose failure would have the most immediate business impact. These journeys were always included in every regression cycle, regardless of change impact or defect history. Fourth, **tiered execution model**: combining the three inputs, I classified every test case into three tiers. Tier 1 (always run): critical user journeys + cases flagged by change impact analysis. Tier 2 (run when capacity allows): cases in historically high-defect areas not directly changed. Tier 3 (quarterly or on-demand): stable areas with no recent change and no defect history — validated by exploratory session rather than full scripted execution.

**Result:** Average regression execution time dropped from 14 hours to 6 hours per cycle — a 57% reduction. Defect detection rate through regression remained constant — the strategy was finding the same proportion of defects with less than half the effort. The tier classification also served as a living guide to suite health — cases that remained in Tier 3 for three consecutive cycles were reviewed for retirement. Over six months, 40 cases were retired, reducing the suite to 280 cases with higher average value per case.

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Risk-Based Regression** | A regression strategy that prioritises test execution based on risk rather than running the full suite every time. |
| **Change Impact Analysis** | Identifying which test cases are most relevant based on the specific changes in a given build. |
| **Defect History Weighting** | Elevating the regression priority of areas with a high historical defect rate — regardless of recent change. |
| **Business Criticality** | The relative importance of a user journey to business outcomes — the highest-criticality journeys are always included in regression. |
| **Tiered Regression Model** | A classification of test cases into execution tiers (always, conditional, quarterly) based on combined risk inputs. |
| **Suite Leanness** | The degree to which a regression suite contains only high-value, current test cases — maintained through regular retirement of outdated or redundant cases. |
