## The Intent
The interviewer is checking whether you can close the communication gap that is endemic in data and AI work — where technically sound findings get lost because they are presented in unfamiliar language. At entry level, this means being able to explain what your test found and why it matters, without assuming the listener knows what an F1 score is.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** I ran a fairness assessment on a loan eligibility model and found that the model's approval rate for applicants from one demographic group was 18 percentage points lower than for a comparable group with similar financial profiles. I needed to present this to the product manager and the head of compliance, neither of whom had a data science background.

**Task:** I needed to communicate the finding clearly enough that both stakeholders understood the issue, its severity, and the decision they needed to make.

**Action:** I structured the communication in three layers. First, the plain-language finding: "When we test the model with applicants who have very similar financial situations, it approves loans for one group at a significantly higher rate than another. Specifically, if we compare 100 applicants from Group A with 100 financially similar applicants from Group B, approximately 18 more applicants from Group A would be approved." I avoided the word "model" where possible and never mentioned accuracy metrics. Second, the business and legal risk: I explained, in one sentence, that this pattern could constitute indirect discrimination under the Equality Act 2010, and that if the model were used for lending decisions at scale, the organisation could face regulatory and reputational consequences. Third, the options: continue the investigation and delay launch, launch with the feature disabled for the affected decision pathway, or address the training data imbalance before proceeding. I recommended the third option and explained what it would involve. I attached the technical detail as an appendix for anyone who wanted it.

**Result:** The head of compliance escalated to legal immediately. The launch was paused. The product manager thanked me for making the finding legible — she said a previous data quality report she had received had been "full of numbers that meant nothing." The technical appendix was picked up by the data science team and used directly for the remediation work.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Can translate a data or AI test finding into plain language and present it with business risk framing and a clear recommendation |
| **Mid** | Tailors communication to different audiences simultaneously — executive summary, business risk framing, and technical detail — and owns the follow-through to resolution |
| **Senior** | Designs communication standards for the data and AI testing function, facilitates cross-functional conversations between data science, legal, product, and compliance, and drives findings to organisational decisions |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Plain-language finding** | A description of a test result expressed in terms a non-specialist can immediately understand, without technical jargon or unexplained metrics |
| **Business risk framing** | Contextualising a technical finding in terms of regulatory exposure, reputational damage, revenue impact, or customer harm |
| **Equality Act 2010** | UK legislation prohibiting discrimination on the basis of protected characteristics — relevant when AI systems make or inform decisions affecting individuals |
| **Indirect discrimination** | A practice that appears neutral but has a disproportionate adverse effect on a particular protected group — a legal concept relevant to AI decision systems |
| **Recommended action** | An explicit statement of what the finding means for the next decision — not just describing the problem, but proposing a response |
| **Technical appendix** | The section of a report containing detailed methodology, metrics, and data — referenced by the main finding but not required reading for non-technical stakeholders |
| **Escalation** | The act of raising a finding to a higher level of authority when the risk or decision is beyond the recipient's remit — a sign the communication was effective |
