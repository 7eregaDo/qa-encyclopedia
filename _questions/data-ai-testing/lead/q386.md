## The Intent
The interviewer is checking whether you can navigate the intersection of engineering quality and regulatory compliance at lead level. In regulated industries, quality is not just a technical practice — it is a legal obligation with material consequences for non-compliance, and a quality leader must be fluent in both dimensions.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** I was leading data and AI quality at a digital health company whose products included an AI triage assistant used by clinical staff to prioritise patient queues, and a predictive readmission risk model used by hospital discharge teams. Both were classified as medical devices under the UK Medical Device Regulations, triggering mandatory clinical validation and post-market surveillance obligations.

**Task:** I needed to design a quality assurance programme that met the regulatory requirements for software as a medical device (SaMD) without creating a compliance process so burdensome that the engineering team could not iterate.

**Action:** I worked closely with our regulatory affairs team to map the MDR and MHRA guidance for SaMD against our existing quality practices, identifying gaps and overlaps. Four areas required significant new practice. First, clinical validation: both models required validation studies using clinically representative datasets — not just internal labelled test sets. I partnered with two NHS trust research teams to design prospective validation studies with independent clinical labelling. Second, intended use documentation: the regulations required precise documentation of the intended use, the intended users, the contraindications, and the known limitations — I built this into the model card format as mandatory sections for regulated features. Third, post-market surveillance: the regulations required continuous collection of real-world performance data and a periodic safety update report. I designed a surveillance dashboard tracking model accuracy, error rates, and clinical outcome proxy metrics, with an annual PSUR template. Fourth, change management: any significant change to a regulated AI feature required a new conformity assessment. I defined "significant change" operationally — changes to training data scope, model architecture, or decision threshold — so engineering teams knew in advance which changes triggered a regulatory re-review. I then negotiated with the regulatory affairs team to create a fast-track path for non-significant changes, preserving iteration speed for the majority of releases.

**Result:** Both products received and maintained their SaMD classifications. The MHRA conducted a routine inspection and cited our post-market surveillance documentation as exemplary. The fast-track path for non-significant changes allowed the engineering team to deploy product improvements at normal velocity — 78% of releases fell below the significant change threshold. The regulatory affairs director told the board that having a quality engineering lead who understood both the technical and regulatory dimensions had halved the time required to prepare conformity documentation.

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **SaMD (Software as a Medical Device)** | Software that performs a medical function without being part of a physical device — subject to medical device regulations in the UK, EU, and US |
| **Clinical validation** | A study using clinically representative data and independent clinical labelling to demonstrate that a medical AI system performs its intended function safely and effectively |
| **Post-market surveillance** | Ongoing collection and analysis of real-world performance data for a regulated AI system, required to identify safety signals after deployment |
| **PSUR (Periodic Safety Update Report)** | A regulatory submission documenting post-market surveillance findings, adverse events, and performance data for a medical device or software — typically submitted annually |
| **Intended use** | A precisely documented statement of what an AI system is designed to do, for which users, in which clinical or operational context — a mandatory component of SaMD documentation |
| **Significant change** | A modification to a regulated AI system substantial enough to require a new conformity assessment — defined by regulatory guidance and operationalised for engineering teams |
| **Conformity assessment** | A formal evaluation demonstrating that a regulated product meets applicable legal requirements before it can be placed on the market |
