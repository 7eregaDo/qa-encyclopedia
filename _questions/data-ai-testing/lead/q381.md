## The Intent
The interviewer is checking whether you can operate as a discipline and thought leader for one of the most consequential and rapidly evolving areas of engineering quality. At lead level, the question is whether you can define what good looks like for your organisation's AI programme — and build the structures that make it achievable at scale.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** I joined a 900-person company that was two years into a significant AI investment — twelve ML-powered features in production, three agentic AI projects in development, and a data platform serving 60 downstream consumers. Each product team had its own quality approach. There was no shared definition of what "trustworthy AI" meant, no portfolio-level visibility, and a recent high-profile model incident had attracted press coverage and board attention.

**Task:** The CTO asked me to develop an organisation-wide data and AI quality vision and present it to the executive team within 60 days.

**Action:** I structured the work in three phases. Phase 1 — discovery: I spent three weeks interviewing 24 people — data scientists, data engineers, product managers, legal, compliance, and two enterprise customers who had asked about AI governance. I mapped every existing quality practice, every recent incident, and every compliance obligation. I also reviewed the AI quality frameworks published by NIST, the Alan Turing Institute, and the EU AI Act to ground the vision in recognised standards. Phase 2 — vision definition: I wrote a 6-page vision document built around five commitments. Accurate: every model is evaluated against a representative labelled test set before deployment, with results published in a model card. Fair: every AI feature affecting individual users is assessed for demographic performance parity before launch, with a designated fairness reviewer sign-off. Safe: every generative AI feature undergoes structured red-teaming before launch; every agentic feature has defined escalation paths and irreversibility guards. Transparent: every deployed AI feature has a published plain-language description of what it does, what data it uses, and its known limitations — accessible to users and customers. Reliable: every pipeline and model has defined SLOs, production monitoring, and a documented incident response path. Phase 3 — roadmap: I mapped each commitment to a 12-month delivery roadmap with named owners, tooling investments, and governance milestones. I presented the vision as a narrative — problem, principles, commitments, roadmap — not a slide deck of requirements.

**Result:** The executive team approved the vision and allocated a dedicated AI quality engineering budget for the first time. The model card standard was published and adopted within one quarter. Two enterprise customers cited the vision document in procurement discussions as evidence of mature AI governance — it directly contributed to two contract renewals. The board risk committee added AI quality to its quarterly review agenda, with my team providing the input.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Understands what trustworthy AI means and why quality standards are needed |
| **Mid** | Implements quality practices within a domain and advocates for shared standards |
| **Senior** | Designs a coherent quality programme across the AI portfolio and drives adoption through peer influence |
| **Lead** | Defines the organisation-wide AI quality vision — grounded in regulation, ethics, and business strategy — secures executive commitment, and builds the governance and culture to sustain it |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Trustworthy AI** | AI that is accurate, fair, safe, transparent, and reliable — a compound quality standard increasingly recognised in regulation and industry frameworks |
| **Model card** | A structured transparency document for a deployed model, summarising its purpose, data, performance, fairness findings, and limitations |
| **AI governance** | The structures, processes, and accountabilities that ensure AI systems are developed and operated in accordance with defined quality, ethical, and regulatory standards |
| **NIST AI RMF** | The US National Institute of Standards and Technology's AI Risk Management Framework — a widely referenced voluntary standard for responsible AI development |
| **Portfolio-level visibility** | An aggregated view of quality health across all AI products — enabling leadership to see the organisation's AI risk posture as a whole |
| **Fairness reviewer** | A designated individual who reviews and approves a model's fairness assessment before deployment — formalising accountability for equitable AI |
| **AI quality budget** | Dedicated financial and headcount resource for the AI quality programme — a signal that the organisation treats AI quality as a strategic investment rather than an incidental cost |
