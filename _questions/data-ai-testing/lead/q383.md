## The Intent
The interviewer is checking whether you can lead under the specific pressures of an AI incident — which combines the technical complexity of a data or model failure with the reputational, legal, and ethical dimensions that make it more consequential than a typical production outage. At lead level, how you respond publicly and internally defines the organisation's AI trustworthiness.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** Our AI-powered credit risk scoring model, used by a partner bank to inform lending decisions, was found to be producing systematically lower credit scores for applicants with names statistically associated with certain ethnic groups — a proxy discrimination issue that had not been detected in pre-launch fairness testing because the protected attribute had not been included in the evaluation dataset. The issue was identified by an investigative journalist who had conducted a controlled test and was preparing to publish.

**Task:** I was notified 48 hours before the article's planned publication. I needed to lead the incident response under an extreme time constraint, with simultaneous technical, legal, regulatory, and communications obligations.

**Action:** I activated a five-track response. Track 1 — technical characterisation: I assembled the data science and data engineering teams immediately to quantify the issue — which model version was affected, what the magnitude of the score disparity was, how many lending decisions had been made using the affected model, and what the false negative rate (qualified applicants incorrectly scored low) was by group. We established within four hours that 2,300 applications had received materially lower scores, of which we estimated approximately 340 may have been declined unfairly based on the disparity magnitude. Track 2 — immediate mitigation: I recommended suspending the model for new decisions immediately and reverting to the previous version pending investigation. The partner bank agreed. Track 3 — legal and regulatory: I briefed the General Counsel and the DPO within two hours. We identified two potential notification obligations: the FCA (as a regulated lending partner) and the ICO (as the proximate data controller). Notifications were prepared. Track 4 — stakeholder communication: I prepared three parallel communications — a technical briefing for the partner bank's CTO, a plain-language briefing for the bank's customer communications team (for affected applicants), and a public statement for our organisation. I insisted the public statement acknowledged the issue directly rather than minimising it — hedged corporate language in AI ethics incidents compounds reputational damage. Track 5 — root cause and systemic fix: I initiated a post-incident review with a clear output requirement — not just what went wrong with this model, but what in the evaluation process allowed it to pass. The answer was that the fairness evaluation had been designed around the demographic attributes in the training data, and the proxy variable (name-based ethnicity signal) was not in the training data, so it was not in the evaluation set.

**Result:** The article was published, but our proactive public statement — acknowledging the issue, quantifying it honestly, describing the immediate suspension, and committing to remediation and affected-applicant review — was cited by the journalist as unusual and was noted positively in the coverage. The FCA notification was accepted without enforcement action, in part because of the speed and completeness of our disclosure. The post-incident review produced a requirement that all fairness evaluations include proxy variable testing, regardless of whether the protected attribute appears in the training data. Three hundred and forty applicants received a formal review and 112 were offered the credit product they had originally applied for.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Understands that AI incidents have technical, ethical, and reputational dimensions |
| **Mid** | Can characterise a model quality incident technically and contribute to the investigation |
| **Senior** | Leads the technical investigation and coordinates remediation for an AI quality incident |
| **Lead** | Commands the full multi-track response — technical characterisation, immediate mitigation, regulatory notification, stakeholder communication, and systemic governance improvement — under time pressure and public scrutiny |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Proxy discrimination** | Indirect discrimination arising when a model uses a feature that correlates with a protected characteristic to produce systematically unequal outcomes |
| **Affected population** | The set of individuals who received materially different outcomes due to a model quality issue — quantification is essential for regulatory notification and remediation |
| **Regulatory notification** | A legally required disclosure to a regulator (e.g., FCA, ICO) when an AI incident meets defined severity thresholds — timing and completeness are critical |
| **Public statement** | An organisation's formal external communication about an AI incident — honest, specific, and action-oriented statements reduce reputational damage relative to hedged or delayed responses |
| **Immediate mitigation** | The fastest available action to stop ongoing harm — typically suspending the affected model — taken before root cause analysis is complete |
| **Post-incident review** | A structured analysis of both the immediate cause and the systemic governance gaps that allowed the incident to occur |
| **Proxy variable testing** | Fairness evaluation that explicitly tests for performance disparities on variables that may serve as proxies for protected characteristics, even when those characteristics are not in the dataset |
