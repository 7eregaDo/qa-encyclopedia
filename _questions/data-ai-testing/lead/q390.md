## The Intent
The interviewer is checking whether you think about your work in terms of durable impact rather than personal achievement. At lead level, the final measure of excellence is not what the organisation's AI looks like while you are there — it is what it looks like five years after you leave. This question asks you to articulate what you are building toward, and why permanence matters.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** After five years leading data and AI quality at my organisation, I made the decision to move into an advisory and fractional CTO role. The function I had built had grown from one person to nine, the governance programme was mature, and the team was operating largely independently. But I had seen the pattern before — in organisations where a quality leader's departure caused a regression to previous habits — and I was determined the programme would outlast me.

**Task:** I treated my final 12 months as a deliberate legacy-building phase, with a specific goal: the organisation's data and AI quality should be materially better in three years than it was at the moment I left, without any further input from me.

**Action:** I organised the legacy work into four categories. People: I identified the three engineers most capable of leading the function and worked with them on a structured succession over 12 months — each took lead ownership of a major programme area, with me in an advisory role rather than a decision-making one. By month 9 they were running the AI review board, leading the regulatory programme, and managing external partnerships without my involvement. I ensured each was formally recognised — two were promoted, one was given a public profile through conference speaking. Practices: I ran a documentation sprint — every framework, playbook, standard, and decision rationale was written down with enough context that someone who had never met me could understand not just what to do but why. I tested this by asking a new team member to use the documentation to independently conduct a fairness assessment — and iterated until they could do so without asking me a single question. Governance: I worked with the CTO to embed the AI quality governance structures into the organisation's formal operating model — the AI review board was written into the engineering governance charter, the model card standard was included in the product development policy, and the quality metrics were added to the quarterly business review template. These were no longer my initiatives — they were company policy. Field: I published a synthesis of the five years' work — a long-form practitioner guide to data and AI quality, freely available and peer-reviewed — so that the frameworks developed internally would contribute to the broader field regardless of what happened internally.

**Result:** Eighteen months after my departure, I received a message from the Head of Data Science: the AI review board had blocked a model deployment that turned out to have a significant bias issue — caught by a process I had designed, run by engineers I had developed, under governance I had institutionalised. None of it required me. That message was the legacy I had worked toward. The practitioner guide had been downloaded 14,000 times and translated into two languages by the open-source community.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Understands the importance of documentation and knowledge sharing |
| **Mid** | Builds practices that the team can follow independently and onboards colleagues proactively |
| **Senior** | Develops team members who can lead programmes independently and encodes practices in shared frameworks |
| **Lead** | Designs the full legacy — people succession, practice codification, governance institutionalisation, and field contribution — so that the discipline's quality is independent of any individual, including themselves |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Succession** | The planned development of individuals who can lead a function or programme after the incumbent moves on |
| **Documentation sprint** | A dedicated period of intensive knowledge capture — producing the written record that allows others to understand and apply frameworks without the original author's presence |
| **Institutionalisation** | The embedding of a practice or standard into an organisation's formal operating model — policy, charter, or governance structure — so it persists regardless of individual turnover |
| **Practitioner guide** | A long-form, experience-based document that synthesises accumulated knowledge for the benefit of others in the field — a form of professional legacy that extends beyond the organisation |
| **Independence test** | A validation method where someone unfamiliar with a framework attempts to use it without assistance — revealing gaps in documentation or assumed knowledge |
| **Field contribution** | Published work — open-source, articles, standards input — that shapes how the broader discipline understands and practices data and AI quality |
| **Discipline legacy** | The lasting state of a professional discipline — in this case, data and AI quality — attributable to an individual's leadership, measured by the quality of practice that persists after their direct involvement ends |
