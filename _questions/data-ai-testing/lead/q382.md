## The Intent
The interviewer is checking whether you can design and operate governance — not just run tests. At lead level, sustainable AI quality requires institutional structures that outlast any individual's tenure and create accountability at the right levels. This is as much organisational design as it is quality engineering.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** Following the adoption of the AI quality vision I had developed, the CTO asked me to turn the vision into operational governance — specifically, to establish a structure that would make consequential AI decisions reviewable by more than one person and create a clear escalation path when AI features presented ethical or safety concerns.

**Task:** I needed to design the governance structure, define the roles and their decision rights, and stand it up within a quarter.

**Action:** I designed a three-tier governance model. Tier 1 — feature-level quality gate: every AI feature release required a completed quality artefact set (model card, fairness report, red-team findings for generative AI, monitoring plan) reviewed and signed off by a designated AI quality reviewer — a senior engineer outside the feature team. This was the routine gate for all AI releases. Tier 2 — AI review board: a standing monthly forum of eight members — two engineering leads, the Head of Data Science, the General Counsel, the Chief People Officer (for HR-adjacent AI), the Head of Product, and two rotating engineers from product teams. The board reviewed any AI feature rated high-risk under our internal risk classification (consequential decisions about individuals, regulated industries, generative public-facing features), any feature where the Tier 1 reviewer had concerns they could not resolve, and any production AI incident with a severity rating of P1 or P2. The board had authority to approve, reject, or require modification before deployment. Tier 3 — executive AI accountability: the CTO owned AI quality at board level, with a quarterly briefing I prepared covering portfolio health, incident trends, regulatory developments, and open governance decisions. I also appointed domain AI quality leads — senior engineers in each major product area responsible for coaching their teams on the standards and being the first point of contact for Tier 1 reviews. This distributed expertise without creating a bottleneck.

**Result:** The AI review board reviewed 14 features in its first six months. Three were returned for modification before approval — including a pricing optimisation model that the General Counsel identified as potentially constituting dynamic pricing discrimination under consumer protection law, a risk no purely technical review would have surfaced. The board's multi-disciplinary composition was cited by the legal team as the specific reason the legal risk was caught. One enterprise customer's procurement team asked to review the governance structure as part of due diligence — and approved the contract in part because the structure existed.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Understands that AI governance involves more than technical review |
| **Mid** | Participates in governance processes and contributes quality artefacts for review |
| **Senior** | Designs and operates quality gates for AI features and advises on governance structures |
| **Lead** | Establishes the governance institution — board composition, decision rights, review processes, escalation paths, and executive accountability — and sustains it as a durable organisational structure |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **AI review board** | A multi-disciplinary standing body with authority to review, approve, or reject AI feature deployments based on quality, ethical, and regulatory criteria |
| **Decision rights** | The defined authority each governance tier or role has to approve, reject, or escalate a decision |
| **Risk classification** | A pre-deployment assessment categorising an AI feature by its potential for harm — determining which governance tier reviews it |
| **Multi-disciplinary governance** | A review structure that includes legal, HR, product, and engineering perspectives — necessary because AI risks span technical, legal, and ethical domains simultaneously |
| **Domain AI quality lead** | A senior engineer embedded in a product area responsible for coaching teams on AI quality standards and conducting Tier 1 reviews |
| **Dynamic pricing discrimination** | A practice where algorithmic pricing systematically charges different prices to different groups in ways that may violate consumer protection law |
| **Governance artefact** | A structured document (model card, fairness report, red-team findings) produced as evidence of quality assessment — the input to governance review |
