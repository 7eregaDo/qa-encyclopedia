## The Intent
The interviewer is checking whether you can operate at the business level, not just the technical level. At lead level, securing investment, headcount, and influence for the data and AI quality function requires making the commercial case — and that means connecting quality directly to the outcomes the business cares about.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** Eighteen months after establishing the data and AI quality function, I was preparing the annual budget review. My team had grown from two to six engineers and had prevented several high-risk AI releases — but the value was largely invisible to the finance and commercial teams because it was expressed in incidents avoided rather than revenue generated. The CFO had flagged the function's cost and asked for a business case.

**Task:** I needed to articulate the commercial value of the data and AI quality function in terms the CFO and CEO would act on, not just validate for the CTO.

**Action:** I built the business case across four value streams. Stream 1 — revenue protection through incident prevention: I documented the three highest-severity AI incidents the function had prevented — the two deployment blockers from the AI review board and one model quality regression caught by canary monitoring. I worked with the commercial team to estimate the revenue and contract risk each would have created if it had reached production. The total estimated avoidance was £4.2M. Stream 2 — contract enablement: I pulled data from the sales team showing that 14 enterprise contract conversations in the previous year had included an explicit question about AI governance. Of these, 11 had proceeded to contract; three had stalled. I interviewed the account managers and confirmed that in at least six cases, the existence of our governance documentation and model cards had been cited by the customer's procurement team as a deciding factor. I estimated conservatively that the governance capability had contributed to £2.8M in contract value. Stream 3 — regulatory cost avoidance: I worked with the legal team to estimate the cost of the FCA and ICO regulatory processes we had avoided by proactive notification and pre-market controls on the credit scoring incident. Legal estimated the avoided regulatory cost (fines, remediation, legal fees) at £800K–£1.5M. Stream 4 — competitive positioning: I obtained three publicly available examples of competitors who had experienced AI quality or ethics incidents, and documented the market share and brand impact they had suffered in the following two quarters. I presented these as the "do nothing" scenario. The total case: the function cost £480K per year to run. The documented value attributable to it in the previous 12 months was between £7.8M and £8.5M — a return of 16–18× on investment.

**Result:** The budget was approved without reduction. The CFO asked me to present the framework to the board as a model for how engineering functions should articulate value. The commercial team asked me to contribute a section on AI governance to their enterprise sales deck — positioning quality as a product differentiator in competitive bids.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Understands that quality has business value beyond defect prevention |
| **Mid** | Can connect quality findings to customer and business impact in stakeholder reporting |
| **Senior** | Frames the quality programme's value in terms of risk reduction and compliance enablement for engineering leadership |
| **Lead** | Builds the full commercial value case for the data and AI quality function — incident prevention, contract enablement, regulatory cost avoidance, and competitive positioning — and presents it to CFO and board level |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Commercial differentiator** | A capability that enables an organisation to win business, retain customers, or command premium pricing relative to competitors |
| **Revenue protection** | The value of preventing incidents that would have caused customer churn, contract loss, or regulatory penalty |
| **Contract enablement** | The contribution of quality capabilities to winning or retaining commercial contracts, particularly with enterprise customers who conduct governance due diligence |
| **Regulatory cost avoidance** | The estimated value of regulatory fines, remediation costs, and legal fees avoided through proactive compliance and quality controls |
| **Return on investment (ROI)** | The ratio of value generated to cost incurred — expressed here as the commercial value attributable to the quality function relative to its operating cost |
| **Do-nothing scenario** | A presentation of what outcomes would have occurred without the quality investment — the counterfactual that makes avoided costs visible |
| **Enterprise sales deck** | The commercial presentation used by sales teams in enterprise deals — inclusion of AI governance messaging signals that quality is a product capability, not a back-office function |
