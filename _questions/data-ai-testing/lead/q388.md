## The Intent
The interviewer is checking whether you operate as a field leader, not just an organisational one. At lead level, the most influential quality practitioners shape how the industry thinks about AI quality — contributing to standards, publishing findings, and participating in cross-industry conversations that define what good looks like for everyone.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** Three years into leading data and AI quality at my organisation, I had developed frameworks — the fairness testing approach, the red-teaming playbook, the agentic AI test taxonomy — that I believed were genuinely novel and applicable beyond our organisation. At the same time, I was aware that the field lacked shared standards: each organisation was independently solving the same problems, often poorly, and the resulting inconsistency created risk for users and the public.

**Task:** I wanted to contribute meaningfully to the external community — not for personal visibility, but to accelerate the maturation of AI quality as a discipline and to learn from practitioners solving problems I had not yet encountered.

**Action:** I pursued four external engagement tracks simultaneously. Track 1 — standards participation: I joined the BSI working group developing the UK's AI assurance guidance, contributing the practitioner perspective on what quality evidence was realistically producible at different organisation sizes. I contributed two sections of the guidance document. Track 2 — open-source contribution: I open-sourced our agentic AI test taxonomy under an Apache 2.0 licence, publishing it with documentation and example test cases. Within four months it had 340 GitHub stars and had been adopted by two other organisations who contacted me directly. Track 3 — published writing: I wrote three long-form articles — on fairness testing for consequential AI, on governing third-party model dependencies, and on red-teaming methodology — published in a peer-reviewed software engineering journal and two practitioner publications. Each was reviewed and improved by external experts before publication, which improved my own frameworks as much as it shared them. Track 4 — conference programme committee: I joined the programme committee for a major AI engineering conference, reviewing 40 submissions per year. This gave me early visibility into emerging practice across the industry and built relationships with quality practitioners at organisations facing analogous problems.

**Result:** The open-source taxonomy was cited in two academic papers. The BSI guidance was adopted as the basis for a UK government AI assurance pilot. A candidate I hired in year four cited the published articles as the reason they applied — the writing had shaped their thinking on the discipline. Two competitor organisations contacted me to ask about collaboration on shared test tooling — a signal that external engagement creates network effects that benefit the organisation commercially as well as the field. I was invited to brief a parliamentary select committee on AI quality standards — the most direct policy influence I had achieved.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|-------|
| **Entry** | Reads external resources and follows developments in the AI quality and safety field |
| **Mid** | Participates in external communities — conferences, forums, open-source projects — and applies external learning to their team's practice |
| **Senior** | Publishes findings, contributes to open-source tooling, and represents the organisation in external conversations about AI quality |
| **Lead** | Shapes the field — contributing to standards bodies, publishing peer-reviewed work, open-sourcing frameworks, and participating in policy — amplifying quality leadership beyond the organisation's boundaries |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Standards body** | An organisation that develops and maintains technical or professional standards — such as BSI (British Standards Institution), ISO, or NIST — whose outputs become reference points for industry practice |
| **Open-source contribution** | Publishing internal tools, frameworks, or documentation under a permissive licence for public use and collaborative improvement |
| **Peer-reviewed publication** | A written work evaluated by independent experts before publication — conferring credibility and ensuring quality of the contribution |
| **Programme committee** | The body responsible for reviewing and selecting submissions to a conference — membership provides early access to emerging practice across the industry |
| **Network effects** | The compounding value created when external engagement generates relationships, collaborations, and inbound opportunities that benefit the organisation beyond the original engagement |
| **Policy influence** | Contribution to government or regulatory thinking — the highest-leverage form of external engagement for a quality discipline leader |
| **Apache 2.0 licence** | A permissive open-source licence allowing free use, modification, and distribution of software with attribution — widely used for corporate open-source contributions |
