## The Intent
The interviewer is checking whether you are aware of the regulatory landscape and can connect it to practical testing changes. At senior level, AI regulation is not a legal team problem — it directly shapes what evidence QA must produce, what risks must be tested, and what documentation must be maintained.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** My organisation was building an AI system used by HR teams to inform promotion decisions — a use case that falls under the EU AI Act's high-risk category for employment-related AI. The legal team flagged the regulatory exposure and asked me to assess what the Act required from a testing and documentation perspective.

**Task:** I needed to understand the Act's requirements for high-risk AI systems, translate them into specific testing obligations, and propose a compliance-ready QA approach.

**Action:** I reviewed the EU AI Act's requirements for high-risk AI systems and mapped them to QA practices. Accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity: the Act requires that high-risk AI systems achieve appropriate levels of accuracy and are resilient to attempts to alter their behaviour. This translated to mandatory accuracy benchmarking on a representative test set, adversarial robustness testing, and documentation of the accuracy threshold and how it was determined. Data governance: training, validation, and test data must be subject to data governance practices that ensure relevance, representativeness, and freedom from errors. This translated to a training data quality report and a formal data governance sign-off before each model release. Human oversight: the system must be designed to allow human oversight and must be capable of being overridden. I added an explicit human override test — verifying that a hiring manager could reject or override the model's recommendation at every decision point. Transparency and documentation: the Act requires technical documentation sufficient to demonstrate compliance, retained for 10 years. I designed a model card template — a structured document recording the model's purpose, training data, evaluation results, fairness assessment, limitations, and intended use — as the standard release artefact. I also flagged that any post-market monitoring findings must be reported to the relevant authority if they indicate a risk.

**Result:** The legal team approved the QA approach as compliant with the Act's requirements. The model card template was adopted as the standard for all AI feature releases, not just high-risk ones. The compliance documentation was cited positively in a due diligence review by a prospective enterprise customer who asked specifically about our AI governance posture.

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **EU AI Act** | European Union legislation that classifies AI systems by risk level and imposes mandatory conformity, documentation, and oversight requirements on high-risk applications |
| **High-risk AI** | An AI system used in contexts with significant potential impact on people's rights or safety — including employment, education, credit, and law enforcement |
| **Conformity assessment** | A mandatory evaluation process demonstrating that a high-risk AI system meets the Act's requirements before it can be deployed |
| **Model card** | A structured document summarising a model's purpose, training data, evaluation results, fairness findings, limitations, and intended use — a standard transparency and compliance artefact |
| **Post-market monitoring** | Ongoing collection of performance and safety data from a deployed AI system, required under the EU AI Act for high-risk applications |
| **Auditability** | The property of a testing and documentation process that allows an external auditor to verify that quality obligations were met — requires structured records, not just internal confidence |
| **Human override** | A design and testing requirement ensuring that a human operator can intervene, correct, or reject an AI system's output at any decision point |
