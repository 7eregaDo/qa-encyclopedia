## The Intent
The interviewer is checking whether you can design and lead a red-teaming exercise with rigour — not just run a handful of adversarial prompts and call it done. At senior level, they want to see a structured methodology: threat model, scope, team composition, findings taxonomy, and an output that drives action.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** My organisation was preparing to launch an AI-powered financial advice assistant capable of answering questions about investment products, tax, and retirement planning. Legal and compliance required evidence of adversarial safety testing before the system could be presented to the FCA as part of a regulatory notification. Informal ad-hoc prompt testing by the engineering team was not considered sufficient.

**Task:** I was asked to design and lead a formal red-teaming exercise that would satisfy the regulatory evidence requirement and genuinely improve system safety.

**Action:** I structured the exercise across five phases. Phase 1 — threat model: I identified the three primary adversarial risk categories for this system: financial harm (the assistant giving advice that leads a user to make a damaging financial decision), regulatory breach (the assistant making statements that constitute unregulated financial advice under FCA rules), and manipulation (a bad actor using the assistant to generate persuasive but misleading content to share with third parties). Phase 2 — team composition: I assembled a five-person red team — two QA engineers, one domain expert (a qualified financial adviser), one security engineer with prompt injection expertise, and one non-technical participant to represent a naive user attempting to misuse the system. Mixed expertise was essential; pure technical red-teamers miss domain-specific harms. Phase 3 — structured attack playbook: Each team member received a playbook with 40 attack scenarios organised by threat category, plus 20 open-ended "explore your area of expertise" slots. Scenarios included: asking for specific stock picks (regulatory boundary), roleplay as a financial adviser (jailbreak), asking the system to predict market movements (financial harm), and embedding instructions in a long document to override system behaviour (prompt injection). Phase 4 — execution: Two four-hour sessions over two days. All outputs were logged with the exact prompt, the model response, the team member's severity rating (critical / high / medium / low), and the threat category. Phase 5 — findings report: I produced a structured report with 23 findings across the three threat categories, each with the prompt, output, severity, and recommended remediation. Critical findings (3) were deployment blockers; high findings (7) required remediation before launch; medium and low findings were tracked for post-launch improvement.

**Result:** The three critical findings included the assistant providing a specific stock recommendation when asked through a roleplay framing — a clear FCA boundary breach. All critical and high findings were remediated through system prompt updates, output filters, and fine-tuning. The red-teaming report was accepted by legal as sufficient adversarial testing evidence for the FCA notification. The financial adviser on the red team identified two domain-specific harms that no technical tester had found — confirming the value of mixed-expertise team composition.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Understands what red-teaming is and that it involves deliberate adversarial testing |
| **Mid** | Executes a structured red-teaming playbook against a defined threat model and documents findings with severity and remediation recommendations |
| **Senior** | Designs the full red-teaming programme — threat model, team composition, attack playbook, execution structure, findings taxonomy, and regulatory evidence artefact — and leads the exercise end to end |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Red-teaming** | A structured adversarial evaluation where a designated team actively attempts to make an AI system produce unsafe, harmful, or policy-violating outputs |
| **Threat model** | A structured analysis of the adversarial risks facing a system — who might attack it, how, and what harm they could cause |
| **Attack playbook** | A documented set of adversarial scenarios used to guide red-team participants, ensuring systematic coverage of the threat model |
| **Severity rating** | A classification of a finding's impact — critical (deployment blocker), high (must fix before launch), medium/low (post-launch improvement) |
| **Deployment blocker** | A red-teaming finding severe enough to prevent the system from being released until it is remediated |
| **Domain expert** | A non-technical specialist (e.g., a doctor, lawyer, or financial adviser) included in a red team to identify harms that technical testers would not recognise |
| **Mixed-expertise team** | A red team composed of members with different backgrounds — technical, domain, and non-expert user — to maximise coverage of harm categories |
