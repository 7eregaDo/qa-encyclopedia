## The Intent
The interviewer is checking whether you understand that data mesh changes the organisational model for data quality — not just the technical architecture. At senior level, they want to see that you can design a quality approach that works with decentralised ownership rather than against it.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** My organisation was migrating from a centralised data warehouse model (one data engineering team responsible for all pipelines) to a data mesh (eight domain teams each owning their own data products). The existing QA approach — a centralised team running quality checks against the warehouse — would not survive the migration: there was no central point to check, and the centralised team could not scale to eight domains.

**Task:** I was asked to design a quality framework that would work in the new decentralised model, maintaining consistency across domains without recreating a centralised bottleneck.

**Action:** I designed the framework on three principles. Principle 1 — federated quality ownership: each domain team was accountable for the quality of their data products, with a defined quality standard they had to meet. I worked with domain leads to co-author the standard rather than imposing it — ensuring they owned it. The standard defined the minimum required monitors (freshness, volume, schema, null rate) and the required data contract format for any data product consumed by another domain. Principle 2 — shared tooling, not shared control: I selected and provided a standard tooling stack (Great Expectations for quality checks, a shared contract schema format, a central data catalogue for lineage visibility) that any domain team could adopt without configuration expertise. The tooling lowered the cost of compliance; it did not mandate how teams used it internally. Principle 3 — interoperability contracts: cross-domain data consumption required a published contract. I designed a contract review process — lightweight, asynchronous, with a 48-hour response SLA — so that any change to a data product with consumers required the consuming teams to acknowledge the change before it was deployed. I ran a two-day workshop introducing all eight domain teams to the standard, the tooling, and the contract process simultaneously, to prevent fragmentation from eight separate onboarding paths.

**Result:** Within four months, all eight domains had published data product contracts and implemented the minimum monitor set. The first cross-domain contract violation alert fired in month two — an upstream domain changed a field type without updating the contract. The consuming domain was notified within 30 minutes and the producing domain corrected the contract before the change reached production. The centralised quality team reduced from five engineers to two — focused on standards, tooling, and cross-domain governance rather than running checks.

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Data mesh** | An architectural and organisational approach where data is owned and served by domain teams as products, rather than managed centrally by a dedicated data engineering team |
| **Data product** | A domain-owned, versioned, documented data asset made available to consumers with defined quality standards and a published contract |
| **Federated governance** | A model where organisation-wide standards are set centrally but implemented and enforced autonomously by each domain team |
| **Interoperability** | The ability of data products from different domains to be combined and consumed reliably — enabled through shared contract standards and catalogue metadata |
| **Data catalogue** | A searchable inventory of data assets, their owners, schemas, lineage, and quality status — essential for visibility in a decentralised data mesh |
| **Cross-domain contract** | A formal agreement between a producing domain and a consuming domain specifying the schema, quality, and freshness expectations for a shared data asset |
| **Self-serve infrastructure** | Shared tooling and platforms that enable domain teams to implement quality practices without requiring specialised expertise or central team involvement |
