## The Intent
The interviewer is checking whether you can navigate the intersection of technical measurement and ethical and legal accountability. At senior level, fairness testing is not a one-off bias check — it is a programme with defined standards, governance, and ongoing measurement.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** My organisation was building an AI system to assist hiring managers in shortlisting job applicants. Legal had flagged that the system was subject to equality legislation and asked the engineering team for assurance that it did not discriminate. The engineering team had no fairness testing in place and no clear understanding of what "fairness" meant technically.

**Task:** I was asked to design a fairness testing framework and present it to the legal and HR teams alongside engineering leadership.

**Action:** I structured the framework in four steps. Step 1 — define fairness criteria: I researched the three most common fairness definitions and assessed which applied to our use case. Demographic parity (equal shortlist rates across groups) was legally relevant under equal opportunity principles but technically problematic for roles where the qualified applicant pool was not demographically uniform. Equalised odds (equal true positive and false positive rates across groups) was more appropriate — it asked whether the model was equally good at identifying qualified candidates regardless of group. I documented the chosen criterion and the legal rationale with input from the legal team. Step 2 — identify protected attributes: working with HR and legal, I identified gender, ethnicity, age, and disability status as the relevant protected characteristics for our jurisdiction. I also identified proxy variables that could serve as indirect proxies — employment gap, institution attended, postcode — and flagged these for review. Step 3 — measurement: I built a test dataset of 1,800 CVs with human-expert labels (shortlist / do not shortlist) balanced across groups. I measured equalised odds — the difference in true positive rate and false positive rate between groups. I found a 14-point gap in true positive rate for candidates from underrepresented ethnic groups — the model was correctly identifying qualified majority-group candidates at a much higher rate. Step 4 — governance: I proposed that every model retrain required a fairness evaluation report approved by a designated fairness reviewer (a combination of a senior engineer and the HR lead) before deployment. I also defined a fairness threshold — no demographic group's true positive rate could be more than 5 percentage points below the highest group — as a hard deployment gate.

**Result:** The model was not deployed. The training data was found to contain historical hiring decisions made by managers later identified as having exhibited discriminatory behaviour — the model had learned and amplified those patterns. The data set was reconstructed using expert-labelled data. I presented the framework to the board as part of the AI governance review, where it was adopted as the standard for all consequential AI decisions.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Can define demographic parity and explain why model accuracy alone does not guarantee fairness |
| **Mid** | Implements fairness metrics across protected groups and identifies proxy variables — and presents findings with business and legal context |
| **Senior** | Designs a complete fairness testing framework — criterion selection, measurement methodology, governance process, and deployment gates — for AI systems in legally regulated contexts |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Demographic parity** | A fairness criterion requiring that a model's positive prediction rate is equal across demographic groups |
| **Equalised odds** | A fairness criterion requiring that a model's true positive rate and false positive rate are equal across groups — ensuring equal performance regardless of group membership |
| **Protected characteristic** | An attribute (gender, race, age, disability) protected against discrimination by law — relevant to fairness assessment in consequential AI decisions |
| **Proxy variable** | A feature that indirectly encodes a protected characteristic, introducing indirect discrimination even without explicit use of the protected attribute |
| **Fairness threshold** | A defined maximum acceptable disparity between groups on a chosen fairness metric — used as a hard deployment gate |
| **Fairness reviewer** | A designated individual or panel responsible for approving a model's fairness evaluation report before deployment |
| **Historical bias** | Bias introduced when a model is trained on data reflecting past discriminatory human decisions — the model learns and perpetuates those patterns |
