## The Intent
The interviewer is checking whether you understand that the choice of metric is itself a quality decision — not a neutral technical detail. At mid level, they want to see that you can interrogate the metric selection and ensure it aligns with what the model actually needs to do.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** My team was evaluating a model to identify patients at risk of hospital readmission within 30 days. The data science team reported 94% accuracy and considered the model ready for review. I was asked to assess whether the evaluation approach was sound before QA sign-off.

**Task:** I needed to review the metric selection and determine whether 94% accuracy was a meaningful result for this use case.

**Action:** I examined the class distribution in the test set: 6% of patients were readmitted within 30 days, 94% were not. This meant a model that predicted "not readmitted" for every single patient would achieve 94% accuracy without learning anything. The accuracy metric was meaningless for this imbalanced problem. I reframed the evaluation around the metrics appropriate to the business objective. The cost of a false negative (missing a patient who will be readmitted) was much higher than a false positive (flagging a patient who would not be readmitted — the downside was an unnecessary check-in call). Therefore, recall for the positive class was the primary metric — we needed to catch as many at-risk patients as possible. I ran the model evaluation using recall, precision, F1, and the ROC-AUC score, and presented a confusion matrix. Recall for the positive class was 41% — the model was missing 59% of patients who would actually be readmitted. The 94% accuracy figure had masked a model that was clinically inadequate.

**Result:** The data science team retrained with a modified class weighting to prioritise recall. The new model achieved 73% recall at acceptable precision. I documented the metric selection rationale as part of the test plan — so that future model updates would be evaluated against the same business-appropriate metrics.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Can define accuracy, precision, and recall and explain why accuracy alone is insufficient for imbalanced datasets |
| **Mid** | Selects evaluation metrics based on business objective and error cost asymmetry, evaluates models using a confusion matrix, and challenges inappropriate metric choices |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Class imbalance** | A condition where one class in a classification dataset is significantly more frequent than another — making accuracy a misleading metric |
| **Recall (sensitivity)** | Of all actual positive instances, the proportion correctly identified by the model — the critical metric when false negatives are costly |
| **Precision** | Of all instances predicted as positive, the proportion that are actually positive — the critical metric when false positives are costly |
| **F1 score** | The harmonic mean of precision and recall — a balanced metric when both false positives and false negatives matter |
| **ROC-AUC** | The area under the Receiver Operating Characteristic curve — a threshold-independent measure of a model's ability to discriminate between classes |
| **Confusion matrix** | A table showing true positives, false positives, true negatives, and false negatives — the foundation for computing all classification metrics |
| **Error cost asymmetry** | The condition where the business cost of one type of error (false negative) is materially different from another (false positive) — determines which metric to prioritise |
