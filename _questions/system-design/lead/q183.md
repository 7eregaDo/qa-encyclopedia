## The Intent
The interviewer is testing whether you can balance team autonomy with systemic coherence. In organisations with many squads, quality erodes not from malice but from local optimisation — teams make reasonable individual decisions that collectively produce an untestable system. A lead should have a model for preventing this without becoming a bottleneck.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** At a platform company with 12 product squads, each squad made independent architecture decisions. Over 18 months this had produced a system where five different logging formats existed, three different approaches to service-to-service authentication were in use, and there was no consistent approach to health endpoints or readiness probes. Integration testing was nearly impossible because the system had no shared conventions.

**Task:** As Principal QE, establish a governance model that restored testability conventions without slowing squad velocity or creating a centralised approval bottleneck.

**Action:** I introduced a three-layer governance model. Layer one: quality fitness functions — automated checks embedded in the CI pipeline that verified every service met a baseline testability contract. This included: a structured logging format check, a health endpoint returning the required schema, a readiness probe, and a contract test present for every declared API consumer. These were enforced, not advisory. A service that failed them could not promote to staging. Layer two: Architecture Decision Records with a lightweight async review process. Any squad making a decision that affected testability or cross-service contracts posted a short ADR in a shared Slack channel with a 48-hour review window. Other squads and the QE platform team could comment; silence was approval. This surfaced 80% of cross-cutting decisions with minimal overhead. Layer three: quarterly architecture council — a two-hour forum with one representative per squad reviewing patterns that had emerged, retiring outdated standards, and making explicit decisions on new ones. I facilitated this and published decisions as versioned standards.

**Result:** Within three quarters, all new services conformed to the testability contract from day one. The fitness functions caught 14 non-compliant patterns before they reached staging. The ADR process prevented two architectural divergences that would have required expensive retrofitting. Squads reported that the standards reduced decision fatigue rather than constraining them.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Follows established conventions; may not know where they come from |
| **Mid** | Raises testability concerns in design reviews; advocates for shared conventions within their squad |
| **Senior** | Defines testability standards for their service area; participates in cross-team design reviews |
| **Lead** | Designs the governance model itself — fitness functions, ADR process, and architecture council — balancing enforcement with autonomy and publishing versioned standards across the organisation |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Architecture Fitness Function** | An automated check that verifies a specific architectural property of a system — such as a logging format or API contract — is maintained as the codebase evolves |
| **Testability Contract** | A defined set of properties a service must expose — health endpoints, structured logs, consumer contracts — that enable it to be integrated, monitored, and tested by other teams |
| **Async Review Process** | A design review mechanism conducted asynchronously (typically via comments in a shared channel or document) with a defined response window, avoiding synchronous meeting overhead |
| **Architecture Council** | A recurring forum where representatives from engineering teams review emerging patterns, retire outdated standards, and make explicit cross-cutting architecture decisions |
| **Readiness Probe** | An endpoint or check that indicates whether a service is ready to receive traffic, used by orchestration platforms (e.g. Kubernetes) and test harnesses to determine when a service is testable |
| **Local Optimisation** | A decision that is rational from a single team's perspective but produces a globally suboptimal outcome when replicated across many teams |
| **Principal QE** | A senior individual contributor quality engineering role responsible for cross-team technical quality strategy, standards, and tooling without direct management authority |
| **Versioned Standard** | A quality or architecture convention published with a version number, enabling teams to understand what changed and when, and to migrate at a managed pace |
