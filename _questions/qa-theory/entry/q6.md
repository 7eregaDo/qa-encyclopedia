## The Intent

The interviewer wants to know whether you understand testing as a planned, managed activity rather than ad-hoc bug hunting. Even at entry level, knowing the components of a test plan shows you can contribute to structured QA processes and understand the bigger picture beyond writing individual test cases.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** I joined a project mid-cycle where testing was happening informally — different testers were checking different things with no shared agreement on scope or approach.

**Task:** I was asked to formalise the testing effort for the remaining two sprints before release.

**Action:** I drafted a lightweight test plan covering: the objectives (what we were trying to achieve), scope (which features were in and out of scope), test approach (functional regression via manual testing, smoke tests after each deploy), entry and exit criteria, a resource list, and a simple risk section noting that the payment module was high-risk and needed extra coverage. I shared it with the team for sign-off before testing began.

**Result:** Everyone aligned on scope within a single meeting. We avoided wasted effort testing already-descoped features, and the payment module received the focused attention it needed — catching two critical defects before release.

---

## Key Terms

| Section | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Objectives** | What the test effort is intended to achieve. |
| **Scope** | What is and is not being tested. |
| **Test Approach** | The types of testing to be performed and the techniques to be used. |
| **Entry Criteria** | Conditions that must be met before testing can begin. |
| **Exit Criteria** | Conditions that must be met before testing is considered complete. |
| **Resources** | People, tools, and environments required. |
| **Schedule** | Timeline for test activities. |
| **Risks & Mitigations** | Known risks to the test effort and how they will be managed. |
