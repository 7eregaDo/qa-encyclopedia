---

## The Intent

The interviewer is checking whether you have formal, structured knowledge of the discipline — not just practical habits. Knowing these principles shows you understand the inherent limitations of testing and can reason about strategy rather than blindly executing test cases.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** Early in a project, I was asked by a developer, "So when you're done testing, can we say it's bug-free?"

**Task:** I needed to set accurate expectations without undermining confidence in the release.

**Action:** I explained that testing can only prove defects exist, not that they don't — referencing principle 1. I also walked through our risk-based approach: we focused testing on the payment and authentication modules (defect clustering, principle 4) rather than trying to test every possible input combination (exhaustive testing is impossible, principle 2).

**Result:** The team aligned on a shared understanding of what "tested" means. The release went ahead with clear, documented risk acceptance rather than a false promise of perfection.

## Key Terms

| # | Principle | Core Idea |
|---|-----------|-----------|
| 1 | **Testing shows presence of defects** | Testing can find bugs; it cannot prove there are none. |
| 2 | **Exhaustive testing is impossible** | You cannot test every input, path, or combination. Prioritise by risk. |
| 3 | **Early testing** | Finding defects early is far cheaper than finding them in production. |
| 4 | **Defect clustering** | A small number of modules typically contain the majority of defects. |
| 5 | **Pesticide paradox** | Repeating the same tests will stop finding new bugs. Tests must evolve. |
| 6 | **Context-dependent** | Testing an e-commerce site differs from testing medical software. |
| 7 | **Absence-of-errors fallacy** | A bug-free system is useless if it doesn't meet user needs. |
