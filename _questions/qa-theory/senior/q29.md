## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing your ability to manage quality as a measurable discipline. Senior QA engineers are expected to define, track, and act on metrics — not just report them. They also want to see that you understand the dangers of gaming metrics and can select indicators that reflect real quality rather than QA activity.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** I was asked by the engineering director to create a QA health dashboard that would be reviewed at monthly leadership meetings. The existing reporting was limited to "number of test cases executed" and "number of bugs found" — both of which were activity metrics with no signal about quality outcomes.

**Task:** I needed to design a metric framework that measured what actually mattered — effectiveness, efficiency, and business impact — and could be understood by both technical and non-technical audiences.

**Action:** I organised the framework into four categories. **Effectiveness metrics**: escaped defect rate (defects reaching production per release — the primary outcome metric), defect detection rate by phase (what percentage of defects are we finding pre-production, and where), and defect rejection rate (how often developers dispute defects — high rates suggest test quality or communication issues). **Efficiency metrics**: test execution time (pipeline runtime trends), test automation coverage (percentage of regression suite automated), and mean time to test a hotfix (how quickly can QA respond to an urgent fix). **Suite health metrics**: flake rate (percentage of non-deterministic test runs), test maintenance ratio (time spent maintaining tests vs. writing new ones), and test age distribution (identifying tests that haven't been updated in 12+ months as debt candidates). **Business impact metrics**: production incident frequency (P1/P2 incidents per quarter), mean time to detect a production defect, and customer-reported defect rate (issues raised by users rather than QA). I presented the full set as a dashboard with trend lines — the direction of travel mattered more than any single number.

**Result:** The dashboard became a standing agenda item. Within two quarters, the visibility of flake rate led to a dedicated maintenance sprint. The escaped defect rate trend — consistently declining — became the headline metric the CTO used to communicate engineering quality to the board.

## Key Terms

| Metric | Category | What It Measures |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| **Escaped Defect Rate** | Effectiveness | Defects reaching production — the primary quality outcome metric. |
| **Defect Detection Rate by Phase** | Effectiveness | Where in the SDLC defects are being found. |
| **Flake Rate** | Suite Health | Percentage of test runs with non-deterministic results. |
| **Automation Coverage** | Efficiency | Percentage of regression tests that are automated. |
| **Test Execution Time** | Efficiency | Total pipeline runtime — impacts delivery frequency. |
| **Customer-Reported Defect Rate** | Business Impact | Defects found by users rather than QA — the ultimate escaped defect signal. |
| **Mean Time to Detect (MTTD)** | Business Impact | Average time between a production defect being introduced and being detected. |
