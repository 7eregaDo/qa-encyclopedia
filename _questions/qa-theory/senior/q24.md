theory: "The Cost of Quality (CoQ) model categorises all quality-related costs into four buckets: prevention costs (activities to avoid defects), appraisal costs (activities to detect defects), internal failure costs (defects found before production), and external failure costs (defects found after production). The model demonstrates that investing in prevention and appraisal reduces the far more expensive failure costs."
---

## The Intent

The interviewer wants to see that you can make economic arguments for quality investment — not just technical ones. Influencing engineering and product decisions requires speaking the language of cost, risk, and return. The CoQ model is the senior QA engineer's primary tool for doing that credibly.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** The development team was pushing to cut the automated regression suite from the CI pipeline to speed up build times. The argument was that "tests slow us down." The pipeline was taking 45 minutes and teams were frustrated.

**Task:** I needed to respond with a business case, not a technical argument, to prevent a decision that would increase long-term quality costs significantly.

**Action:** I pulled six months of data and built a CoQ analysis. I calculated our current **appraisal costs** (the 45-minute pipeline × daily runs × engineer cost per hour = approximately £18,000/year). I then calculated our **internal failure costs** over the same period (average 3 hours to triage, fix, and retest a defect caught in staging × 180 defects × average engineer cost = £54,000). Finally, I estimated **external failure costs** from the 12 defects that had escaped to production (average 8 hours of incident response, hotfix, and retest × £150/hr = £14,400, plus estimated customer churn impact). The total cost of failures was over four times the appraisal investment. I then modelled what would happen if we cut the pipeline: assuming even a 20% increase in escaped defects, external failure costs would rise by £35,000/year — nearly double the pipeline cost. As an alternative, I proposed optimising the pipeline (parallelisation, test splitting) to cut runtime to 18 minutes rather than removing tests.

**Result:** The proposal to remove tests was withdrawn. The pipeline was optimised instead, cutting runtime to 20 minutes. The CoQ framing shifted the conversation from "tests are overhead" to "tests are cheaper than failures."

---

## Key Terms

| CoQ Category | Examples |
|-------------|---------|
| **Prevention Costs** | Test strategy design, team training, process improvement, static analysis tooling. |
| **Appraisal Costs** | Test execution, QA salaries, test environments, automated pipeline runtime. |
| **Internal Failure Costs** | Defect rework, retest cycles, sprint delays, failed deployments. |
| **External Failure Costs** | Production incidents, hotfixes, customer support, SLA penalties, churn, reputational damage. |
| **Defect Cost Curve** | The exponential increase in defect fix cost as discovery moves later in the SDLC. |
| **Optimum Quality Cost** | The point where the cost of further prevention/appraisal investment exceeds the reduction in failure costs. |
