## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing whether you can operate at a strategic level — not just execute within an existing framework but design one. This is the defining question for senior and lead QA roles. They want to see structured thinking, stakeholder awareness, and the ability to translate business goals into a quality programme.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** I joined a scale-up as their first dedicated QA hire. There was no test strategy, no formal test cases, no environments policy, and no shared definition of quality. The team was shipping weekly with growing confidence issues.

**Task:** I needed to build a QA function from zero — without disrupting a team that was already moving fast and was sceptical of "process overhead."

**Action:** I worked through five stages. First, **discovery**: I spent two weeks observing — attending standups, reading post-mortems, reviewing the backlog, and interviewing developers and the product owner about where pain lived. I didn't prescribe anything yet. Second, **risk assessment**: I mapped the product's risk landscape — which areas were revenue-critical, which had the most churn, which were least tested. This gave me a prioritisation foundation. Third, **strategy definition**: I wrote a lightweight QA strategy document covering: testing scope and types (functional, regression, API, exploratory), tool choices (Playwright for E2E, Postman for API, Jira for defect tracking), environments policy (dev, staging, prod), entry and exit criteria, and a coverage model. I kept it to four pages — readable, not exhaustive. Fourth, **phased implementation**: I introduced practices in three phases over 90 days — phase one was foundational (defect tracking, smoke suite, story kick-offs), phase two was structural (regression suite, API test layer, RTM), phase three was optimisation (CI integration, metrics dashboard, exploratory sessions). Fifth, **metrics from day one**: I tracked escaped defect rate, defect find rate by phase, and test execution coverage from week one so I could show progress over time.

**Result:** Within 90 days, escaped defects to production dropped by 45%. The team went from sceptical to actively requesting QA involvement in planning. The strategy document became the reference point for hiring the next two QA engineers.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Describe the components of a test plan and why structure matters. |
| **Mid** | Define a test approach for a single product area, selecting techniques and tools based on risk. |
| **Senior** | Build an end-to-end QA strategy across the full SDLC, phased for adoption, aligned to business goals, and measured through meaningful KPIs. |

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **QA Strategy** | A high-level document defining the overall approach to quality for a product or organisation. |
| **Risk Landscape** | A map of the areas, features, and processes most likely to produce high-impact defects. |
| **Phased Implementation** | Rolling out QA practices in stages to manage change and build team buy-in. |
| **Escaped Defect Rate** | The number of defects that reach production despite testing — a primary indicator of QA effectiveness. |
| **Definition of Done** | A shared checklist of quality criteria a story must meet before it is considered complete. |
| **Test Maturity Model** | A framework (e.g., TMMi) for assessing and improving the sophistication of a QA function over time. |
