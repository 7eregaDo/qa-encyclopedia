---

## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing crisis competence and learning orientation. How you respond to failure reveals more about your engineering maturity than how you perform under normal conditions. They want to see that you contribute actively during an incident, conduct honest retrospective analysis without blame, and drive durable improvements — not just patch the immediate gap.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** A P1 production incident occurred on a Friday afternoon: a data migration script had silently corrupted pricing data for approximately 8% of the product catalogue, causing customers to be overcharged. The incident was live for 2 hours before detection.

**Task:** I needed to support the incident response immediately, then lead the QA post-mortem to understand how this escaped all testing and prevent recurrence.

**Action:** During the incident, my immediate contribution was **test support for the hotfix**: as the developer wrote the fix, I designed targeted test cases in real time — covering the corruption scenario, adjacent data fields, and rollback behaviour — so the hotfix could be verified rapidly and confidently before deployment. I also ran a quick data integrity check script against staging to confirm the fix resolved all affected records, not just the reported ones. After the incident was resolved, I led the **QA post-mortem** (separate from but feeding into the broader incident review). I asked three questions: What did we test? What did we not test? Why? The findings were stark — the migration script had no automated tests because it was classified as a "one-off ops task" and fell outside the standard development workflow. There was no policy requiring QA sign-off on data migration scripts. Third, I drove **systemic improvements**: I worked with the engineering manager to extend the definition of done to include data migration scripts, created a migration testing checklist (forward run, rollback, data integrity checks, volume testing), and added a pipeline step requiring all migration scripts to be reviewed by QA before execution in any environment above dev.

**Result:** The immediate fix was deployed in 90 minutes with confidence. The post-mortem produced five concrete process improvements. In the following 18 months, zero migration-related incidents occurred. The migration testing checklist was adopted by two other teams.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Describe how you would report and retest a production defect. |
| **Mid** | Explain post-incident analysis from a QA perspective — identifying coverage gaps and immediate test improvements. |
| **Senior** | Lead incident response testing support, conduct a blameless QA post-mortem, drive systemic process improvements, and measure their effectiveness over time. |

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **P1 Incident** | A production incident of the highest severity — typically causing significant customer impact or data loss. |
| **Blameless Post-Mortem** | A retrospective focused on systemic causes of failure, not individual blame. |
| **Hotfix** | An emergency fix deployed directly to production to resolve a critical incident. |
| **Escaped Defect** | A defect that passed through all testing stages and reached production. |
| **Coverage Gap** | An area of the system that had no test coverage at the time a defect was introduced. |
| **Definition of Done** | A shared checklist of quality criteria, extended here to cover non-standard artefacts like migration scripts. |
| **Data Integrity Testing** | Verification that data is accurate, complete, and uncorrupted after a transformation or migration. |
