## The Intent

The interviewer is testing executive-level communication and business acumen. Senior QA engineers must be able to justify their team's existence and investment in terms a CFO, CPO, or CEO can act on. Answering in purely technical terms (test coverage percentages, defect counts) is a failure at this level.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** During a company-wide cost review, the QA team's headcount was questioned. The CTO asked me to present the business case for maintaining and growing the function at the next leadership meeting.

**Task:** I needed to translate QA activity into financial and business impact language — not testing metrics.

**Action:** I built a three-part case. First, **cost of defects by phase**: using six months of defect data, I calculated the average resolution cost at each phase — a requirements-phase defect cost approximately £50 in a 30-minute review; the same defect caught in system testing cost £400 in developer time, retest, and delay; the same defect in production cost an average of £2,800 in support tickets, hotfix deployment, and customer communication. With 340 defects caught pre-production over the period, I estimated £680,000 in avoided production costs. Second, **production incident reduction**: I showed a 60% reduction in P1 production incidents over 12 months since formalising QA practices, correlated with a reduction in customer escalations and a measurable improvement in NPS. Third, **investment vs. return**: the QA team's fully-loaded annual cost was £280,000. The avoided production cost estimate alone was more than double that. I presented this as a conservative floor, noting that reputational and churn costs were not included.

**Result:** Headcount was not only maintained but approved for one additional hire. The presentation was shared by the CTO as a model for how engineering sub-functions should justify investment.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Cost of Quality (CoQ)** | The total cost of ensuring quality (prevention + appraisal) plus the cost of failures (internal + external). |
| **Prevention Costs** | Investment in activities that prevent defects — training, process design, test automation. |
| **Appraisal Costs** | Costs of inspecting and testing to find defects — QA salaries, tools, environments. |
| **Internal Failure Costs** | Cost of defects found before production — rework, retest, delays. |
| **External Failure Costs** | Cost of defects found in production — support, hotfixes, customer churn, reputation damage. |
| **Escaped Defect Rate** | Defects reaching production — the primary metric linking QA investment to business risk. |
| **NPS (Net Promoter Score)** | A customer satisfaction metric that correlates with product quality perception. |
