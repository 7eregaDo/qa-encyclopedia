## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing executive communication and financial acumen. Lead-level automation professionals must be able to secure significant investment from boards and C-suites who do not understand test suites but do understand revenue, cost, and risk. They want to see a structured financial case with conservative assumptions, clear ROI modelling, and a risk-of-inaction argument — not a technical justification dressed in financial language.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** I needed to secure a £600,000 investment over two years for an automation infrastructure platform — a central test results dashboard, cloud browser farm, performance testing tooling, and a shared automation library. The ask required board approval. The CFO's initial response when the CTO mentioned it informally was: "we already have testing, why do we need to spend more on it?"

**Task:** I needed to build a business case that answered the CFO's implicit question — what is wrong with what we have, what does the investment fix, and why is the return worth the cost — in a language that was immediately credible to a financial audience.

**Action:** I built the case around four financial arguments. First, **the cost of the current state**: I calculated the fully-loaded annual cost of manual and inefficient automated testing across the organisation. QA engineer time spent on manual regression that could be automated: 8 FTE-months per year at £8,000/month = £768,000. Production incidents attributable to inadequate test coverage: 22 incidents in the past year, averaging £14,000 in engineering time and customer impact each = £308,000. Total current cost of inadequate automation: £1,076,000 annually. Second, **the projected return**: based on the coverage targets the investment would enable, I projected a 60% reduction in manual regression time (saving £461,000/year) and a 45% reduction in production incidents (saving £138,000/year). Total annual saving: £599,000. Against a £300,000/year investment, the net annual return after break-even was £299,000 — with break-even at 12 months. Third, **the competitive argument**: I added a velocity component — the current manual regression cycle was adding 3 days to every release cycle. With automation, this would be reduced to 4 hours, enabling weekly releases instead of biweekly. I quantified this in terms of feature delivery rate — the ability to ship customer-requested features twice as frequently was a competitive differentiator the sales team could use. Fourth, **the risk of inaction**: I modelled the trajectory without investment — based on the past two years of data, production incidents were growing at 30% per year as the product complexity increased. Without automation infrastructure, the cost of quality would reach £1.8M within three years.

**Result:** The board approved the full £600,000 investment after a 25-minute presentation. The CFO's only question was about the incident reduction assumption — I had used 45%, and they asked if I could defend it. I showed the comparable companies data and the historical correlation between automation coverage and incident rate in our own product history. They accepted the assumption. The investment was described in the board minutes as "a cost reduction programme with a 12-month payback" — which is exactly the framing I had built for.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Explain to a product owner why a specific automation investment would save testing time. |
| **Mid** | Build a team-level ROI case for automation tooling — cost of current state, projected savings, and break-even timeline. |
| **Senior** | Present an automation investment case to VP-level stakeholders with financial modelling and risk-of-inaction argument. |
| **Lead** | Build and present a board-ready automation infrastructure investment case — fully-loaded current cost analysis, projected ROI with conservative assumptions, competitive velocity argument, risk-of-inaction trajectory, and a 12-month payback model. |

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Fully-Loaded Cost** | The total cost of a current state including direct costs, engineering time, and customer impact — the baseline for ROI calculation. |
| **Break-Even Timeline** | The point at which cumulative savings equal the investment cost — the most compelling single metric for financial audiences. |
| **Risk-of-Inaction** | A projection of costs if the investment is not made — often the most powerful argument for a budget-conscious audience. |
| **Velocity Argument** | The business case framing automation as a delivery speed enabler — connecting testing efficiency to competitive advantage. |
| **Conservative Assumption** | A deliberately cautious projection — builds credibility by not overpromising and surviving challenge. |
| **Board-Ready** | A presentation format and language level appropriate for a board of directors — financial, strategic, and concise. |
