## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing the ability to manage organisational complexity without defaulting to either centralised control (which kills autonomy) or laissez-faire (which produces fragmentation). Lead-level automation professionals must design governance systems that are simultaneously consistent and flexible — and that improve quality through enablement rather than enforcement alone.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** I was responsible for automation quality across 16 squads with dramatically different maturity: four squads had sophisticated, well-maintained automation suites; six were mid-maturity with functional but inconsistent coverage; and six had minimal or no automation. Each squad had chosen its own tooling — Playwright, Cypress, Selenium, and one squad using a custom Python framework. There was no shared reporting, no common standards, and no mechanism to identify where investment was most needed.

**Task:** I needed to design a governance model that raised the quality floor across all squads, created organisational visibility, and invested support where it was most needed — without imposing uniformity that would damage higher-maturity squads.

**Action:** I built the governance model around four pillars. First, **universal minimum standards**: three non-negotiable standards for every squad regardless of maturity or tooling — test results must be published in JUnit XML format to the central dashboard after every CI run, all automation PRs must have at least one peer reviewer who is not the author, and every squad must have a named automation owner responsible for suite health. These standards were tool-agnostic and maturity-agnostic. Second, **outcome-based health scoring**: I built a monthly automation health score per squad using four metrics: coverage delta (are they keeping up with new features?), flake rate (is the suite reliable?), mean time to fix a failing test (are failures being investigated?), and CI integration completeness (is the suite running on every PR or only sometimes?). Scores were published transparently — not as a ranking but as a dashboard showing each squad's trajectory over time. Third, **maturity-tiered support investment**: I allocated my team's support capacity in inverse proportion to squad maturity. High-maturity squads received one monthly office hours session — available for questions, not required. Mid-maturity squads received biweekly pairing sessions with a senior automation engineer from my team. Low-maturity squads received weekly embedded support for three months, with a structured 90-day improvement plan and milestone targets. Fourth, **community-driven standard evolution**: the Automation Guild — senior automation practitioners from each squad — met monthly to propose, debate, and adopt new shared standards. Standards proposed by the guild had higher adoption than those mandated centrally, because the people who would follow them had designed them.

**Result:** At the six-month mark, all 16 squads were publishing results to the central dashboard. The six low-maturity squads had all reached mid-maturity standard — none had failed to improve with the embedded support model. The three highest-performing squads had not been disrupted by the governance model — their higher standards were captured in the guild's evolving best practices, not mandated as requirements for others. Organisation-wide flake rate, visible for the first time, averaged 8% — a starting point for a targeted improvement programme in the following quarter.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Follow team automation standards and raise quality questions during code review. |
| **Mid** | Define automation standards for a team and measure compliance through PR review and suite health metrics. |
| **Senior** | Design a multi-team automation governance model with shared standards, outcome metrics, and a support model for lower-maturity teams. |
| **Lead** | Build an organisational automation governance system — universal minimum standards, outcome-based health scoring, maturity-tiered support investment, and community-driven standard evolution — across 15+ squads with diverse tooling and maturity levels. |

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Automation Health Score** | A composite metric combining coverage, flake rate, fix time, and CI integration — providing a single comparable indicator per squad. |
| **Maturity-Tiered Support** | Allocating coaching and support capacity in inverse proportion to squad maturity — most support where it is most needed. |
| **Tool-Agnostic Standard** | A quality requirement that applies regardless of which automation tool a squad uses — enabling governance across diverse tooling. |
| **Automation Guild** | A cross-squad community of automation practitioners that governs shared standards through consensus rather than mandate. |
| **Transparency Without Ranking** | Publishing health scores as trajectories rather than league tables — encourages improvement without creating competitive anxiety. |
| **Embedded Support** | Direct, sustained coaching provided within a squad's workflow — the most effective intervention for low-maturity teams. |
