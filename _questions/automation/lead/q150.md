## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing strategic foresight and the ability to think about automation in a rapidly changing landscape. This is a question about intellectual humility (acknowledging uncertainty), critical evaluation (not adopting every new tool uncritically), and principled decision-making (anchoring to durable principles rather than specific implementations). Lead-level professionals must demonstrate that their strategy is not brittle to tooling change.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** As AI-assisted testing tools began to emerge and mature — tools claiming to auto-generate test cases, self-heal locators, and detect visual regressions without manual baseline management — engineering leadership at the board level began asking whether the organisation's £600,000 automation infrastructure investment was at risk of becoming obsolete. The CTO asked me to assess the AI testing landscape and present a view on how the strategy should respond.

**Task:** I needed to produce an honest, evidence-based assessment of AI testing tools' current and near-future capabilities, identify where the organisation should invest, and articulate a strategy that remained valuable regardless of how quickly AI tooling matured.

**Action:** I structured the assessment and response across four dimensions. First, **critical evaluation of AI testing tools**: I ran a six-week structured evaluation of four AI-assisted testing tools — evaluating each against the same set of real test scenarios from our product. The results were nuanced: AI test generation was most effective for CRUD-heavy, form-based features (reducing test authoring time by 60–70%) but poor for complex stateful workflows, domain-specific business rule testing, and security-focused scenarios. Self-healing locators worked well for stable, semantic UIs but introduced the masking risks I had already documented. I published an honest internal assessment — naming what worked, what didn't, and where the gap between marketing claims and observed capability existed. Second, **principle-based strategy anchoring**: I reframed the automation strategy around five durable principles that were tool-agnostic and AI-resistant: test at the right layer (pyramid), assert on behaviour not implementation, isolate tests from each other, make failures immediately diagnosable, and measure effectiveness by defect detection not coverage percentage. These principles applied whether the tests were written by humans, generated by AI, or some combination — and they provided a framework for evaluating any new tool. Third, **selective AI adoption**: based on the evaluation, I proposed adopting AI assistance in three specific areas where the evidence supported it — AI-assisted test case suggestion for new features (human review required before automation), AI-powered test result triage (reducing time to diagnose CI failures by surfacing the most likely root cause), and AI-enhanced accessibility scanning (catching a broader range of WCAG issues than rule-based tools alone). Each adoption was treated as a bounded experiment with a defined success metric and a 90-day evaluation. Fourth, **continuous tooling evaluation model**: I established a quarterly tooling review — a half-day session where the Automation Guild assessed new tools against the organisation's evaluation criteria, ran targeted POCs for high-interest tools, and made adoption/watch/reject decisions. This institutionalised continuous evaluation without requiring ad-hoc decisions every time a new tool was announced.

**Result:** The assessment was presented to the board — the CTO described it as "the most intellectually honest assessment of AI testing we've seen, which is exactly what we needed." The three selective AI adoptions were approved as experiments. The principle-based reframing of the strategy — independent of any specific tool — gave the board confidence that the automation investment was not at risk of obsolescence. The quarterly tooling review became a standing engineering activity, ensuring the organisation remained informed without becoming reactive to every new tool announcement.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Stay informed about new automation tools and experiment with AI-assisted features as they become available in existing frameworks. |
| **Mid** | Evaluate a specific AI testing tool against defined criteria and make a team-level adoption recommendation. |
| **Senior** | Design a structured AI tool evaluation process and integrate selective AI adoption into an existing automation strategy. |
| **Lead** | Produce an honest, board-ready assessment of the AI testing landscape, anchor the automation strategy to tool-agnostic principles, design selective AI adoption as bounded experiments, and institutionalise continuous tooling evaluation as an organisational capability. |

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Principle-Based Strategy** | An automation approach anchored to durable, tool-agnostic principles — resilient to tooling landscape change. |
| **AI-Assisted Test Generation** | Using AI models to suggest or generate test cases — effective for some scenarios, poor for complex domain-specific testing. |
| **Bounded Experiment** | A time-limited, scope-limited trial of a new tool or approach with a defined success metric — enables evidence-based adoption decisions. |
| **Continuous Tooling Evaluation** | An institutionalised, periodic process for assessing new tools against defined criteria — prevents reactive adoption or neglect. |
| **Intellectual Honesty** | Acknowledging the limitations of AI tools alongside their capabilities — more credible to technical and non-technical audiences than uncritical adoption or dismissal. |
| **Automation Guild Tooling Review** | A quarterly community session assessing new tools and making adoption recommendations — distributed, expert-driven tooling governance. |
