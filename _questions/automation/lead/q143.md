## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing organisational development thinking and the ability to build capability at scale. Lead-level automation professionals must be able to go beyond building their own team's capability and raise the automation literacy of the whole engineering organisation. This question distinguishes those who can teach from those who can build teaching systems.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** I joined an organisation as Head of Quality Engineering where automation was owned entirely by a small central QA team — six engineers writing all automation for 180 developers across 14 squads. The model was unsustainable: the central team was a bottleneck, developers had no automation skills, and coverage was years behind delivery. The CTO wanted a shift to embedded automation capability — developers and embedded QA engineers writing automation as a standard practice.

**Task:** I needed to design a programme that would build automation capability across the organisation — not just teach a few developers to write tests, but create a self-sustaining culture where automation was a natural part of engineering practice.

**Action:** I designed the programme across five phases over 18 months. First, **foundation — shared infrastructure**: before teaching anyone anything, I ensured the tools were easy to use. I built a starter kit — a pre-configured automation project template with the framework installed, page object structure in place, CI integration configured, and 10 example tests demonstrating common patterns. Any developer could fork the starter kit and write their first test in 30 minutes. Removing setup friction was the prerequisite for learning. Second, **structured learning pathway**: I created a three-level curriculum — Automation Fundamentals (what to automate, how assertions work, basic page object usage — 4 hours, self-paced), Automation Practitioner (data-driven testing, API automation, CI integration — 8 hours, cohort-based with exercises), and Automation Engineer (framework architecture, performance testing, advanced patterns — 16 hours, mentored project). Each level produced a practical deliverable — a working test contribution to the participant's squad's suite. Third, **squad automation champions**: I identified one engineer per squad to become an Automation Champion — a practitioner-level person who was the first point of contact for squad automation questions. Champions received additional mentoring from my team and met monthly as a community. They multiplied the programme's reach without requiring central team involvement in every squad. Fourth, **contribution incentives**: I worked with engineering managers to make automation contribution visible in performance conversations — writing tests, improving coverage, and mentoring others on automation were added to the engineering career framework as explicit competencies at every level. Fifth, **measurement and accountability**: I tracked per-squad automation coverage monthly, published it in the engineering all-hands, and celebrated squads that hit milestones. Coverage was a shared metric — not a QA metric.

**Result:** At the 18-month mark, 67 engineers across 14 squads had completed at least the Fundamentals level. 23 had reached Practitioner. Organisation-wide automation coverage had increased from 31% to 68%. The central QA team's time spent writing automation for other squads had dropped from 80% to 20% of their capacity — the rest was invested in framework development, tooling, and advanced support. Two squads were fully self-sufficient in automation — writing, maintaining, and reviewing their own tests without QA team involvement.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Learn automation skills through structured resources and apply them to your own test contributions. |
| **Mid** | Mentor a junior engineer in automation through pairing and code review. |
| **Senior** | Design an automation curriculum for a team — learning pathway, practical exercises, and skill assessment. |
| **Lead** | Design an organisation-wide automation enablement programme — shared infrastructure, tiered curriculum, squad champions, career framework integration, measurement, and a 18-month roadmap to distributed automation capability. |

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Automation Enablement** | An organisational programme building distributed automation capability — distinct from a central team writing automation for others. |
| **Starter Kit** | A pre-configured automation project template that removes setup friction — the prerequisite for learning. |
| **Automation Champion** | A squad-embedded practitioner who is the first point of contact for automation questions — multiplies programme reach. |
| **Tiered Curriculum** | A learning pathway with multiple levels — each producing a practical deliverable tied to real work. |
| **Distributed Capability** | Automation competence embedded across all squads — the alternative to central team dependency. |
| **Career Framework Integration** | Making automation contribution explicitly visible in career progression criteria — the most effective incentive for sustained adoption. |
