## The Intent
The interviewer is checking whether you can design and sustain governance that works at scale — not just set standards and hope people follow them. At lead level, the challenge is maintaining quality consistency across teams with different priorities, velocities, and technical contexts, without creating a bureaucracy that slows delivery.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** After successfully rolling out SLOs across nine of twelve product teams, I observed a pattern: SLOs were being set but not reviewed. Three teams had been in breach for multiple sprints with no remediation plan. Two teams had set thresholds so conservative they were permanently green but meaningless. The governance mechanism was missing.

**Task:** I needed to design a lightweight governance process that created accountability without becoming a compliance burden — and that respected product team autonomy.

**Action:** I introduced three governance components. First, a monthly SLO review forum: a 45-minute cross-team session where any team currently in breach presented their situation and remediation plan. Attendance was required from one engineering lead per team. I chaired the session but did not own the fixes — that remained with the product team. The social accountability of presenting a breach to peers proved more effective than any escalation process. Second, SLO calibration criteria: I published a standard that flagged SLOs as "trivially green" if they had not been breached once in the past quarter — teams were required to tighten them. This addressed the sandbagging problem. Third, an escalation path: if a team remained in breach for more than six weeks without an approved remediation plan, I would raise it with the VP of Engineering as a risk item — not as a blame mechanism, but as a signal that the team needed resourcing support. I made the escalation criteria public so there were no surprises. I also introduced an SLO exemption process: teams could formally request a temporary relaxation with a documented reason and a time-bounded expiry — removing the incentive to set weak SLOs to avoid breach.

**Result:** Within two months, all three persistently breaching teams had active remediation plans. One team used the SLO review forum to surface a shared infrastructure dependency causing their breach — which the platform team resolved, fixing the SLO for two teams simultaneously. The sandbagging rate dropped: six SLOs were tightened following the calibration criteria review. The governance model was adopted by the wider engineering quality programme as a template for other non-functional standards.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|-------|
| **Entry** | Understands what SLOs are and why teams should be accountable for them |
| **Mid** | Implements SLOs within their team and tracks compliance over time |
| **Senior** | Drives SLO adoption across adjacent teams and escalates persistent breaches through engineering leadership |
| **Lead** | Designs the governance model — review cadence, calibration criteria, escalation paths, exemption process — that sustains SLO quality across the organisation at scale |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **SLO governance** | The processes and accountability structures that ensure SLOs are set meaningfully, reviewed regularly, and acted upon when breached |
| **SLO review forum** | A regular cross-team meeting where SLO compliance is reviewed, breaches are presented, and remediation plans are agreed |
| **Sandbagging** | The practice of setting intentionally weak SLO thresholds to avoid breach — a governance failure that makes SLOs meaningless |
| **Calibration criteria** | Standards that define what a meaningful SLO looks like — used to identify and challenge thresholds that are set too conservatively |
| **Escalation path** | A defined process for raising a persistent breach to a higher level of authority — used as a last resort when peer accountability has not produced remediation |
| **SLO exemption** | A formally approved, time-bounded relaxation of an SLO threshold — providing a legitimate alternative to sandbagging under genuine constraints |
| **Peer accountability** | The social mechanism by which engineers are motivated to address problems by the expectation of presenting their status to colleagues — often more effective than top-down enforcement |
