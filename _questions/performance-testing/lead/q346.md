## The Intent
The interviewer is checking whether you have operated at the intersection of engineering and product, not just within engineering. At lead level, the most impactful performance work prevents bad architectural decisions before they are built — and that requires a seat at the product strategy table.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** Our product team was planning a new real-time analytics feature for enterprise customers — a dashboard refreshing every 5 seconds across up to 500 concurrent enterprise users. The feature was on the roadmap for Q3. I was not in the product planning meetings and the engineering team had not yet been consulted on feasibility.

**Task:** I identified the performance risk proactively and sought to influence the roadmap before the feature entered development.

**Action:** I modelled the load implications before the feature was specced: 500 users × 1 request per 5 seconds = 100 req/s sustained, every request hitting the analytics database. I ran a quick exploratory load test at 80 req/s against the analytics service (below the projected load) and found database CPU at 88% — already close to saturation. At 100 req/s the service would fail. I prepared a two-page brief for the product director, framed not as a blocker but as a design choice: "As currently architectured, the 5-second refresh for 500 users is not deliverable without a database redesign estimated at 8–12 weeks. Two alternatives: a 30-second refresh is deliverable within the current architecture and would cover 90% of the stated user need; or a push-based architecture (websockets + pre-computed aggregates) would deliver the 5-second experience at a 6-week engineering investment." I presented this before the feature entered the sprint backlog.

**Result:** The product director chose the 30-second refresh for the initial release with a commitment to revisit the push architecture in Q4. The feature shipped on schedule. Without early engagement, the team would have committed to a 5-second refresh, discovered the constraint mid-sprint, and either delayed the release or shipped a degraded experience. The product director subsequently invited me to all major feature planning sessions as a standing attendee.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Understands that performance constraints can affect what features are feasible |
| **Mid** | Flags performance risks during feature design and proposes alternative approaches |
| **Senior** | Proactively models performance implications of planned features and presents findings to engineering leads before development begins |
| **Lead** | Earns a standing role in product planning, translates performance constraints into product choices with business framing, and shapes roadmap decisions as a strategic partner |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Proactive performance engagement** | Assessing the performance implications of planned features before development begins, rather than testing what has already been built |
| **Feasibility brief** | A concise document presenting the performance constraints of a proposed feature alongside alternative design options with cost and impact estimates |
| **Push-based architecture** | A pattern where the server proactively sends data to clients when it changes, rather than clients polling on a fixed interval — more efficient for real-time features at scale |
| **Pre-computed aggregates** | Summary data calculated and stored in advance (e.g., on a schedule or event trigger) to avoid expensive real-time computation per request |
| **Design choice framing** | Presenting a performance constraint not as a blocker but as a decision between alternatives — each with different user experience and engineering cost trade-offs |
| **Standing attendee** | A consistent presence in a recurring meeting — in this context, performance engineering gaining influence in product planning as a trusted strategic voice |
| **Roadmap influence** | The ability to shape which features are built, in what form, and in what sequence — the highest-leverage form of performance engineering impact |
