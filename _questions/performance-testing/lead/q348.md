## The Intent
The interviewer is checking whether you can make and justify significant investment decisions, manage vendor relationships, and govern tooling adoption across teams with different contexts and opinions. At lead level, the wrong tooling decision costs money, time, and political capital — the right one multiplies team capability.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** Our organisation had five different load testing tools across twelve teams — JMeter, Gatling, k6, Locust, and one commercial tool whose licence had not been renewed. The fragmentation meant no shared scripts, no common knowledge base, no CI integration standard, and significant onboarding overhead each time an engineer moved teams.

**Task:** I was asked to evaluate the tooling landscape and recommend a standardisation strategy, with a budget ceiling of £40,000 per year for commercial tooling.

**Action:** I ran a structured evaluation over six weeks. I defined four evaluation dimensions: technical capability (protocol support, CI integration, scripting model), total cost of ownership (licence cost + training + maintenance time), team adoption friction (learning curve, alignment with existing language skills — most teams used JavaScript or Python), and ecosystem integration (Grafana, Prometheus, GitHub Actions). I convened a tooling working group with three senior engineers representing different team contexts, to prevent the decision being perceived as top-down. We evaluated k6 (free, JavaScript, excellent CI integration, Grafana native), Gatling Enterprise (commercial, strong reporting, Java/Scala barrier), and BlazeMeter (cloud-based, high cost, strong at scale but over-specified for most teams). We ran a proof-of-concept with each tool against a shared reference scenario and scored them on the dimensions. k6 scored highest overall, with BlazeMeter reserved as an optional add-on for large-scale distributed tests requiring cloud execution. I presented the recommendation with a two-year roadmap: months 1–3, migrate CI integration to k6 across all teams; months 4–9, consolidate existing scripts; months 10–24, deprecate legacy tooling. Migration was supported with a shared script library I would build and maintain. Teams retaining JMeter for specific legacy reasons could do so with a documented exception.

**Result:** Ten of twelve teams adopted k6 within nine months. The shared script library accumulated 34 reusable components in the first year. Onboarding time for a new engineer to run their first performance test dropped from an estimated 2–3 days to under 3 hours. The two exception teams retained JMeter with a documented and time-bounded rationale.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Can compare performance tools on basic criteria and explain why a given tool was chosen |
| **Mid** | Evaluates tooling options against technical requirements and recommends a choice with rationale |
| **Senior** | Leads a structured tooling evaluation across multiple options, runs proofs-of-concept, and manages team adoption |
| **Lead** | Designs the organisational tooling strategy — evaluation framework, vendor governance, adoption roadmap, exception process, and shared asset management — across all teams |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Total cost of ownership (TCO)** | The full cost of a tooling decision over time — including licence fees, training investment, maintenance effort, and integration development |
| **Tooling fragmentation** | A state where multiple teams use different tools for the same purpose, creating knowledge silos, incompatible artefacts, and duplication of effort |
| **Proof of concept (PoC)** | A time-boxed evaluation of a tool against a real scenario to validate that it meets requirements before a full commitment |
| **Working group** | A small, cross-team group convened to provide input on a decision — improving quality and building buy-in simultaneously |
| **Shared script library** | A version-controlled repository of reusable performance test components — parameterised journey scripts, utility functions, data generators — maintained centrally and available to all teams |
| **Tooling exception** | A formally documented permission for a team to use a non-standard tool, with a stated rationale and review date — preventing shadow tooling while acknowledging legitimate edge cases |
| **Adoption roadmap** | A phased plan for migrating teams to a new tooling standard — sequencing migrations to minimise disruption and providing support at each stage |
