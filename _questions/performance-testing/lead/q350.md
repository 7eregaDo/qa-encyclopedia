## The Intent
The interviewer is checking whether you build for permanence or for personal indispensability. At lead level, the question is not whether you are good at performance engineering — it is whether the organisation will still be good at it in three years when you may not be there. This is the distinction between a practitioner and a discipline builder.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** After four years building the performance engineering practice at my organisation, I was offered a role at a different company. The function I had built had significant single-person dependencies — I was the primary author of the tooling standards, the chair of the SLO review forum, the person engineers called when they had a difficult performance problem, and the only person with the full context of the capacity model.

**Task:** Before leaving, I had a responsibility to ensure the practice was self-sustaining. I had eight weeks. I treated it as a structured knowledge transfer and capability-building exercise, not just documentation.

**Action:** I identified four categories of dependency and addressed each. Knowledge dependencies: I ran a documentation sprint — producing an architecture decision record for every non-obvious tooling choice, a written capacity modelling guide with worked examples, and an annotated library of the twenty most complex k6 scripts with inline explanations. I published all of it in the engineering wiki under a dedicated performance engineering section. People dependencies: I identified two senior engineers who had the most performance context — one in the platform team, one in the largest product team. I worked with them for six weeks in a structured shadowing arrangement: they led every engagement, I advised. Both were formally recognised as performance engineering leads before I left, with their managers' support. Process dependencies: I documented the SLO review forum format, the incident response playbook, and the quarterly capacity review template so that any engineer could chair them. I ran the last two forums with one of the new leads chairing and me attending as a participant. Tooling dependencies: I ensured the k6 shared script library had a CONTRIBUTING guide, a designated maintainer (one of the new leads), and a deprecation policy so it would not stagnate.

**Result:** Eighteen months after my departure, I spoke to the VP of Engineering at a conference. She told me the SLO review forum was still running monthly, the script library had grown from 34 to 61 components, and the team had handled two major performance incidents without external support. The practice had not only survived — it had grown. She cited the documentation sprint as the single highest-value transition activity.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Understands the value of documentation and knowledge sharing |
| **Mid** | Documents their own work thoroughly and onboards colleagues proactively |
| **Senior** | Builds team capability through coaching and structured knowledge sharing, reducing single-person dependency within their domain |
| **Lead** | Designs the full knowledge transfer and capability-building programme that makes the discipline self-sustaining — treating legacy as a deliberate engineering output, not an afterthought |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Knowledge dependency** | A situation where a critical capability or decision-making context resides with a single individual — a fragility risk for the organisation |
| **Architecture decision record (ADR)** | A document capturing a significant technical decision, the context in which it was made, the options considered, and the rationale for the choice |
| **Shadowing arrangement** | A structured knowledge transfer method where a successor leads engagements while the outgoing expert advises, building confidence and context simultaneously |
| **CONTRIBUTING guide** | A document in a shared repository explaining how to add, modify, or deprecate assets — enabling others to maintain it without needing to ask the original author |
| **Designated maintainer** | A named individual with explicit responsibility for the ongoing health of a shared asset — preventing the tragedy of the commons in open team resources |
| **Deprecation policy** | A documented process for removing outdated assets from a shared library — ensuring the library remains trustworthy and current over time |
| **Discipline legacy** | The lasting organisational capability that remains after an individual contributor leaves — the highest-order measure of a lead's impact |
