## The Intent
The interviewer is checking whether you can operate as a discipline leader, not just a practitioner. At lead level, the question is not "what did you test?" but "how did you define what performance means for the organisation, and how did you get everyone building toward it?"

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** I was the most senior QA engineer at a 600-person technology company with 12 product teams. Each team had its own definition of "fast enough," its own tooling choices, and its own testing cadence — or none. Customers were filing performance complaints against different products inconsistently, and there was no organisation-level picture of performance health.

**Task:** I was asked by the VP of Engineering to define a performance engineering vision and present it to engineering leadership, with a 12-month adoption roadmap.

**Action:** I structured the vision around three commitments. First, a shared performance standard: I proposed that every customer-facing product define and publish SLOs for p95 response time, availability, and error rate, with a central dashboard showing compliance across all products. I convened a working group of senior engineers from four product teams to validate that the proposed thresholds were achievable and meaningful — I did not set them unilaterally. Second, a common tooling baseline: I recommended k6 for load testing (already used by three teams), Grafana and Prometheus for observability (already partially deployed), and Lighthouse CI for front-end performance — creating a supported, documented standard rather than a mandate. Teams using different tools were not required to migrate immediately; the standard applied to new work. Third, a performance engineering community of practice: monthly cross-team sessions where teams shared findings, tooling improvements, and lessons from incidents. I positioned performance as a shared engineering craft, not a QA function. I presented the vision as a 3-page narrative — problem, principles, roadmap — not a slide deck of metrics.

**Result:** The VP approved the vision and allocated two quarters of platform engineering time to build the central SLO dashboard. Within eight months, nine of twelve product teams had published SLOs. The community of practice surfaced a shared caching pattern that three teams independently needed — implemented once and shared. Two teams avoided separate tooling purchases by adopting the standard. The VP cited the initiative in the annual engineering review as a model for cross-team quality alignment.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Understands what a performance standard is and why consistency across teams matters |
| **Mid** | Implements performance standards within their team and advocates for adoption in adjacent teams |
| **Senior** | Designs the technical components of a performance programme — tooling, gates, observability — and drives adoption across a small number of teams |
| **Lead** | Defines the organisation-wide performance vision, builds the governance structures and community that sustain it, and connects it to business strategy and executive accountability |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Performance vision** | A narrative articulation of the performance quality outcomes an organisation commits to, the principles guiding investment, and the roadmap for achieving them |
| **SLO (Service Level Objective)** | An internal performance target for a specific metric — published per product as part of a shared performance standard |
| **Community of practice** | A voluntary cross-team group that meets regularly to share knowledge, solve shared problems, and raise collective capability in a discipline |
| **Tooling standard** | An organisation-level recommendation for a common set of tools — reducing fragmentation, enabling shared expertise, and lowering support cost |
| **Central SLO dashboard** | An aggregated view of SLO compliance across all products, giving leadership a portfolio-level performance health signal |
| **Governance** | The structures (standards, reviews, escalation paths) that sustain a programme after the initial vision has been set |
| **Engineering narrative** | A prose-format document communicating strategy and rationale — more effective than metric-heavy slide decks for building leadership alignment |
