## The Intent
The interviewer is checking whether you can drive the conversation about what "good enough" looks like, rather than waiting for someone to hand you a number. At mid level, you are expected to facilitate this negotiation across engineering, product, and business stakeholders.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** I joined a project where performance tests existed but had no defined pass/fail criteria. Results were reported as raw numbers and the go/no-go decision was made subjectively in a meeting.

**Task:** I was asked to establish formal performance thresholds that would make the decision objective and auditable.

**Action:** I gathered data from four sources. First, user research: the UX team had data showing that 53% of mobile users abandon a page that takes longer than 3 seconds to load — this gave me the user experience ceiling. Second, the existing baseline: our p95 response time was 1.6 s under normal load — this was the current state. Third, industry benchmark: Google's Core Web Vitals recommended under 2.5 s for Largest Contentful Paint. Fourth, business SLA: our enterprise contracts specified 99.9% availability and "response times comparable to industry standard." I proposed: p95 under 2 s (achievable, better than industry benchmark), error rate under 0.5%, availability above 99.9% measured over a 30-day rolling window. I presented these to the product manager and engineering lead, who approved them.

**Result:** All subsequent performance tests had formal pass/fail criteria. The first test under the new thresholds failed the p95 target (2.4 s) — which had previously been considered acceptable — triggering a targeted optimisation that reduced it to 1.7 s.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Understands what a threshold is and why it must be agreed before testing begins |
| **Mid** | Facilitates the threshold-setting process, drawing on user research, baselines, benchmarks, and business commitments to produce defensible targets |
| **Senior** | Embeds SLA definition into the feature inception process, connects performance budgets to product KPIs, and reviews thresholds as the system evolves |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **SLA (Service Level Agreement)** | A formal commitment — internal or contractual — specifying the performance level a system must maintain |
| **SLO (Service Level Objective)** | An internal target, typically more stringent than the SLA, used operationally to provide headroom before an SLA breach |
| **SLI (Service Level Indicator)** | The specific metric used to measure performance against an SLO (e.g., p95 response time, error rate, availability) |
| **Core Web Vitals** | Google's set of user-experience metrics — LCP, CLS, and INP — used as industry benchmarks for web page performance |
| **Performance budget** | A set of maximum thresholds for performance metrics that a product team commits to maintaining across releases |
| **Availability** | The percentage of time a system is operational and accessible, typically expressed as a monthly or annual figure |
| **Error budget** | The allowable amount of downtime or degraded performance within an SLO period — when exhausted, releases are paused until the system recovers |
