## The Intent
The interviewer is checking whether you can drive cultural change, not just technical improvement. At senior level, the most scalable performance improvement is a team that thinks about performance as a default — and creating that requires influence, not authority.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** I joined a team where performance was universally acknowledged as important but consistently deprioritised. Engineers said "we'll optimise it later" — and later never came. The team was talented, well-intentioned, and genuinely stretched on feature delivery.

**Task:** I needed to shift performance from a theoretical priority to a practical one, without adding friction to an already pressured team.

**Action:** I focused on three levers. First, visibility: I added a performance widget to the existing team dashboard — the one displayed on the office screen — showing p95 response times for the five core user journeys in real time. Engineers started noticing and mentioning it in standup unprompted within two weeks. Second, connection to outcomes: I partnered with the product manager to pull Google Analytics data showing bounce rate correlated with page load time on our key conversion pages. I presented a single slide: "A 1-second improvement in checkout load time is associated with a 7% increase in completed transactions, based on our own data." Engineers who had tuned out "performance matters" responded to "our slowness costs us money." Third, removing friction: I created a performance starter kit — a k6 script template, a Grafana dashboard template, and a one-page test plan template — and ran a 90-minute workshop showing engineers how to run their own performance check in under 10 minutes. I positioned it as a tool for engineers to protect their own work, not a QA gate to pass.

**Result:** Within a quarter, three engineers had voluntarily run performance checks on their own features before raising a PR — without being asked. One caught a regression himself and fixed it in the same branch. The performance widget became a source of team pride rather than anxiety.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Can explain why performance culture matters and describe what a performance-aware team looks like |
| **Mid** | Advocates for performance practices within their immediate team and demonstrates value through concrete findings |
| **Senior** | Drives cultural change through visibility, business outcome framing, and tooling — making performance-aware behaviour the default rather than the exception |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Performance culture** | A team environment in which performance is treated as a shared engineering responsibility, considered at design, development, and review stages rather than delegated to a specialist |
| **Visibility** | Making performance data continuously accessible and prominent — a precondition for it being acted upon |
| **Business outcome framing** | Connecting a technical metric (response time) to a commercial result (conversion rate, revenue) to make the case for investment in non-functional quality |
| **Path of least resistance** | Designing systems and tooling so that the correct behaviour (e.g., running a performance check) is easier than the incorrect one (skipping it) |
| **Performance starter kit** | A pre-built, low-friction set of tools and templates enabling engineers to run their own performance checks without specialist knowledge |
| **Shift-left** | Moving quality activities earlier in the development cycle — in performance, getting engineers to test their own code rather than waiting for a QA gate |
| **Psychological safety** | An environment where engineers feel safe to surface performance issues they have introduced without fear of blame — essential for early disclosure and fast remediation |
