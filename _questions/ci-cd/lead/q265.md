## The Intent
The interviewer is testing your ability to create durable behavioural change — one of the most important and hardest lead-level skills. Technical pipeline improvements degrade without cultural ownership. They want to see how you've shifted the norm from "the platform team's problem" to "every engineer's responsibility."

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** After a major pipeline modernisation, the new pipeline was fast, reliable, and well-instrumented — but within three months, flakiness was creeping back, pipeline durations were increasing, and teams were re-running failures without investigating them. The improvement was eroding because pipeline health had no cultural owner at squad level.

**Task:** Build a culture where squad engineers treated their pipeline health with the same care they gave to code quality and production reliability.

**Action:** I worked across four levers simultaneously, having learned from cultural change work in earlier modules. Make it theirs: I moved pipeline health metrics — pass rate, median duration, flaky test count — into each squad's existing engineering dashboard alongside their deployment frequency and incident metrics. Pipeline health was no longer a platform team concern; it was a squad metric reviewed in their own sprint ceremonies. Treat pipeline failures as code failures: I changed the team norm so that a flaky test was raised as a defect ticket in the squad's backlog with a severity, not added to an informal "re-run and ignore" list. I worked with engineering managers to include pipeline health in the same conversation as code review standards. Remove friction from improvement: I made fixing a flaky test as easy as possible — a documented playbook, a `make fix-flaky TEST=<name>` command that ran the test in isolation, and a fast path to merge the fix without full PR review. When improvement is easy, it happens. Celebrate it: I introduced a monthly "pipeline champion" — an engineer who had fixed a meaningful flaky test or pipeline bottleneck — announced in the engineering all-hands with a brief technical summary of what they fixed and why it mattered. Engineers who might not get recognition for feature delivery got it for quality work instead.

**Result:** Within two quarters, squads were raising flaky test tickets without being prompted. The average time a test spent in the flaky quarantine list dropped from 47 days to 9 days. Three squads proactively optimised their pipeline duration without any request from the platform team. The "pipeline champion" recognition was cited in four performance reviews as evidence of quality ownership.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Re-runs failed tests without investigating; does not see pipeline health as their responsibility |
| **Mid** | Investigates failures before re-running; raises flaky tests as defects; advocates for pipeline health within the squad |
| **Senior** | Models pipeline ownership publicly; runs flakiness investigations; contributes pipeline health data to retrospectives |
| **Lead** | Designs the systemic levers — visibility, defect classification, friction reduction, and recognition — that shift pipeline ownership from platform team to every squad, and measures cultural shift through quarantine age and unsolicited squad behaviour |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Pipeline Ownership Culture** | The organisational norm in which engineering squads treat their pipeline health — speed, reliability, and quality gate integrity — as their own responsibility rather than a platform team concern |
| **Squad Engineering Dashboard** | A squad-level view combining delivery metrics (deployment frequency, incident rate) with pipeline health metrics (pass rate, flakiness count), making pipeline health part of the squad's performance picture |
| **Flaky Test Defect** | The classification of a flaky test as a formal engineering defect with severity and ownership, rather than an informal annoyance to be worked around |
| **Friction Reduction** | The removal of practical obstacles to making quality improvements — documented playbooks, one-command fix tooling, and expedited review paths — so that doing the right thing requires minimal effort |
| **Pipeline Champion** | A recognition programme that publicly acknowledges engineers who improve pipeline health, creating cultural incentives for quality ownership work |
| **Quarantine Age** | The average time a flaky test spends in the non-blocking quarantine suite before being fixed or removed, used as a metric for how seriously the team treats pipeline quality debt |
| **Unsolicited Squad Behaviour** | Spontaneous pipeline improvement actions taken by squads without platform team prompting — the primary evidence that ownership culture has taken root |
| **Norm Shift** | A change in the default behaviour of a group — from re-running failures to investigating them — achieved through consistent reinforcement of the new expectation over time |
