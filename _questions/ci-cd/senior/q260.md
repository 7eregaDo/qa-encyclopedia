## The Intent
The interviewer is testing whether you can connect CI/CD quality work to business-relevant engineering performance metrics. DORA metrics are the industry standard for this — but the value is in using them to drive decisions, not just measuring them. They want to see that you've used them analytically, not just cited them.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** I was presenting a proposal for a significant pipeline infrastructure investment to the VP Engineering. The team had qualitative dissatisfaction with the pipeline but I needed a data-based argument for the investment and a way to measure its impact afterwards.

**Task:** Establish DORA metric baselines, use them to identify the highest-impact improvement areas, and define the expected post-investment metric targets.

**Action:** I measured the four DORA metrics over the preceding 90 days. Deployment frequency: we were deploying to production 2.3 times per week across 12 services — well below the "high performer" threshold of multiple times per day. Root cause: the 45-minute pipeline was a deterrent to frequent merging. Lead time for changes: median time from commit to production was 4.2 days. Root causes: long pipeline, manual release gate requiring scheduling, and a staging freeze period. Change failure rate: 12% of deployments required a hotfix or rollback — significantly above the high-performer threshold of under 5%. Root cause: insufficient automated testing coverage meant defects reached production. Mean time to restore: when incidents occurred, average restoration time was 67 minutes. Root cause: no automated rollback, no synthetic monitoring for rapid detection. From this analysis, I identified two highest-leverage investments: reducing pipeline duration (addresses deployment frequency and lead time) and introducing canary deployments with automated rollback (addresses change failure rate and MTTR). I presented target metrics for six months post-investment: deployment frequency to 3+ per week, lead time to under 24 hours, change failure rate to under 5%, and MTTR to under 20 minutes. I also defined how each metric would be measured — GitHub deployment events, commit timestamps, incident log, and PagerDuty alert-to-resolve times.

**Result:** The investment was approved. Six months later: deployment frequency reached 4.1 per week; lead time dropped to 18 hours; change failure rate fell to 4.3%; MTTR dropped to 19 minutes. The DORA framework gave the VP Engineering a clear before/after story that was used in the engineering organisation's annual review presentation.

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **DORA Metrics** | Four evidence-based software delivery performance metrics — deployment frequency, lead time for changes, change failure rate, and mean time to restore — validated by the DevOps Research and Assessment programme |
| **Deployment Frequency** | How often an organisation successfully releases to production, ranging from less than once per month (low performer) to multiple times per day (elite performer) |
| **Lead Time for Changes** | The time elapsed from a developer committing code to that code running in production, a measure of delivery pipeline efficiency |
| **Change Failure Rate** | The percentage of deployments that result in a degradation requiring remediation — a hotfix, rollback, or patch — indicating the quality of changes reaching production |
| **Mean Time to Restore (MTTR)** | The average time to recover from a production failure, from initial detection to full service restoration |
| **Elite Performer** | DORA's highest performance category, characterised by deployment frequency of multiple times per day, lead time under one hour, change failure rate under 5%, and MTTR under one hour |
| **Metric Decomposition** | The process of tracing a DORA metric to its contributing root causes — for example, decomposing high lead time into pipeline duration, manual gate scheduling, and staging freeze time |
| **DevOps Research and Assessment (DORA)** | A research programme now part of Google Cloud that produced the four-metric framework through large-scale empirical studies of software delivery performance |
