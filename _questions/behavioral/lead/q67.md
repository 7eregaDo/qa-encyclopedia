## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing organisational design thinking and the ability to hold structural tension productively. Lead-level QA professionals must be able to design systems that give teams freedom without creating chaos — and articulate the principle that underlies that design clearly. This is a question about philosophy as much as practice.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** I was leading QA across six product squads, each with their own embedded QA engineer. The squads were using four different test frameworks, three different defect tracking workflows, and two completely different approaches to defining "done" from a quality perspective. Cross-squad work was difficult — engineers couldn't read each other's test code, quality metrics were incomparable, and onboarding between squads took weeks. At the same time, when I had proposed standardisation previously, squad leads had pushed back hard, citing autonomy as essential to their velocity.

**Task:** I needed to introduce enough standardisation to enable cross-squad visibility and collaboration, without creating a centralised process that squads would route around or resent.

**Action:** I started by separating what needed to be consistent from what should be free. I called this the **thin standards layer** — a small set of non-negotiables that served shared organisational goals, with everything else explicitly left to squad discretion. To define the thin layer, I asked one question for each potential standard: "Does inconsistency here create a problem for the organisation that individual squads cannot solve themselves?" If yes, it belonged in the standard. If no, it belonged to the squad. The thin layer I proposed had three elements: a common quality metrics format (so escaped defect rates and coverage data were comparable across squads), a shared definition of done template (squads could add to it, but had to include the baseline), and a common test result reporting schema for CI pipelines (so a central quality dashboard was possible without dictating how tests were written). Everything else — framework choice, test design approach, tooling preferences — remained with the squad. I introduced the thin layer in a working group with all six squad QA engineers, not as a mandate from above but as a collaborative design session. Their input shaped the final form of each standard.

**Result:** All six squads adopted the thin layer within one sprint — because it had been designed with them, not imposed on them. For the first time, I could produce a cross-squad quality dashboard that the CTO could read in a single view. Squad velocity was unaffected — in the retrospectives following adoption, no squad cited the standards as a burden. Two squads voluntarily adopted each other's framework tooling after seeing it in the shared dashboard, which would never have happened without the visibility the standards created.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Follow team standards while flagging when they feel like overhead. |
| **Mid** | Introduce a team-level standard through a working agreement process, handling resistance constructively. |
| **Senior** | Design a test strategy that balances consistency and flexibility across a multi-team product. |
| **Lead** | Design an organisational thin standards layer that enables cross-team visibility and collaboration while explicitly protecting squad autonomy — defined collaboratively, not mandated. |

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Thin Standards Layer** | A minimal set of non-negotiable organisational standards that serve shared goals, with everything else left to team discretion. |
| **Standardise Outcomes, Not Methods** | A principle allowing teams to choose how they achieve a goal while requiring consistency in what they report and how they interface. |
| **Squad Autonomy** | The freedom of a product squad to choose its own tools, practices, and approaches within organisational boundaries. |
| **Collaborative Standard Design** | Involving the people who will follow a standard in designing it — the most effective way to achieve adoption without resistance. |
| **Cross-Squad Visibility** | The ability to compare quality signals and share knowledge across teams — enabled by consistent metrics and reporting formats. |
| **Organisational Fragmentation** | The state where too much autonomy has created incompatible practices, incomparable metrics, and knowledge silos. |
