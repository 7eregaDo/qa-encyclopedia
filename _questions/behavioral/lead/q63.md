## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing political and strategic acumen at the executive level. Lead QA professionals must be able to navigate C-suite dynamics, position quality as a strategic asset rather than an operational function, and maintain that positioning through business cycles when quality investment is under pressure. This question distinguishes QA leaders who have operated at board-adjacent levels from those who have not.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** I had built a strong quality programme at a growth-stage company, but when a new CFO joined with a cost reduction mandate, quality investment was immediately flagged as a target. The new CFO had no prior exposure to quality as a strategic function and viewed it as overhead. Within my first meeting with them, they questioned whether the QA team could be reduced by half.

**Task:** I needed to build executive sponsorship with a new C-level stakeholder who was actively sceptical, while maintaining the existing sponsorship I had with the CTO — and doing both without becoming politically exposed.

**Action:** I approached the CFO relationship with a single governing principle: speak their language before asking them to speak mine. I requested a 45-minute meeting framed explicitly as "I want to understand your priorities so I can show you how quality connects to them." In that meeting, I listened more than I spoke. The CFO's priorities were clear: reducing customer churn, protecting gross margin, and demonstrating operational efficiency to the board ahead of a Series C raise. I left with notes and a clear picture of what would make quality relevant to them. Over the following two weeks I built a Quality ROI report — not a testing metrics dashboard, but a board-ready financial analysis: cost of quality incidents in the prior 12 months (£340,000 in engineering time, SLA credits, and attributed churn), projected cost reduction from the current quality programme (£180,000 annually at current trajectory), and a sensitivity analysis showing that a 50% reduction in the QA team would increase escaped defect costs by an estimated £220,000 — a net loss of £130,000 against the headcount saving. I presented this in a second meeting, without any advocacy language — just the model and the assumptions. I invited them to challenge the assumptions. They did, we revised two inputs together, and the conclusion held. I also proposed a quarterly quality financial review with the CFO as a standing meeting — making them a stakeholder in quality outcomes rather than an observer.

**Result:** The headcount reduction proposal was withdrawn. The CFO became an active sponsor — they cited the quality ROI analysis in the Series C investor materials as evidence of operational maturity. The quarterly review became a forum where quality metrics were directly connected to gross margin and churn data. Two years later, when a board member asked about product reliability, the CFO answered the question — not me.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Build credibility with a direct manager through consistent delivery and clear communication. |
| **Mid** | Build stakeholder trust across teams through demonstrated value and transparent communication. |
| **Senior** | Establish and maintain sponsorship with VP-level stakeholders through data-driven influence. |
| **Lead** | Build and sustain C-suite sponsorship by connecting quality outcomes to board-level business priorities — financial, strategic, and reputational. |

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Executive Sponsorship** | Active, visible support from C-level leadership that provides political protection and resource access for a programme. |
| **Stakeholder Language** | Communicating in terms of the stakeholder's priorities — financial, strategic, or operational — rather than in functional jargon. |
| **Quality ROI Report** | A board-readable financial analysis connecting quality investment to business outcomes — the primary tool for executive sponsorship building. |
| **Sensitivity Analysis** | A model showing how the outcome changes under different assumptions — builds credibility by acknowledging uncertainty. |
| **Standing Governance** | A recurring forum that keeps an executive engaged as an active stakeholder rather than a passive observer. |
| **Sponsorship Maintenance** | The ongoing effort to keep executive sponsors engaged and informed — sponsorship that is not actively maintained will fade. |
