## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing decision-making quality and leadership composure under pressure. At Lead level, decisions carry organisational consequences — people's roles, product direction, and business risk. They want to see that you have a structured approach to uncertainty, that you involve the right people without abdicating the decision, and that you own the outcome regardless of how it plays out.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** A zero-day vulnerability was disclosed in a core infrastructure library at 11pm on a Thursday. Our security team assessed it as critical — active exploitation in the wild — but the patch released by the library maintainer was brand new, untested by our team, and had community reports of breaking changes in certain configurations. I had to decide within two hours whether to deploy the patch immediately (protecting against the known vulnerability but risking an unknown regression) or hold until morning testing could be completed (accepting 10 hours of known vulnerability exposure across a platform with 85,000 active users).

**Task:** I needed to make the call — not escalate it, not defer it, and not spread the decision across a committee at midnight. The CTO was travelling and had delegated the decision to me explicitly.

**Action:** I worked through a structured decision process in 45 minutes. First, **define the decision clearly**: this was not "patch or don't patch" — it was "patch now versus patch in 10 hours after targeted testing." Second, **assess what I knew versus what I assumed**: I knew the vulnerability was being actively exploited externally. I knew our platform held payment data. I assumed the breaking change reports affected configurations we did not use — but I could not verify this in the available time. Third, **reduce the uncertainty where possible**: I called our most experienced infrastructure engineer directly. In 15 minutes they reviewed the community reports and confirmed that the breaking changes were specific to a Redis configuration pattern we did not use — reducing my assumption to a more confident assessment. Fourth, **model the asymmetry of outcomes**: a successful patch with no regression = vulnerability closed, no user impact. A patch with a regression = vulnerability closed, potential service disruption affecting users. Holding until morning = 10 hours of known critical vulnerability exposure on a platform with payment data. The asymmetry was clear — the risk of the patch regression was recoverable; the risk of a breach was not. Fifth, **commit and prepare for both outcomes**: I authorised the immediate patch deployment and simultaneously put the on-call engineering team on standby with a rollback plan ready to execute within five minutes if a regression was detected.

**Result:** The patch deployed without incident. No regression occurred. The CTO reviewed the decision log in the morning and described the decision framework as exactly what they would have wanted to see. I wrote up the decision process as a template for future high-stakes out-of-hours decisions — so the next person in that position had a starting framework rather than a blank page.

| **Mid** | Describe owning a consequential decision with incomplete information and clear stakeholder communication. |

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Decision Framework** | A structured approach to making decisions under uncertainty — separating known from assumed, modelling outcomes, and committing clearly. |
| **Asymmetric Risk** | A situation where the consequences of the two options are not equivalent — one outcome is recoverable, the other is not. |
| **Delegated Authority** | Explicit permission from a senior leader to make a decision on their behalf — carries full ownership of the outcome. |
| **Rollback Plan** | A prepared, tested procedure for reverting a change if it causes problems — essential for high-stakes deployments. |
| **Decision Log** | A documented record of what was decided, why, what information was available, and what the outcome was — enables institutional learning. |
| **Uncertainty Reduction** | Active steps taken before a decision to convert assumptions into more confident assessments — within the available time. |
