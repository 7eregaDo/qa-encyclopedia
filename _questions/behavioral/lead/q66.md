## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing people management maturity and professional courage. Managing underperformance is one of the most uncomfortable responsibilities of leadership, and many leads avoid it or handle it poorly — through vague feedback, excessive patience, or sudden escalation. They want to see that you approach it with clarity, fairness, and genuine care for the person — while also being willing to reach a difficult conclusion if improvement does not materialise.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** A mid-level QA engineer on my team had been delivering below expectations for approximately three months — test cases were incomplete, defect reports were consistently missing reproduction context, and they had missed two sprint commitments without flagging the risk in advance. Their previous performance review had been positive, which made the decline more notable. The rest of the team was starting to compensate for the gaps, which was creating quiet resentment.

**Task:** I needed to address the underperformance directly, understand its root cause, and either support a return to expected performance or move toward a formal process — without damaging the team dynamic or the person's dignity.

**Action:** I began with a direct but private conversation. I did not open with performance data — I opened with a genuine question: "I've noticed a change in your work over the last few months and I want to understand what's going on for you." What emerged was significant: they were dealing with a serious family health situation that had been consuming their focus and energy for the past three months. They had not disclosed it because they were worried about being perceived as unreliable. This did not excuse the impact on the team, but it completely reframed the root cause — this was not a capability or motivation gap; it was a person under significant personal stress who had not been supported. I took two immediate actions. First, I worked with HR to explore flexible working arrangements and a temporary reduction in sprint commitments — giving them space to stabilise without going on formal leave. Second, I had a separate honest conversation about the impact: "I understand what you're going through, and I want to support you. At the same time, the team has been covering gaps and that's not sustainable. I need us to agree on what you can commit to reliably for the next four weeks, so we can set expectations clearly." We agreed on a reduced, clearly scoped workload with weekly check-ins. I documented the arrangement and the support provided. After eight weeks, as the personal situation stabilised, performance returned to expected levels with no further intervention needed.

**Result:** The engineer remained on the team and returned to full performance within two months. Importantly, they told me six months later that the way the situation had been handled had been the reason they stayed at the company. The team dynamic also improved — once the workload was redistributed formally rather than informally absorbed, the quiet resentment dissipated. The situation reinforced for me that underperformance almost always has a cause — and finding it before escalating to a formal process is both the right thing and the effective thing.

| **Mid** | Describe addressing a peer's impact on team delivery through a direct, professional conversation. |

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Capability Gap** | Underperformance caused by lacking the skills or knowledge required for the role. |
| **Motivation Gap** | Underperformance caused by reduced engagement, unclear expectations, or personal circumstances — not skill deficiency. |
| **Performance Improvement Plan (PIP)** | A formal, documented agreement specifying expected improvements, support provided, and consequences — used when informal support has not produced results. |
| **Root Cause of Underperformance** | The underlying reason for below-expectation output — must be identified before an appropriate response can be designed. |
| **Psychological Safety in Performance Conversations** | Creating conditions where a person feels safe disclosing the real cause of their difficulties — essential for accurate diagnosis. |
| **Documented Support** | A formal record of accommodations, check-ins, and conversations provided — protects both the employee and the organisation. |
