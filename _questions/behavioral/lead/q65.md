## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing whether you invest in building leadership capacity below you — which is both a sign of security and a marker of genuine leadership maturity. Lead-level QA professionals who do not develop successors create fragile functions entirely dependent on their own presence. They want to see a deliberate, structured approach to leadership development — not just a vague commitment to "growing people."

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** I had two strong senior QA engineers on my team — both technically excellent, both respected by their peers — but neither had ever managed people, owned a budget, or presented to the leadership team. The business was growing, and it was clear that within 12–18 months I would need one of them to step into a QA Lead role as the team expanded. Neither felt ready, and one was actively resistant to the idea of management responsibility.

**Task:** I needed to create a structured development path for both of them that built genuine lead-level capability — not just technical seniority — while respecting the one who was uncertain about the management track.

**Action:** I approached development across four leadership dimensions for each engineer, calibrated to their current gaps and interests. For the engineer who was resistant to management, I reframed the conversation: I was not asking them to become a people manager — I was asking them to develop organisational influence. The first dimension was **stakeholder communication**: I began inviting both engineers to present their area's quality metrics at the monthly engineering leadership review. Not to shadow me — to present, with me present as a support. The first time was uncomfortable; by the third time both were confident. The second dimension was **decision ownership**: I delegated specific decisions entirely to each of them — one owned the toolchain selection for a new project, the other owned the test strategy for a platform migration. I was available for counsel but did not review or override their decisions unless they were clearly harmful. The third dimension was **people development**: I asked each of them to mentor one of the junior engineers on the team — with a defined 90-day plan and structured check-ins with me. This introduced them to coaching and feedback without the full weight of performance management. The fourth dimension was **strategic contribution**: I asked each to write a one-page proposal on a quality improvement they believed in and present it at the next quarterly planning session. Both were adopted — which gave them direct experience of translating ideas into organisational outcomes. Throughout, I held monthly development 1:1s distinct from our regular 1:1s, focused specifically on leadership growth.

**Result:** Within 14 months, the engineer who had been resistant to management voluntarily asked to take on a team lead role when an opportunity arose — they had discovered through the indirect leadership experiences that influence was what they actually valued, and the role gave them that. The other engineer was promoted to QA Lead and managed a team of four within 18 months. Neither succession created disruption because both had been functioning at near-lead level for months before the title change. My own ability to take on broader responsibility was directly enabled by having two successors ready.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Describe receiving development support from a lead and applying it to grow. |
| **Mid** | Describe mentoring a junior engineer with a structured development plan. |
| **Senior** | Describe developing a mid-level engineer toward senior capability through graduated autonomy. |
| **Lead** | Design a structured leadership development programme for senior engineers — spanning stakeholder influence, decision ownership, people development, and strategic contribution — with succession as an explicit outcome. |

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Succession Planning** | The intentional development of people who can step into key roles when needed — protects against single points of failure. |
| **Leadership Dimensions** | The distinct areas of capability required for lead-level effectiveness — stakeholder influence, people development, strategic thinking, decision ownership. |
| **Delegated Decision** | A decision explicitly handed to a developing engineer to own fully — builds accountability and judgment. |
| **Indirect Leadership** | Leading through influence, mentoring, and strategic contribution rather than direct line management — an entry point to leadership for engineers who are uncertain about management. |
| **Development 1:1** | A regular conversation focused specifically on growth and leadership development — distinct from operational check-ins. |
| **Single Point of Failure** | A function or team where one person's departure would cause significant disruption — succession planning eliminates this. |
