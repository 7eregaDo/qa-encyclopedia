## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing your ability to take ownership of a testing effort in imperfect conditions — which is to say, most real projects. They want to see structured thinking, stakeholder engagement, and the confidence to make pragmatic decisions without complete information, while being transparent about what remains unknown.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** I was asked to lead testing for a new multi-currency pricing feature. The feature had been partially specced — we had general business goals and some example scenarios — but the edge cases around currency conversion, rounding behaviour, and tax calculation across regions were entirely undefined. Development was already underway.

**Task:** I needed to build a test approach that gave us meaningful coverage without waiting for complete requirements that might never arrive, while also driving clarity on the undefined areas.

**Action:** I took four steps in parallel. First, **assumption mapping**: I listed every assumption the undefined areas required me to make — e.g., "I assume rounding follows standard banker's rounding," "I assume tax is applied post-conversion, not pre." I shared this with the product owner and developer as a document titled "Testing Assumptions — Needs Confirmation." This forced the conversation about what was actually intended without me having to write the requirements myself. Second, **risk-based test design**: I divided the feature into areas by risk — currency conversion logic was highest risk (financial impact), display formatting was medium, and copy/label content was lowest. I wrote detailed test cases for high-risk areas first and used exploratory charters for undefined medium-risk areas. Third, **developer collaboration**: I ran a daily 15-minute sync with the lead developer throughout the feature build — asking questions as I designed tests rather than raising them all at the end. This resolved most ambiguities in context, while development was still fluid enough to accommodate corrections. Fourth, **coverage confidence reporting**: in my daily test status updates I introduced a "confidence" indicator per area — High, Medium, or Low — based on how well-defined the requirements were. Stakeholders could see at a glance where coverage was solid and where it rested on assumptions.

**Result:** The feature shipped with full coverage on high-risk areas and documented assumptions on the remaining two. Three of my assumption documents were converted into formal acceptance criteria that were added to the story retrospectively. One assumption was wrong — the product owner confirmed tax should be applied pre-conversion — and we caught it before development completed rather than in testing. The confidence indicator framework was adopted by the team for all subsequent complex features.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Describe asking clarifying questions and flagging ambiguity to a lead or product owner. |
| **Mid** | Lead a testing effort under ambiguity — assumption mapping, risk-based prioritisation, developer collaboration, and transparent coverage reporting. |
| **Senior** | Design a team-level practice for handling ambiguous requirements — embedding QA in discovery, defining testability as a story acceptance criterion, and building traceability from the earliest stages. |

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Assumption Mapping** | Documenting the assumptions your test design rests on, so they can be validated or corrected. |
| **Coverage Confidence** | A qualitative indicator of how reliable test coverage is, given the completeness of the underlying requirements. |
| **Exploratory Charter** | A focused mission for an exploratory testing session — used when formal test cases cannot be written due to undefined requirements. |
| **Risk-Based Test Design** | Prioritising test case creation based on the likelihood and impact of failure in each area. |
| **Definition of Testable** | A criterion ensuring that a story's acceptance criteria are specific enough to be verified by a test case. |
