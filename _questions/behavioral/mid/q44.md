## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing cross-functional maturity and conflict navigation. At mid level, QA engineers frequently sit between product, development, and business stakeholders with genuinely different goals. They want to see that you can hold the quality perspective without becoming a political actor, and that you can facilitate resolution rather than escalating conflict.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** I was caught between two conflicting demands in the same week. The product owner was pushing for a full regression sign-off on a new feature by Friday for a customer demo. The development lead was asking me to deprioritise that feature and focus on verifying a critical infrastructure change that the engineering team needed deployed before the weekend. Both were legitimate, both were urgent, and both stakeholders believed their request should take priority.

**Task:** I needed to resolve the conflict transparently without making an enemy of either stakeholder or making a unilateral decision that wasn't mine to make.

**Action:** I brought both stakeholders into a single conversation — a 20-minute Slack huddle — rather than managing them separately, which would have led to me saying different things to each person. I presented the situation neutrally: "Here is what I have capacity for by Friday, and here are the two requests. I can complete one fully and the other partially — or split time between them and do neither fully. I need you both to agree on which is the higher priority, because I'm not the right person to make that call." I also provided concrete information to help them decide: the customer demo risk (if the feature regression wasn't complete, we'd be demoing untested code) versus the infrastructure risk (if the infrastructure change went in unverified, there was a known risk of a performance regression affecting all users). Within ten minutes, both stakeholders agreed: infrastructure verification first, feature regression second, with the product owner accepting a partial feature sign-off for the demo with a documented risk caveat.

**Result:** Infrastructure was verified and deployed safely. The feature demo went ahead with a risk caveat that the product owner had agreed to in advance — which meant no surprises. Both stakeholders later acknowledged that bringing them into the same conversation had been the right call. The incident became a reference point for how the team handled priority conflicts going forward.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Escalate a priority conflict to a manager or lead rather than making the call yourself. |
| **Mid** | Facilitate a structured resolution between competing stakeholders with full transparency about constraints and trade-offs. |
| **Senior** | Build team-level prioritisation frameworks and escalation protocols that prevent ad-hoc priority conflicts from becoming recurring crises. |

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Stakeholder Alignment** | The process of reaching a shared understanding and agreement between parties with different needs or priorities. |
| **Priority Conflict** | A situation where two or more legitimate requests compete for the same limited resource. |
| **Transparent Trade-off** | Making the cost and consequence of each option visible to all decision-makers before a choice is made. |
| **Neutral Facilitation** | Presenting a conflict without advocating for one side, enabling stakeholders to make their own informed decision. |
| **Risk Caveat** | A documented acknowledgement that a decision was made with known, accepted risk. |
