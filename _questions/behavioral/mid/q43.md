## The Intent

The interviewer is testing pattern recognition, root cause thinking, and the ability to drive change beyond your immediate work. They want to see that you look upstream from defects to process, and that you can make a compelling case for systemic change without needing to be asked.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** Over two consecutive quarters, I noticed that a disproportionate number of production defects — roughly 60% — were originating from a single team within a multi-team product. The defects weren't concentrated in one feature area; they were spread across everything that team touched. The pattern was consistent enough that I was confident it wasn't bad luck.

**Task:** I needed to investigate the root cause and propose a solution without pointing blame at the team, which would have been counterproductive and unfair.

**Action:** I ran a quiet analysis over two weeks. I pulled the defect data, tagged each defect by originating team, and then looked for correlations: story complexity, sprint velocity, test coverage at merge, review process, and sprint ceremony attendance. Three patterns emerged: the team had the highest velocity in the organisation (moving very fast), the lowest unit test coverage (developers were not writing tests), and no QA involvement in story refinement (requirements were being clarified only after development began). I wrote a short analysis document — framed as "observations and hypotheses," not accusations — and shared it first with the engineering manager for that team, then proposed presenting it to the wider engineering leadership. I recommended three targeted interventions: introducing a minimum unit test coverage gate for that team's pipeline, adding QA to their story refinement sessions, and temporarily reducing their sprint velocity target to create space for quality practices to take hold.

**Result:** The engineering manager was receptive — they had suspected something similar but lacked the data to act. All three interventions were adopted. Over the following quarter, that team's contribution to production defects dropped from 60% to 22% of the total — in line with their share of the codebase. The unit test coverage gate was later extended to all teams as a standard pipeline requirement.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Identify a recurring defect pattern and raise it to a lead or manager with supporting observations. |
| **Mid** | Conduct a data-driven root cause analysis, frame findings constructively, and propose specific structural interventions with measurable outcomes. |
| **Senior** | Build organisation-wide quality monitoring that surfaces systemic patterns automatically, and lead cross-team improvement programmes with executive sponsorship. |

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Systemic Problem** | A recurring issue caused by a flaw in process, structure, or culture — not fixable by addressing individual incidents. |
| **Root Cause Analysis (RCA)** | A structured investigation into the underlying cause of a problem, rather than its symptoms. |
| **Defect Attribution** | Identifying which team, process, or phase of the SDLC introduced a defect. |
| **Velocity vs. Quality Trade-off** | The tension between moving fast and maintaining quality — high velocity without quality investment typically increases defect rate. |
| **Structural Intervention** | A change to process, tooling, or workflow that addresses a root cause, rather than a one-time fix to an individual problem. |
