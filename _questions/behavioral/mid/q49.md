## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing influence without authority, empathy, and professional resilience. Being technically skilled is not enough if you can't integrate effectively with resistant teams. They want to see that you respond to resistance with curiosity and adaptation rather than frustration or escalation.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** I was brought in to provide QA support for a startup-culture feature team that had never had a dedicated QA engineer. The team was fast-moving, proud of their autonomy, and openly sceptical — one developer said in my first team meeting, "We've always shipped without QA, so I'm not sure what the value-add is here."

**Task:** I needed to establish credibility and demonstrate genuine value quickly, without imposing process that the team would resent or circumvent.

**Action:** I made a deliberate decision not to introduce any new process for the first two weeks. Instead, I embedded myself in their workflow: attended their standups, read their PRs, joined their Slack channels, and asked questions rather than making recommendations. By the end of week two, I had a clear picture of where their pain was — intermittent production bugs that they were fixing reactively, and a growing backlog of customer-reported issues that were damaging their reputation with a key enterprise client. I used this knowledge to make my first contribution highly targeted: I offered to write a set of automated smoke tests for the three features the enterprise client used most heavily, framing it as "I can give you five minutes of confidence after every deploy that those features are working." No new meetings, no process, no overhead — just a green or red signal in their existing Slack deploy channel. The team agreed because it solved their pain without adding friction.

**Result:** The smoke tests caught a broken feature in the second week — before the enterprise client noticed. The developer who had been most sceptical sent a message in Slack saying "okay, that was useful." From that foothold, I gradually introduced lightweight practices — exploratory charters, story kick-offs — as the team's trust grew. By month three, the same developer was proactively asking me to review acceptance criteria before stories were developed. The relationship was built through demonstrated value, not imposed process.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Adapt your communication style to a resistant colleague and find a small way to demonstrate value. |
| **Mid** | Embed in a resistant team, diagnose their real pain, and introduce targeted QA value that solves their problem before introducing process. |
| **Senior** | Design an embedded QA model that works with autonomous teams at scale — lightweight, high-value, non-prescriptive — and build the organisational case for QA as a strategic function rather than a compliance overhead. |

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Resistance to QA** | Scepticism or reluctance toward QA involvement, usually rooted in past experience or cultural values. |
| **Embedded QA** | A model where QA engineers work directly within feature teams rather than in a separate QA function. |
| **Demonstrated Value** | Building credibility through concrete, visible results rather than proposals or promises. |
| **Low-Friction Integration** | Introducing QA contributions in a way that minimises additional overhead or process change for the team. |
| **Trust Building** | The process of establishing credibility and goodwill with a sceptical team — the prerequisite for influence. |
