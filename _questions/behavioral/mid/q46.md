## The Intent

The interviewer wants to see that you can operate in grey areas without either blocking releases unnecessarily or waving things through irresponsibly. Mid-level engineers are expected to own the recommendation, not just present data and wait for someone else to decide. They also want to confirm that you document decisions properly so there is no ambiguity about what was and wasn't tested.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** A test environment outage consumed 1.5 days of a 3-day testing window. By the time of the release sign-off meeting, I had executed 71% of planned test cases. The untested 29% covered lower-risk areas — UI copy changes, secondary navigation, and help text — but the gap was real and needed to be named.

**Task:** I needed to make a clear release recommendation, communicate the coverage gap honestly, and ensure the business was making an informed decision — not an unknowing one.

**Action:** I prepared a one-page release readiness summary with four sections. First, what was tested and the outcome — 71% execution, zero critical defects found, two minor defects deferred with product owner agreement. Second, what was not tested and why — specific list of untested areas with a plain-language description of each, plus the reason (environment outage, documented). Third, my risk assessment of the untested areas — all were low-risk based on the nature of the changes (cosmetic, no logic changes, no database interaction), and none had been modified in this release cycle. Fourth, my recommendation — release with documented residual risk, with a commitment to complete the remaining 29% in the next available testing window and deploy a follow-up patch if anything was found. I presented this at the sign-off meeting and was explicit: "I'm recommending release, and I want you to know exactly what we haven't tested and why I believe it's acceptable."

**Result:** The release was approved by the product owner and engineering manager with full awareness of the gap. The remaining test cases were executed two days post-release — nothing was found. The documented residual risk record meant that if anything had gone wrong in those areas, there was a clear, honest paper trail. No one was blindsided.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Report coverage gaps honestly to a lead and flag the risk clearly. |
| **Mid** | Own the release recommendation — document coverage state, risk assessment, and rationale, and present it clearly to stakeholders. |
| **Senior** | Build a release readiness framework with tiered risk thresholds, automated coverage reporting, and pre-agreed policies for releasing with defined residual risk levels. |

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Release Recommendation** | A QA engineer's formal assessment of whether a build is ready for production deployment. |
| **Residual Risk** | Known, untested or partially tested areas where a defect could exist at time of release. |
| **Release Readiness Summary** | A concise document covering test execution status, open defects, coverage gaps, and a recommendation. |
| **Risk Acceptance** | A stakeholder's explicit acknowledgement that they understand and accept a defined level of residual risk. |
| **Coverage Gap Documentation** | A formal record of what was not tested, why, and what the assessed risk is — essential for post-release accountability. |
