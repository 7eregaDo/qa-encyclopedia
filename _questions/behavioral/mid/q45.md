## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing leadership potential and collaborative instincts. At mid level, the ability to elevate others is as important as individual performance. They want to see that you invest in colleagues with genuine care, use effective teaching methods, and measure success by the mentee's growth — not by how much they depend on you.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** A junior QA engineer joined our team with no prior professional testing experience — they had come from a customer support background and were transitioning into QA. They were eager but writing test cases that were either too high-level to be executable or so granular they were testing implementation details rather than user behaviour.

**Task:** I was asked to informally mentor them for their first three months. I needed to improve their test design skills without micromanaging or undermining their confidence.

**Action:** I used three approaches. First, **structured review with explanation**: rather than rewriting their test cases, I returned them with specific comments — "this step assumes the user is already logged in, but there's no precondition stating that" or "this expected result says 'the page loads correctly' — what does correctly mean here?" I always explained the why behind each comment, not just the what. Second, **pair testing sessions**: once a week we would test a story together — I would narrate my thinking out loud as I designed and executed tests, so they could observe the decision-making process, not just the output. After two sessions, I reversed it — they narrated while I asked questions. Third, **graduated autonomy**: in the first month, I reviewed all their work before it was submitted. In the second month, I reviewed after submission and before sign-off. By the third month, I only reviewed if they flagged uncertainty. This gave them increasing ownership while maintaining a safety net.

**Result:** By the end of month three, their test cases were consistently executable, well-scoped, and required minimal rework. More importantly, when they encountered a new scenario they hadn't seen before, they could reason through it independently rather than asking what to do. They told me at their three-month review that the pair testing sessions had been the most useful part of their onboarding. They went on to become one of the most thorough testers on the team.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Describe helping a peer with a specific task or explaining a concept clearly. |
| **Mid** | Describe a structured mentoring relationship — calibrated feedback, graduated autonomy, and measurable growth over time. |
| **Senior** | Build a team-wide onboarding and mentoring programme, define career progression expectations, and create the conditions for junior engineers to grow without requiring senior attention for every decision. |

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Mentoring** | A developmental relationship focused on building a person's skills, confidence, and professional judgement over time. |
| **Graduated Autonomy** | Progressively reducing guidance as a mentee's competence grows, building independence without removing support too early. |
| **Pair Testing** | A collaborative testing session where two people test together, sharing thinking and building skills simultaneously. |
| **Specific Feedback** | Feedback that references a concrete example and explains both what the issue is and why it matters. |
| **Psychological Safety** | An environment where a junior team member feels safe to ask questions, make mistakes, and learn without fear of judgement. |
