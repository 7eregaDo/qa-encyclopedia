## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing analytical thinking and stakeholder influence. Mid-level engineers are expected to move beyond reporting data to using it as a persuasion tool. They want to see that you understand what motivates different stakeholders and can frame data to speak directly to those motivations.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** The engineering team was debating whether to invest a sprint in test automation or continue with manual testing. The argument against automation was that "we move too fast — automated tests break constantly and cost more to maintain than they save." Several engineers had had bad experiences with fragile Selenium suites in previous roles, and the opinion was strongly held.

**Task:** I needed to either validate or challenge that belief with actual data from our context — not generic arguments about the benefits of automation.

**Action:** I spent two days pulling data from three sources. First, our Jira time-tracking data: I calculated the total time spent on manual regression over the previous quarter — 47 engineer-hours per sprint cycle, across the team. Second, our defect log: I identified which defects in the last six months were in areas already covered by our limited existing automation versus areas with only manual coverage. The automated areas had a 12% defect escape rate to production; the manual-only areas had a 34% escape rate. Third, I looked at our existing automation suite's maintenance history — 11 tests had been updated in six months across 80 tests, an average of 14 minutes per update. This directly contradicted the "automation breaks constantly" narrative with our own data. I built a simple one-page summary showing these three data points and a projected ROI: at our current regression frequency, automation of the top 30 manual tests would break even in 8 weeks and save approximately 20 engineer-hours per sprint thereafter.

**Result:** The conversation shifted entirely. The engineers who had been most resistant acknowledged that the data from our context was different from what they had experienced before. The sprint investment was approved. The automation was built and broke even in week 9 — one week later than projected. The "automation breaks constantly" narrative was replaced with "we had bad automation before, but designed well it's worth it."

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Use defect counts or test results to support a recommendation to a lead or product owner. |
| **Mid** | Conduct a multi-source data analysis, frame it for a specific stakeholder's concerns, and change a strongly-held opinion with evidence from the team's own context. |
| **Senior** | Build a quality metrics programme that continuously generates the data needed to make investment decisions, and present quality ROI to executive audiences in financial terms. |

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Data-Driven Influence** | Using evidence to shift a stakeholder's position from opinion or assumption to informed decision. |
| **Defect Escape Rate** | The percentage of defects that pass through testing and reach production — a key comparative metric. |
| **ROI Projection** | An estimate of when an investment will break even and what returns it will generate over time. |
| **Contextual Data** | Data drawn from the team's own history — more persuasive than generic industry benchmarks when challenging a strongly-held belief. |
| **Audience Framing** | Presenting data in terms of what the specific audience cares about — cost for finance, risk for product, velocity for engineering. |
