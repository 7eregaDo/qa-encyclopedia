## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing your professionalism, communication skills, and ability to hold your position under pushback without becoming adversarial. They also want to see that you approach disputes with evidence and empathy — not ego. The ability to navigate this dynamic constructively is a core QA competency at every level.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** I raised a defect on a date picker that allowed users to select past dates for a future-only booking field. The developer closed it as "works as designed," saying the product owner hadn't explicitly specified that past dates should be disabled.

**Task:** I needed to reopen and justify the defect without creating conflict, while ensuring the user impact was properly considered before the release.

**Action:** I went back to the requirements document and found that while it didn't explicitly say "disable past dates," it did state "users can only book appointments for future availability." I also looked at competitor implementations and UX conventions — date pickers for future-only bookings universally disable past dates. I reopened the defect with three additions to the ticket: a direct quote from the requirements, two screenshots of industry-standard implementations for context, and a clear user impact statement ("users can select invalid dates, submit the form, and receive a confusing server-side error rather than clear inline validation"). I sent a short Slack message to the developer before they saw the update — not to argue, but to say I'd added context and would welcome a quick chat if they disagreed. We spoke for five minutes, agreed the requirements were ambiguous, and looped in the product owner to make the final call.

**Result:** The product owner confirmed the field should restrict past dates — the requirement had been implied but not written explicitly. The fix was made that sprint. The developer thanked me for handling it without escalating over their head. The product owner updated the requirement to be explicit for future reference.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Show you use evidence and requirements to support your position, and that you escalate respectfully rather than abandoning the issue or arguing. |
| **Mid** | Describe a situation involving genuine ambiguity — where neither party was wrong — and how you facilitated a three-way resolution with the product owner. |
| **Senior** | Discuss how you build a team culture where defect disputes are rare because acceptance criteria are clear upfront, and how you handle systemic patterns of invalid rejections. |

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Defect Rejection** | A developer's decision to close a bug as invalid, works as designed, or not reproducible. |
| **Works as Designed (WAD)** | A closure reason indicating the behaviour is intentional — valid only if the design itself is correct. |
| **Requirements Reference** | Citing a specific requirement to provide objective evidence for a defect's validity. |
| **Escalation Path** | The chain of people to involve when a dispute cannot be resolved at the current level. |
| **Three Amigos** | A collaborative session involving developer, tester, and product owner — useful for resolving ambiguous defect disputes. |
