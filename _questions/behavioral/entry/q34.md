## The Intent

The interviewer is deliberately asking you to admit a failure. They are not looking for perfection — they are assessing self-awareness, honesty, and a growth mindset. Candidates who claim they have never missed anything are unconvincing. Candidates who describe a miss and show genuine learning from it demonstrate the maturity that high-functioning QA teams require.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** I was testing a user profile update feature. All my test cases passed and I signed off on the story. After release, a user reported that updating their email address also silently updated their notification preferences to default values — resetting customisations they had spent time configuring.

**Task:** I needed to understand how this had slipped through, own my part in it honestly, and make sure it didn't happen again.

**Action:** I did a brief personal post-mortem. Looking back at my test cases, I had focused entirely on the fields I was testing — first name, last name, email — and had not checked adjacent data that the same save operation touched. I had no test case for "verify that fields not being edited remain unchanged after save." This was a gap in my test design thinking — I was testing what I expected to change, not what I expected to remain the same. I raised this finding openly in the team retrospective, taking ownership rather than attributing it to unclear requirements. I also proposed a new checklist item for all future data update test cases: "Verify that unrelated fields are not affected by the save operation."

**Result:** The defect was fixed in a hotfix the following day. The checklist item was adopted by the team and caught a similar side-effect issue two sprints later — in testing, before it reached production. The retrospective discussion led to a broader conversation about negative and side-effect testing that improved the whole team's test design.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Honestly describe a miss, take clear ownership, identify the specific root cause, and explain a concrete change you made as a result. |
| **Mid** | Describe a miss with wider team impact, how you communicated it, led the post-mortem, and drove a process change. |
| **Senior** | Discuss systemic coverage gaps that led to a pattern of misses, and the structural changes you made to the test strategy, team practices, or tooling to address the root cause. |

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Coverage Gap** | An area of the system that was not tested and through which a defect escaped. |
| **Side-Effect Testing** | Verifying that a change in one area does not unintentionally affect adjacent or unrelated areas. |
| **Root Cause Analysis** | Identifying the underlying reason a defect was not caught, rather than just describing what was missed. |
| **Growth Mindset** | The belief that skills and practices can be improved through reflection and deliberate change. |
| **Retrospective** | A team ceremony for reflecting on what went well, what didn't, and what to change — the right forum for owning and learning from misses. |
