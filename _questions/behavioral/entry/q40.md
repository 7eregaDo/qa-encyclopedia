## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing emotional maturity, coachability, and self-awareness. They are not looking for a story where you were right and the feedback was wrong. They want evidence that you can hear difficult feedback, process it honestly, and grow from it. Candidates who reframe every piece of critical feedback as a misunderstanding are a red flag.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** During a sprint retrospective, the lead developer gave me direct feedback that my defect reports were too vague — specifically that the reproduction steps often skipped context that made bugs difficult to reproduce, and that developers were losing time trying to recreate conditions I had documented incompletely.

**Task:** The feedback stung a little — I thought my reports were thorough. But I needed to hear it honestly and respond constructively rather than defensively.

**Action:** My first response in the moment was to say "thank you — can you show me a specific example?" rather than explaining myself. The developer pulled up a recent ticket and walked me through exactly what was missing: I had written "log in and go to settings" without specifying which user account, which browser, or whether I was in a specific data state. Once I saw a concrete example, the feedback landed clearly — I hadn't been providing enough environmental context because I assumed the developer would know what I knew. I asked the developer to share two or three more examples so I could identify the pattern. Over the following week, I rewrote my defect template to include mandatory fields: test environment, browser/device, user account or role, precondition state, and data used. I also added a personal rule: before submitting any ticket, I would ask "could someone who wasn't in the room when I found this reproduce it from this report alone?"

**Result:** Within two sprints, the same developer told me unprompted that my defect reports had become noticeably clearer and that they were no longer losing time on reproduction. The template I created was later adopted as the team's standard defect format. The feedback, though uncomfortable initially, was one of the most practically useful things I received early in my career.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Demonstrate receptiveness, a specific concrete change made in response, and genuine reflection — not just acknowledgement. |
| **Mid** | Describe receiving feedback that required a significant shift in approach or mindset — not just a process tweak. |
| **Senior** | Discuss actively seeking feedback from multiple sources (peers, stakeholders, reports), building a personal feedback culture, and modelling constructive feedback reception for junior team members. |

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Constructive Feedback** | Specific, actionable criticism aimed at improving performance — distinct from vague or personal criticism. |
| **Defensive Response** | Reacting to feedback by explaining, justifying, or dismissing rather than listening and reflecting. |
| **Coachability** | The ability to receive feedback, adapt behaviour, and improve — a highly valued trait in collaborative teams. |
| **Defect Report Quality** | The clarity, completeness, and reproducibility of a bug report — directly impacts developer efficiency. |
| **Growth Mindset** | The belief that abilities and practices can be improved through effort, feedback, and reflection. |
