## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing conflict resolution at leadership level, political awareness, and the ability to maintain relationships under sustained disagreement. They want to see that you can hold your professional position firmly while remaining constructive, and that you understand the difference between a disagreement you need to win and one you need to resolve.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** The VP of Engineering and I had a sustained, six-week disagreement over the role of QA in the deployment pipeline. Their position was that automated tests in CI were sufficient and that QA sign-off before production was unnecessary overhead that slowed deployment frequency. My position was that automated tests caught structural defects but not behavioural or UX regressions, and that removing QA sign-off would increase escaped defects in non-automatable areas.

**Task:** The disagreement was beginning to affect team morale — engineers were caught between two conflicting senior positions — and needed to be resolved, not just managed.

**Action:** I took three steps. First, **separate the relationship from the disagreement**: I had a direct, private conversation with the VP acknowledging that we had a genuine difference of view and that the team was feeling it. I proposed that we commit to resolving it within two weeks through evidence, not opinion. The VP agreed. Second, **design a shared experiment**: I proposed a four-sprint trial where QA sign-off was removed from low-risk deployments (config changes, copy updates, minor UI adjustments) but retained for medium and high-risk changes as classified by our existing risk tier framework. We agreed to track escaped defect rate by risk tier across the trial period. Third, **let the data arbitrate**: at the end of four sprints, the data showed that low-risk deployments without QA sign-off had a 3% escaped defect rate — statistically identical to when sign-off had been required. Medium and high-risk changes with sign-off maintained a 6% rate, while a control period without sign-off (from a previous quarter) had shown 19%. The data supported a nuanced position: the VP was right about low-risk changes, and I was right about medium and high-risk changes.

**Result:** We co-presented the findings to the engineering leadership team and jointly proposed a tiered sign-off policy — exactly the kind of shared ownership that wouldn't have been possible if either of us had simply won the argument. The policy was adopted, deployment frequency increased for low-risk changes, and QA sign-off was preserved where it demonstrably added value. The VP became one of my strongest advocates for the following year's QA investment request.

| Level | Expected Depth |

| **Mid** | Resolve a disagreement with a peer or stakeholder using data and structured conversation. |
| **Senior** | Navigate a sustained leadership-level disagreement — separating relationship from debate, designing a shared evidence-based resolution, and emerging with a stronger working relationship than before. |

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Shared Experiment** | A time-bounded test designed to resolve a disagreement through evidence rather than opinion. |
| **Risk Tier Framework** | A classification system for changes by risk level — enables differentiated policies rather than blanket rules. |
| **Relationship Maintenance** | Actively preserving the working relationship during a disagreement — distinct from resolving the disagreement itself. |
| **Co-ownership of Resolution** | Presenting a solution jointly with the opposing party, so neither side is seen to have lost. |
| **Evidence Arbitration** | Using data to resolve a disagreement — effective when both parties agree upfront to be bound by the result. |
