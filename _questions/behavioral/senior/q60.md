## The Intent

The interviewer is looking for intellectual honesty, adaptability, and the absence of ego-defensive behaviour. This is one of the most revealing questions at senior level because many people in leadership positions become attached to their strategies and resist contradictory evidence. Candidates who cannot describe a time their strategy was wrong are either lacking self-awareness or have not operated at sufficient complexity. The best answer demonstrates that you updated your position quickly when the evidence warranted it.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** I had built a QA strategy centred heavily on end-to-end automated testing — a comprehensive E2E suite covering 180 user journeys, running in a full staging environment. My reasoning was that E2E tests gave the highest confidence in user-facing behaviour and were the most direct reflection of real user experience. I had invested six months and significant team capacity in building and stabilising the suite.

**Task:** After six months of operation, the data told a story I had not anticipated. I needed to honestly assess it and respond to it — even though it meant acknowledging that a major strategic investment had been partially misdirected.

**Action:** The evidence came from two sources. First, our defect attribution analysis showed that over the previous quarter, 71% of production defects had been caught not by E2E tests, but by unit tests and API-level integration tests — which we had invested in only lightly. The E2E suite, despite its size, had caught 12% of production defects. Second, our pipeline data showed the E2E suite was consuming 40 minutes of CI runtime and had a 19% flake rate, making it the primary source of pipeline delays and developer frustration. I had prioritised the wrong layer. I shared this analysis with the team and the VP of Engineering without softening it: "The data shows our strategy over-indexed on E2E coverage and under-invested in the layers that are actually catching the most defects. I got this wrong and I want to correct it." I then proposed a revised strategy: reduce the E2E suite to 60 high-value journeys (from 180), invest the recovered capacity in expanding the API integration test layer and supporting developers in improving unit test coverage. I modelled the expected improvement in pipeline runtime and projected defect detection based on the attribution data.

**Result:** The revised strategy was implemented over two sprints. Pipeline runtime fell from 40 minutes to 18 minutes. The flake rate dropped from 19% to 4%. Over the following quarter, the proportion of defects caught pre-production increased by 22% — the new investment in the mid-layers was more effective per hour of effort than the E2E suite had been. The most important outcome, however, was the trust it built with the engineering team: watching a senior leader publicly update their position based on evidence made it safer for everyone else to do the same.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |

| **Mid** | Describe revising a test strategy based on defect data or coverage analysis — with a specific before/after comparison. |
| **Senior** | Describe publicly acknowledging a strategic error, updating the strategy based on evidence, and modelling intellectual honesty as a leadership behaviour for the team. |

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Intellectual Honesty** | The willingness to update beliefs and strategies when evidence contradicts them — regardless of prior investment. |
| **Defect Attribution Analysis** | Identifying which testing layer or phase detected each defect — reveals where the strategy is working and where it isn't. |
| **Testing Pyramid** | A model recommending more unit tests, fewer integration tests, and even fewer E2E tests — based on speed and cost of feedback. |
| **Strategy Revision** | A deliberate change to a high-level approach based on evidence that the current approach is not achieving its goals. |
| **Sunk Cost Fallacy** | The tendency to continue investing in a failing strategy because of what has already been spent — the bias that intellectual honesty must overcome. |
