## The Intent

The interviewer is testing pragmatism, professional judgment, and the ability to operate in the real world rather than an ideal one. They are specifically checking that you are not dogmatic — that you can distinguish principle from preference, and that you can accept a business decision without either capitulating silently or becoming obstructive. This question also reveals how you handle being overruled.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** I had introduced a standard requiring all production deployments to be preceded by a full automated regression run — a practice I believed strongly in and had implemented successfully over the previous year. During a period of aggressive market expansion, the business needed to deploy configuration and pricing changes multiple times per day to support regional launches. Our regression suite took 35 minutes to run, making the standard incompatible with the required deployment frequency.

**Task:** The VP of Engineering asked me to suspend the full regression gate for configuration-only deployments. I disagreed — I had seen configuration changes cause production issues before — but I needed to engage with the business reality rather than simply refusing.

**Action:** I did not immediately agree or disagree. I asked for 48 hours to analyse the risk properly. I reviewed 12 months of deployment history and identified every incident where a configuration change had caused a production problem. I found four incidents — all of them involving changes to pricing logic or feature flag interactions, not pure configuration values. I used this to propose a narrower version of the standard: full regression was suspended for pure configuration deployments (environment variables, copy, regional settings), but retained for pricing, feature flag, and permission-related changes. I documented the decision formally — including my original recommendation, the business rationale for the change, the risk I assessed as accepted, and a commitment to revisit the standard in 90 days once deployment tooling improvements were planned. I also added a lightweight post-deploy smoke test (3 minutes) as a minimum gate for all configuration deployments, so there was still an automated check — just not the full suite.

**Result:** The modified standard was adopted. Over the following quarter, zero production incidents originated from configuration deployments. The pricing and feature flag gate remained in place and caught two issues during that period. At the 90-day review, the deployment tooling had been optimised enough that full regression runtime had fallen to 12 minutes — and the full standard was quietly reinstated. The documentation of the decision meant there was no ambiguity about what had been agreed and why, which protected both me and the VP if anything had gone wrong.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Describe accepting a decision you disagreed with and executing it professionally. |
| **Mid** | Propose a modified alternative to a standard that balances quality and business need, and document the accepted risk. |
| **Senior** | Distinguish non-negotiable standards from best practice, negotiate pragmatic adaptations with documented risk acceptance, and revisit decisions when conditions change. |

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Non-Negotiable Standard** | A quality requirement that cannot be compromised — typically involving safety, data integrity, or legal compliance. |
| **Best Practice** | A recommended approach that represents high quality but can be adapted based on context and constraints. |
| **Risk Documentation** | A formal record of what standard was relaxed, why, what risk was accepted, and who approved it. |
| **Pragmatic Adaptation** | A modification to a standard that preserves its intent while accommodating a real-world constraint. |
| **Standard Reinstatement** | Returning to the original standard when the conditions that required the adaptation no longer exist. |
