## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing organisational influence and leadership legacy. This is one of the most demanding senior questions because it asks about impact beyond your direct authority. They want to see that you have operated at a systems level — changing how people think, not just what they do — and that the change was durable beyond your personal involvement.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** I joined a 200-person engineering organisation where quality was treated as a release-time activity — something that happened at the end of development, owned exclusively by QA. The language in the organisation reflected this: "has it been QA'd?" was the standard release question. Developers did not write unit tests as a norm, product managers did not include acceptance criteria by default, and post-release bugs were attributed to "QA missing things" rather than to the development process.

**Task:** I wanted to shift the organisational mental model from "QA is a function that checks things" to "quality is an engineering value that everyone owns." This was a multi-year effort and required operating well beyond the QA team.

**Action:** I worked across four organisational levers over 18 months. **Language**: I was deliberate about the words used in every forum I had access to. I stopped using "QA sign-off" and started using "team quality check." I replaced "QA found a bug" with "we found a bug in testing." Small shifts, but language shapes mental models. I also introduced "quality" as a standing agenda item in the monthly engineering all-hands — making it as visible as velocity and delivery. **Structural incentives**: I worked with engineering managers to add quality contributions to the engineering performance framework — writing tests, improving coverage, participating in exploratory sessions, and contributing to post-mortems were made explicitly visible in career progression criteria. This changed the incentive structure so that quality investment was professionally rewarded, not just morally encouraged. **Visible wins and shared credit**: every time a significant defect was caught pre-production, I made the story visible — in Slack, in all-hands, in retrospectives — and always credited the whole team, not just QA. When developers caught bugs in code review, I called it out publicly. This reinforced the behaviour and made quality ownership aspirational. **Leadership modelling**: I was deliberate about how I spoke about quality failures. I never said "QA missed this" — I said "we missed this" and led the root cause analysis to show that the failure was systemic, not individual. Over time, engineering managers began using the same language.

**Result:** At the 18-month mark, a developer survey showed that 78% of engineers agreed with the statement "quality is everyone's responsibility" — up from 31% at the start. Unit test coverage across the codebase had increased from 24% to 61% without a mandate — driven by cultural shift, not enforcement. The phrase "has it been QA'd?" disappeared from release conversations. The CPO cited the quality culture shift in a public blog post about the company's engineering values. Most importantly, the change continued after I moved into a new role — which is the only real test of whether a culture change has taken hold.

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Culture Change** | A sustained shift in the beliefs, values, and behaviours of an organisation — not just its processes. |
| **Language as a Lever** | The deliberate use of words and framing to shape how people think about a concept over time. |
| **Structural Incentives** | Changes to performance frameworks, career criteria, or recognition systems that reward desired behaviours. |
| **Leadership Modelling** | Demonstrating the behaviour and mindset you want to see in others through your own visible actions. |
| **Durable Change** | An organisational improvement that persists after the person who drove it has moved on — the highest test of culture change. |
