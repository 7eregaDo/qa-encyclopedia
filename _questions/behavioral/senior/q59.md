## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing your talent philosophy and leadership depth. Senior QA leaders are expected to build teams, not just join them. They want to understand what you look for, how you evaluate it, how your hiring decisions have played out, and how deliberately you invest in people after hiring them. The quality of a team two years after a senior hire is one of the most lasting measures of their leadership.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** After the QA transformation I had led was showing results, I was given approval to grow the team from two to five engineers over six months. I needed to hire three QA engineers at different levels — one senior, one mid, one junior — while maintaining delivery throughput and onboarding each hire effectively.

**Task:** I needed to design a hiring process that identified the right people, not just the most credentialed ones, and a development model that got each hire to full effectiveness as quickly as possible without burning them out or setting them up to fail.

**Action:** For **hiring**, I built a process around three signals I had learned to trust over years of interviewing. First, **thinking out loud under ambiguity**: rather than asking candidates to recite test case templates, I gave them a real, poorly-specified feature description and asked how they would approach testing it. I was not looking for the right answer — I was watching how they dealt with not knowing. The best candidates asked clarifying questions, stated their assumptions explicitly, and prioritised by risk. Second, **learning velocity**: I asked every candidate to describe something technical they had taught themselves in the last 12 months. I was assessing whether they were self-directed learners — because QA tooling evolves faster than any onboarding programme can anticipate. Third, **collaboration instinct**: I asked for a specific story about a disagreement with a developer. I was looking for candidates who approached it as a shared problem, not a battle. For **development**, I built a 90-day plan for each hire: structured in three phases — observe and learn (weeks 1–3), contribute with support (weeks 4–8), own independently (weeks 9–12). I held weekly 1:1s with a consistent agenda: what went well, what was hard, what do you need from me. I also gave each hire a stretch project — something slightly beyond their current level — within their first month. Not to pressure them, but to show them I believed they were capable of more than their current role required.

**Result:** All three hires were contributing independently within 10 weeks — ahead of the 12-week target. The senior hire went on to lead the automation programme and was promoted to QA lead within 18 months. The junior hire — who had come from a non-technical background — became one of the team's most valued exploratory testers within a year, demonstrating that hiring for thinking over credentials had been the right call. The team's output per engineer increased by 40% over the following year despite the complexity of onboarding three new people simultaneously.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Hiring Signal** | A specific, observable indicator during an interview that predicts performance in the role. |
| **Ambiguity Tolerance** | The ability to work effectively and make good decisions without complete information — a core QA signal. |
| **Learning Velocity** | How quickly a person acquires new skills — a leading indicator of long-term performance in a fast-changing field. |
| **Stretch Project** | An assignment slightly beyond a person's current level, designed to accelerate growth while building confidence. |
| **90-Day Onboarding Plan** | A structured development programme for new hires, divided into phases of increasing autonomy. |
