## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing crisis leadership — specifically whether you can shift from planned execution to reactive response without losing composure, clarity, or the confidence of your team. They want to see that you make decisions quickly with incomplete information, communicate the rationale clearly, and lead people — not just tasks — through a high-pressure period.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** Three days into a two-week sprint, a critical security vulnerability was disclosed in a third-party authentication library we were using across the entire platform. The vulnerability was public, actively being exploited in the wild, and our security team assessed it as requiring immediate remediation — within 48 hours. My QA team had four engineers mid-way through planned sprint testing for a major feature release scheduled for the following week.

**Task:** I needed to immediately redirect the entire team to support the security patch verification effort, communicate the impact on the sprint commitments to stakeholders, and ensure the security work was done thoroughly under extreme time pressure — without cutting corners on verification quality.

**Action:** I called the team together within 30 minutes of the security assessment landing. I was direct: the sprint plan was suspended, the security patch was now the only priority, and I needed to explain why. I gave them the full context — the nature of the vulnerability, the exploitation risk, and what our customers stood to lose if we delayed. Giving the team the full picture was non-negotiable — people work harder and better when they understand what they are protecting. I then divided the response. I assigned two engineers to build a targeted security regression suite — testing authentication flows, session handling, token validation, and privilege escalation scenarios — while I worked with the security lead to understand the full blast radius of the patch. The other two engineers maintained monitoring on the production systems and prepared rollback test scenarios in case the patch introduced instability. I also immediately communicated to the product owner and CPO: the feature release would slip by at least one week, and here is why. I did not apologise for the decision — I explained it.

**Result:** The security patch was verified and deployed within 31 hours. The authentication regression suite — built in crisis conditions — was retained as a permanent security testing layer. The feature release slipped by six days. No stakeholder pushed back once the context was clear. Two engineers told me later that the way I had explained the stakes had made the 48-hour push feel meaningful rather than just stressful.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Adapt to a change in priorities directed by a lead and execute the revised plan effectively. |
| **Mid** | Re-prioritise your own work and communicate the impact on commitments to your direct stakeholders. |
| **Senior** | Re-prioritise a team's entire workload in a crisis — rapid assessment, decisive direction, full-context communication, stakeholder management, and sustained leadership through the response. |

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Crisis Prioritisation** | The rapid, decisive reallocation of resources to address an unexpected high-severity situation. |
| **Blast Radius** | The scope of systems, users, or data potentially affected by a vulnerability or failure. |
| **Security Regression** | A targeted test suite verifying that security controls are functioning correctly after a patch or change. |
| **Full-Context Communication** | Sharing the complete rationale for a decision with the team — builds trust and motivation during a crisis. |
| **Stakeholder Management in Crisis** | Proactively communicating plan changes to stakeholders before they ask — prevents confusion and demonstrates control. |
