## The Intent

The interviewer is assessing business acumen, executive communication, and the ability to advocate for your function effectively. Senior QA leaders must compete for budget and headcount alongside every other engineering function. They want to see that you can build a rigorous financial case — not just explain why testing is important in principle.

---

## The STAR Script

**Situation:** Following a year of significant growth, our QA team of two was covering a product that had tripled in complexity. Defect escape rate was climbing, sprint throughput was constrained by testing bottlenecks, and the team was burning out. I needed to make the case for two additional QA engineers and an investment in a commercial test management platform — a total ask of approximately £160,000 annually.

**Task:** I needed to present this at the quarterly budget review to the CTO and CFO — neither of whom thought in terms of test coverage or sprint velocity. I had 10 minutes.

**Action:** I built the case around three financial arguments. First, **the cost of the current state**: I calculated the fully-loaded cost of our current defect escape rate — 18 production incidents in the past year, averaging 6 hours of engineering time each to triage, fix, and deploy a hotfix, plus estimated customer churn from the two most severe incidents. Total: approximately £290,000 in direct and attributed costs. Second, **the projected return**: based on the defect reduction trajectory I had seen in two previous companies after similar QA investment, I projected a 50% reduction in production incidents within 12 months — £145,000 in avoided costs. Against a £160,000 investment, break-even was 13 months, with ongoing savings thereafter. I was explicit that this was a projection, not a guarantee, and presented a conservative scenario (30% reduction) alongside the base case. Third, **the risk of not investing**: I showed a trend line — defect escape rate had increased 40% year-on-year as the product grew and the team stayed flat. Without investment, the trajectory pointed toward a significant customer-facing incident within two quarters — I named the two areas I considered highest risk.

**Result:** Both headcount requests and the tooling budget were approved in full — the first time QA investment had been approved without negotiation in the company's history. The CFO commented that the break-even analysis was what made the difference — it reframed the ask from a cost to an investment with a defined return. Within 11 months, production incidents had fallen 48% — just inside the base case projection.

## Seniority Shift

| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Propose a tool or process improvement to a direct manager with a clear rationale. |
| **Mid** | Build a team-level business case with before/after metrics and a cost estimate. |
| **Senior** | Present a formal investment case to C-level stakeholders with ROI modelling, risk scenarios, and a defensible financial argument that competes with other budget priorities. |

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Investment Case** | A formal argument for spending money, framed around expected return rather than operational need. |
| **Break-Even Analysis** | The calculation of when cumulative savings from an investment will equal its cost. |
| **Fully-Loaded Cost** | The total cost of an outcome including direct costs, engineering time, customer impact, and opportunity cost. |
| **Conservative Scenario** | A lower-bound projection used to demonstrate that the investment is viable even if outcomes are worse than expected. |
| **Risk of Inaction** | The cost or consequence of not making the investment — often the most compelling argument for a budget holder. |
