## The Intent
The interviewer is testing your people leadership capability in one of the most competitive talent markets in engineering. Application security engineers are scarce, expensive, and have many options. They want to see how you hire, develop, and retain this specific talent profile — and how you structure the team's work so it multiplies the organisation's security capability rather than creating a bottleneck.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** I built an application security team from scratch at a Series D company. The CISO gave me a budget for four security engineers and a mandate to build the function over 18 months. I had to define the team's operating model, hire the right profile of engineer, and ensure the team delivered results fast enough to maintain executive confidence during the build period.

**Task:** Build a high-performing four-person application security team that delivered organisational impact from day one and remained effective as the team grew.

**Action:** I made deliberate decisions across four dimensions. Operating model first: before hiring, I defined what "high leverage" looked like for this team. The team's primary output was not finding vulnerabilities themselves — it was increasing the organisation's capacity to prevent, find, and fix vulnerabilities. This meant hiring engineers who were strong communicators and educators, not just technical specialists. Role design: I designed two distinct role profiles. AppSec Engineers (3) — responsible for penetration testing, code review, tooling development, and squad enablement. Security Architect (1) — responsible for threat modelling, standards development, and senior engineering partnership. I explicitly avoided hiring generalist security engineers who didn't have deep application-layer expertise. Hiring for communication: in interviews, I used a practical exercise — "explain this vulnerability to a developer who has never heard of SQL injection" — as a filter. Engineers who explained it in attacker terms without relating to developer context failed; those who used code examples and framed it from a developer perspective passed. Retention: application security engineers leave for one of three reasons — lack of interesting work, lack of learning, or better compensation elsewhere. I structured the role to be genuinely interesting (real penetration tests, novel vulnerability classes, building tooling), gave each engineer budget for two conferences and one certification per year, and benchmarked compensation quarterly against security market rates. Career progression: I created a security specialist career ladder with four levels, each defined by scope of impact — from individual service security to org-wide programme contribution. This gave engineers a visible path without needing to move into management.

**Result:** I hired four engineers in five months. Attrition over the following two years was zero. Two engineers were promoted within 18 months. The team's NPS from product squads — measured in the engineering survey — was 72 (high). Two engineers received external speaking invitations based on their conference talks about techniques developed at the company.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Focused on individual technical growth within the team |
| **Mid** | Mentors junior colleagues; contributes to hiring panels; participates in team knowledge sharing |
| **Senior** | Leads technical interviews; contributes to role design; mentors across the team |
| **Lead** | Defines operating model; designs role profiles; sets hiring criteria; creates career progression; manages retention proactively; measures team impact through squad NPS and engineer promotion rates |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **High-Leverage Security Team** | A team whose primary output is increasing the organisation's security capability — through education, tooling, and standards — rather than centralised execution of all security testing |
| **AppSec Engineer** | An application security specialist combining penetration testing expertise with developer communication skills, capable of both finding vulnerabilities and enabling teams to prevent them |
| **Security Architect** | A senior security role focused on threat modelling, standards design, and strategic partnership with engineering leadership, shaping security at the system design level |
| **Security Specialist Career Ladder** | A defined progression path for security engineers based on scope of organisational impact, providing a growth trajectory without requiring management transition |
| **Market Rate Benchmarking** | The regular comparison of compensation against current market data for equivalent roles, used to proactively retain high-demand security talent |
| **Squad NPS (Security)** | A net promoter score measuring how product squads rate the application security team's helpfulness and effectiveness, used as a team impact metric |
| **Communication Filter** | An interview exercise assessing whether a security engineer can explain technical concepts in developer-relevant terms, filtering for the communication capability essential to a high-leverage team |
| **Conference Investment** | Annual budget for security engineers to attend and speak at conferences, providing learning opportunities and recognition that support retention |
