## The Intent
The interviewer is testing your ability to operate at the executive layer where security competes with feature delivery for budget. Security investment is chronically under-funded in organisations that have not experienced a major breach. A lead who can frame security testing in financial terms — not just risk terms — is significantly more effective at securing the resources the programme needs.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** I was Head of Security Quality Engineering at a Series C fintech with 180 engineers. The security testing programme needed £200,000 in annual investment — two security engineers, tooling licences, and two external pen tests. The CFO had declined a previous request, citing insufficient ROI evidence. The CEO was aware of security risk in principle but was being pulled toward feature investment.

**Task:** Build and present a business case that secured the full investment from the CFO and CEO.

**Action:** I structured the case around three financial arguments and one strategic argument. Cost of a breach: I used IBM's Cost of a Data Breach Report (current year) to establish a baseline. The average breach cost for a company of our size and sector was £3.2M. I then applied a probability adjustment — using OWASP's published exploitation likelihood data for the unmitigated vulnerabilities already in our backlog (two BOLA vulnerabilities, one SQL injection), I estimated an annualised breach probability without the investment of approximately 18%. Expected annual loss without investment: 18% × £3.2M = £576,000. Regulatory penalty exposure: as a regulated fintech, a data breach involving customer financial data could trigger FCA enforcement and GDPR fines. I modelled the fine scenario conservatively at £500,000 (well below the GDPR 4% of global turnover maximum). Cyber insurance impact: I obtained a quote from our broker showing that implementing the proposed programme controls would reduce the annual cyber insurance premium by £35,000. Investment vs expected loss: the £200,000 annual investment prevented an expected annual loss of £576,000 plus £500,000 fine risk — a positive expected value of £876,000 minus £200,000 = £676,000. Payback period: under 4 months. Strategic argument: I closed with the point that our three largest enterprise prospects had asked about security testing practices in their due diligence questionnaires. Inability to answer these questions had caused two prospect delays in the previous quarter.

**Result:** The CFO approved the full investment. The enterprise prospect framing was the decisive factor for the CEO. The programme was funded for three years, not one, because the CFO wanted to lock in the insurance premium reduction over the longer term.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Not involved in investment decisions |
| **Mid** | Can articulate the risk cost of specific vulnerabilities in technical terms |
| **Senior** | Quantifies security risk in business terms; presents findings with business impact framing to engineering leadership |
| **Lead** | Constructs full financial business cases with breach probability modelling, regulatory penalty exposure, insurance impact, and strategic competitive framing; presents to CFO and CEO; secures multi-year investment |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Annualised Breach Probability** | The estimated likelihood of a security breach occurring within a 12-month period, calculated from vulnerability exploit likelihood and threat actor data |
| **Expected Annual Loss** | The annualised breach probability multiplied by the average breach cost — the financial basis for quantifying the value of preventive security investment |
| **IBM Cost of a Data Breach Report** | An annual industry research report providing average breach cost data by industry, company size, and region, used as a baseline for security investment business cases |
| **GDPR Fine** | A financial penalty for data protection violations, up to 4% of global annual turnover or €20M (whichever is greater) — used to model regulatory exposure in security investment cases |
| **Cyber Insurance Premium** | The annual cost of insurance coverage for cyber incidents; reduced by demonstrating strong security controls, providing a measurable financial ROI for security investment |
| **Due Diligence Questionnaire** | A security assessment document issued by enterprise prospects to evaluate the security maturity of potential vendors — used here as a strategic argument for security investment |
| **Positive Expected Value** | An investment decision criterion where the expected benefit (prevented losses) exceeds the cost — the basis for framing security as a financially rational investment |
| **Multi-Year Investment** | A commitment spanning multiple budget cycles, providing programme stability and enabling long-term security capability building rather than year-by-year uncertainty |
