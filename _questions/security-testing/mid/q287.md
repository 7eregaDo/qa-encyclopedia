## The Intent
The interviewer is testing one of the most important distinctions in security testing: the class of vulnerabilities that automated scanners cannot find. Business logic flaws require a human who understands what the application is supposed to prevent and can design test cases around those constraints. They want to see that you can think beyond exploitation techniques to business intent.

## The STAR Script
**Situation:** I was testing an e-commerce platform's discount and coupon system. Functional testing had confirmed that valid coupons reduced the price correctly. I wanted to investigate whether the business rules around discount application could be abused.

**Task:** Design and execute test cases for business logic vulnerabilities in the discount and checkout flow.

**Action:** I identified the business rules from the product specification and tested for violations of each. Coupon reuse: the spec stated coupons were single-use per account. I applied a coupon, completed the checkout, then placed a second order using the same coupon code — it was accepted again. The single-use check was only enforced on the frontend. Negative price manipulation: I added a product to the cart, applied a percentage discount coupon, and then applied a second coupon (the system allowed stacking). I kept applying coupons until the cart total went negative — the checkout completed and the platform credited my account with the difference. Race condition on limited stock: the spec stated a maximum of one unit per customer for a limited-edition product. I sent two simultaneous POST `/cart/add` requests for the same product from the same account using concurrent HTTP requests. Both succeeded, allowing me to add two units — the check was not atomic. Price manipulation via cart editing: I added an item to the cart, noted its cart line ID, then edited the cart JSON in the request to change the unit price field from 49.99 to 0.01. The server used the client-supplied price instead of looking up the price server-side — a critical finding. Free shipping threshold bypass: the spec required a minimum £50 order for free shipping. I added £50 of items, qualified for free shipping, then removed items to bring the total below £50 — the free shipping remained.

**Result:** Five business logic findings, including the critical client-supplied price manipulation. All were fixed before the promotional launch. The race condition required introducing database-level locking on the cart add operation.

## Seniority Shift
| Level | Expected Depth |
|-------|---------------|
| **Entry** | Tests obvious single-step rule violations; may miss multi-step or race condition scenarios |
| **Mid** | Designs business logic test cases from the product spec; tests coupon abuse, race conditions, negative values, price manipulation, and state machine violations; uses concurrent requests to test atomicity |
| **Senior** | Designs the business logic security test strategy; integrates domain expertise into threat modelling; identifies logic vulnerabilities in financial calculations, workflow automation, and multi-party systems |

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Business Logic Vulnerability** | A security flaw arising from incorrect enforcement of business rules — allowing individually valid operations to combine into an unintended, exploitable outcome |
| **Coupon Reuse** | A business logic flaw where single-use discount codes can be applied multiple times due to missing or bypassable server-side enforcement |
| **Price Manipulation** | A vulnerability where the server accepts client-supplied prices rather than looking them up from the authoritative data store, allowing arbitrary price reduction |
| **Race Condition (Business Logic)** | A flaw where simultaneous requests bypass a per-user or per-resource limit because the check is not atomic at the database level |
| **State Machine Violation** | A business logic flaw where an operation is accepted in a resource state where it should be forbidden — such as applying a discount after checkout is complete |
| **Atomicity** | The property that an operation either completes fully or not at all, with no intermediate state visible to other operations — required to prevent race conditions in concurrent business logic |
| **Negative Price** | An edge case where stacking discounts or applying a larger discount than the item value produces a negative cart total — which should be prevented by a business rule floor of zero |
| **Adversarial Workflow Testing** | Security testing that deliberately sequences valid operations to produce an unintended outcome, as opposed to testing individual steps in isolation |
